📌 Key Concepts at a Glance
Core high-yield facts from personality psychology
🧬

Personality Definition

The sum total of all relatively unchanging ways of acting, thinking, and feeling that are typical for a person and make each individual unique.

Core Concept
🔮

Psychoanalytic Theory (Freud)

Personality results from the struggle among opposing forces within the mind. The unique balance of forces creates unique personality.

Theory
🌱

Humanistic Theory

Humans are born with a healthy, positive drive to realize full potential. Society often interferes with this self-actualization.

Theory
📚

Learning Theory

Personality is learned through interactions with other members of society. Behavioral approach to personality development.

Theory
📊

Trait Theory

Focuses on describing the consistencies and organization of personality — what traits are, not why we behave.

Exam Favorite
⭐

Five-Factor Model (OCEAN)

Neuroticism, Extraversion, Openness, Agreeableness, Conscientiousness — complete personality description influenced by inheritance + experience.

📈 Trait Hierarchy (Allport)
From rarest to most common

🔺 Cardinal Traits

Extremely rare. Dominate a person's entire life. Example: Zewail's lifelong quest for scientific knowledge. Very few people possess these.

🔶 Central Traits

Common and important. Organize much of behavior toward goals like intimacy, power, prestige. Examples: honesty, generosity, conscientiousness.

🔽 Secondary Traits

Present only in specific situations. Example: being anxious only when speaking in public, or being rude to salespeople. Not part of general description.

🔍 Diagnostic Criteria & Assessment
Core criteria for personality disorders and assessment methods

✅ Personality Disorder — Diagnostic Criteria

  • 1️⃣Persistent patterns of behaviors, emotions, and cognition
  • 2️⃣Must begin in adolescence
  • 3️⃣Must be pervasive across multiple contexts
  • 4️⃣Must be inflexible and stable over time
  • 5️⃣Must lead to distress or impairment in function

💊 Management & Clinical Applications
Trait-based predictions, assessment approaches, and clinical implications

🚨 High-Risk Trait Combinations

⚠️ Critical — Screen & Monitor

High Neuroticism + Low Agreeableness

Strongly predicts depression and divorce. Also linked to diabetes, heart disease, and earlier mortality. These patients require proactive mental health screening and support.

⚡ Important — Track Longitudinally

Very High Neuroticism (isolated)

Worrying, temperamental, self-conscious, emotional. More likely to have serious health problems (diabetes, heart disease) and die somewhat earlier. Monitor for anxiety/mood disorders.

✅ Protective — Encourage

High Conscientiousness

Careful, reliable, hardworking. Predicts fewer careless accidents, better nutrition/exercise, longer lifespan, and success in academic and occupational domains.

📋 Assessment Selection Guide

🗣️ Interview

Best for initial evaluation. Subjective questioning reveals the patient's narrative and history. Use structured interviews for reliability.

👀 Observational Methods

Ideal when self-report is unreliable. Watch behavior in natural or simulated settings using rating scales. Captures actual behavior rather than stated behavior.

🔮 Projective Tests

Rorschach inkblots and TAT. Use when needing to reveal unconscious dynamics. Requires trained interpretation. Results are more subjective.

📝 Objective Tests

True/False standardized questionnaires. Best for reliable, comparable data. Easily administered and scored. Standard for research and large-scale screening.

🏥 Personality Disorder — General Management Principles

📌 Identify Early

Recognize persistent patterns beginning in adolescence. Early identification prevents progression and functional impairment.

📌 Assess Pervasiveness

Confirm patterns are pervasive (not situation-specific) and inflexible (resistant to change). Distinguish from transient states.

📌 Measure Functional Impact

Patterns must lead to distress or impairment to qualify as a disorder. Functioning level determines treatment intensity.

📌 Cluster-Specific Approach

Cluster A: social skills and reality testing. Cluster B: emotional regulation and impulse control. Cluster C: anxiety management and building autonomy.

🔬 Allport's Insight — Behavioral Prediction

Clinical Pearl

Finding Out What a Person Values

According to Allport, the best way to understand and predict someone's behavior is to find out what they value. Example: a person who values money more than family will spend more time working. This applies directly to patient motivation and treatment adherence.
