💡 Doctor's Pearls & Exam Focus
Key exam-oriented concepts emphasized by the lecturer
⭐ Normal CT ≠ No Stroke
A normal non-contrast CT does NOT rule out acute ischemic stroke. Its primary purpose in the acute setting is to exclude hemorrhage before administering tPA. Ischemic changes may not appear on CT for 6–12 hours. DWI MRI is the gold standard for early ischemic detection.
⭐ MCA = Most Embolized Artery
The Middle Cerebral Artery is the direct continuation of the internal carotid artery and receives the highest proportion of cardiac output to the brain. This makes it the most common target for emboli from cardiac sources (AF, mural thrombi, valvular vegetations).
⭐ Sources of Emboli
Cardiac: Atrial fibrillation, MI with mural thrombus, valvular disease, endocarditis. Arterial: Atherosclerotic plaques in carotid arteries. AF is the most common cardiac embolic source in non-valvular patients.
⭐ tPA Window & Criteria
The 90-minute to 4.5-hour window is critical. Alteplase (tPA) eligibility requires: confirmed ischemic stroke, no hemorrhage on CT, onset within 4.5 hours, no absolute contraindications (recent surgery, active bleeding, BP > 185/110, etc.).
⭐ Circle of Willis Functions
Connects vertebrobasilar and internal carotid systems. Provides collateral circulation, equalizes pressures, and is a common site for berry aneurysms (especially anterior communicating artery).
⭐ Red Neurons = Earliest Change
Eosinophilic neuronal necrosis ("red neurons") is the first microscopic/morphological change after ischemic brain injury — representing irreversible acute cellular damage.
🚨 Pitfall: UMN vs LMN Confusion
Students frequently confuse Upper and Lower Motor Neuron signs. Remember the key distinctions:

🔴 UMN Signs

  • Spasticity (hypertonia)
  • Hyperreflexia
  • Positive Babinski (+)
  • No fasciculations
  • No significant atrophy

🔵 LMN Signs

  • Flaccidity (hypotonia)
  • Hyporeflexia/areflexia
  • Negative Babinski (−)
  • Fasciculations present
  • Significant atrophy
🚨 Pitfall: Early Anticoagulation After tPA
Never start anticoagulants (rivaroxaban, warfarin) immediately after thrombolysis. Delay at least 24 hours to minimize catastrophic hemorrhagic transformation risk. In this case, rivaroxaban was started on Day 2.
🚨 Pitfall: Facial Palsy Localization
Central (UMN): Forehead SPARED — bilateral cortical innervation of upper face.
Peripheral (LMN): Entire ipsilateral face affected — no bilateral backup.

🔎 Rule-Out Logic

❌ Hemorrhagic Stroke
Excluded by non-contrast CT brain showing no hemorrhage. This is the primary reason CT is performed emergently — to differentiate ischemic from hemorrhagic stroke before thrombolysis.
❌ Anterior Cerebral Artery (ACA) Stroke
ACA territory strokes predominantly affect the lower limb (medial cortex supply). This patient has upper limb > lower limb deficits — classic MCA territory pattern.
❌ Posterior Circulation Stroke
Posterior circulation (vertebrobasilar) strokes present with vertigo, ataxia, cranial nerve palsies, visual field cuts — not the expressive aphasia and hemiplegia seen here.
❌ Bell's Palsy (LMN Facial Palsy)
Bell's palsy affects the entire ipsilateral face including forehead. This patient has forehead-sparing facial weakness — indicating a central (UMN) cause.

Localization & Pathophysiology
expand_more

Localization: Left Middle Cerebral Artery (MCA) territory

  • Motor cortex (lateral): Right hemiplegia, upper limb > lower limb (lateral homunculus = arm/face via MCA; medial = leg via ACA)
  • Broca's area (left frontal): Expressive aphasia — confirms dominant hemisphere involvement
  • Frontal eye fields: Leftward gaze deviation ("eyes look toward the lesion")
  • Facial motor cortex (UMN): Contralateral central facial palsy, forehead spared

Pathophysiology:

  • Non-adherence to warfarin → uncontrolled AF → atrial stasis → mural thrombus formation
  • Thrombus dislodges → embolus travels through ICA → lodges in MCA (most common target)
  • Sudden occlusion → focal cerebral ischemia → neuronal injury → "red neurons" (earliest histologic change)
  • UMN damage → loss of descending inhibition → spasticity, hyperreflexia, +Babinski

Most Likely Diagnosis

Acute Cardioembolic Ischemic Stroke — Left MCA Territory

Evidence supporting this diagnosis:

  • ✅ Sudden onset of focal neurological deficits (embolic pattern)
  • ✅ Right-sided deficits (UL > LL) — classic MCA distribution
  • ✅ Expressive aphasia — left (dominant) hemisphere involvement
  • ✅ Definitive embolic source: untreated AF (irregularly irregular rhythm)
  • ✅ CT: No hemorrhage — consistent with ischemic rather than hemorrhagic stroke

3
Initial Investigation & First Step
expand_more

Immediate Investigation: Non-contrast CT brain (NCCT)

  • Purpose: Exclude hemorrhage — NOT to confirm ischemic stroke
  • NCCT is fast, widely available, and highly sensitive for acute hemorrhage
  • A "normal" CT in the acute setting is expected and does NOT rule out ischemic stroke

Additional Workup:

  • ECG — confirm AF
  • CBC, BMP, coagulation studies (PT/INR), glucose
  • Echocardiogram — assess for mural thrombus / valvular disease
  • CT Angiography (CTA) — identify large vessel occlusion for potential thrombectomy
4
Definitive Management
expand_more

Acute Management (within 4.5-hour window):

  • IV Alteplase (tPA) — fibrinolytic; activates plasminogen → plasmin → fibrin clot breakdown
  • Administered at 1.5 hours post-onset → within the therapeutic window
  • Requires: CT excluding hemorrhage, informed consent, BP control (< 185/110)

Secondary Prevention (Day 2+):

  • Rivaroxaban (Factor Xa inhibitor) — delayed until Day 2 to avoid hemorrhagic transformation
  • Purpose: long-term stroke prevention in non-valvular AF
  • Alternative: warfarin with INR monitoring (patient was previously non-adherent)

Supportive:

  • BP management, glucose control, DVT prophylaxis, dysphagia screening
  • Rehabilitation: PT, OT, speech therapy for aphasia
  • Address medication adherence
