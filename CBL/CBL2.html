Exam Focus — Golden Rules

⭐ Intact corneal reflex + normal sensory/motor exam is the single most important clinical finding to differentiate Classical TN from Secondary TN. Always test and document this.
⭐ MRI must evaluate the Root Entry Zone (REZ) at the cerebellopontine angle. Look specifically for vascular compression (SCA/AICA) AND exclude mass lesions or demyelinating plaques (MS).
⭐ UMN vs LMN facial weakness: UMN (central) = forehead sparing (contralateral cortical lesion); LMN (peripheral) = entire hemiface paralyzed (CN VII damage). This patient has neither — ruling out CPA pathology.
⭐ V2 and V3 are the most commonly involved branches in trigeminal neuralgia. Isolated V1 involvement is uncommon and should raise suspicion for secondary causes.
warning

🚨 Red Flags & Common Pitfalls

⚠️ PITFALL #1: Confusing dental/jaw pathology with neuropathic pain. Always obtain an OPG first to rule out primary dental causes before diagnosing trigeminal neuralgia!
🚨 PITFALL #2 — LIFE-THREATENING: Carbamazepine can cause skin rash with mucosal involvement (Stevens-Johnson Syndrome / TEN). This requires IMMEDIATE drug discontinuation. Do not re-challenge!
⚠️ PITFALL #3: Failing to differentiate Classical TN (neurovascular compression) from Secondary TN (demyelinating plaques in MS, CPA tumors). History of numbness, bilateral symptoms, or young age → think secondary.
⚠️ PITFALL #4: High/maximal doses of carbamazepine cause dose-dependent ataxia. Always start low (200 mg BID) and titrate gradually. Monitor for CNS side effects.

Step 1: Localization & Pathophysiology

expand_more
  • Lesion Site: Root Entry Zone (REZ) of the left trigeminal nerve at the pons
  • Mechanism: Compression by a vascular loop (SCA/AICA branch) → focal demyelination → ephaptic cross-transmission between sensory fibers → paroxysmal ectopic discharges
  • Why paroxysmal? Demyelinated axons develop abnormal sodium channel expression → lowered threshold for firing → innocuous touch triggers volley of pain signals (allodynia)
  • Transition zone: The REZ is where central myelin (oligodendrocytes) meets peripheral myelin (Schwann cells) — this zone is uniquely vulnerable to compression injury
  • 75% vessel-nerve contact = significant compression confirming neurovascular conflict

Step 2: Most Likely Diagnosis

expand_more
  • Diagnosis: Classical Trigeminal Neuralgia (Type 1)
  • Supporting evidence:
  • ✅ Paroxysmal, electric shock-like pain in V3 distribution
  • ✅ Duration 30 sec – 2 min (characteristic brief attacks)
  • ✅ Triggered by non-noxious stimuli (allodynia)
  • ✅ Normal neurological exam — no sensory loss, intact corneal reflex
  • ✅ MRI confirms neurovascular compression at REZ
  • ✅ No mass lesions or demyelinating plaques (rules out secondary causes)
  • ✅ Previous successful response to carbamazepine
  • ✅ Relapsing-remitting pattern (3-year remission followed by recurrence)

Step 3: Initial Investigation Strategy

expand_more
  • 1. OPG (Orthopantomogram): FIRST step — rule out dental/jaw pathology before diagnosing neuralgia
  • 2. Comprehensive neurological exam: Sensory testing of V1/V2/V3, motor testing of mastication, corneal reflex
  • 3. MRI brainstem (with CISS/FIESTA sequences): Evaluate cerebellopontine angle and REZ for:
    — Neurovascular compression (confirms classical TN)
    — Mass lesions (meningioma, schwannoma)
    — Demyelinating plaques (multiple sclerosis)
  • Rule-out investigations: ESR/CRP if GCA suspected in elderly; skin inspection for vesicles (HZV)

Step 4: Definitive Management

expand_more
  • First-line: Carbamazepine
  • — Start low: 200 mg BID → titrate based on response and tolerability
  • — Mechanism: Voltage-gated Na⁺ channel blocker → stabilizes hyperexcited neural membranes
  • Alternative if CBZ fails/not tolerated: Oxcarbazepine — preferred in elderly patients (better tolerability)
  • Monitoring: Watch for dose-dependent ataxia at high doses
  • 🚨 STOP IMMEDIATELY if skin rash with mucosal involvement develops (SJS/TEN risk)
  • Surgical option (refractory cases): Microvascular Decompression (MVD) — separates compressing vessel from trigeminal nerve at REZ
  • Other surgical options: Gamma knife radiosurgery, percutaneous procedures (balloon compression, glycerol rhizotomy, radiofrequency thermocoagulation)
