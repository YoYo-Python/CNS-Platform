💡 Doctor's Exam Pearls
stars
Golden Rules for Exams
🧠

Neuro-Endocrine Circuit

Prolactin → inhibits GnRH pulsatility → suppresses LH/FSH → anovulation → amenorrhea. This is the core mechanism linking hyperprolactinemia to reproductive dysfunction. The inhibition occurs at the hypothalamic level.

👁️

Visual Pathway Anatomy

A mass pressing upward from the sella selectively damages the crossing nasal fibers in the optic chiasm → bitemporal hemianopia (NOT homonymous). Nasal fibers carry temporal visual fields.

💊

Medical Therapy is FIRST-LINE

Even with a macroadenoma causing compressive visual field defects, dopamine agonists (cabergoline) are first-line treatment — NOT surgery. Surgery is reserved for medical therapy failure/resistance.

🔬

Histology Pearls

Lactotrophs reside in the pars distalis. EM shows large electron-dense secretory granules. Identified by Azocarmine stain. The sella turcica is part of the sphenoid bone.

📊

Baseline Evaluation Before Treatment

Always check: TSH + Free T4 (thyroid), Cortisol (adrenal), and β-hCG (exclude pregnancy) before initiating dopamine agonist therapy.

warning 🚨 Common Pitfalls & Exam Traps
  • Normal visual acuity (6/6) ≠ No visual pathology. Central macular vision is spared early because of bilateral cortical representation. You MUST test peripheral fields with Goldmann perimetry!
  • Chiasm vs. Tract confusion: Chiasm compression = bitemporal hemianopia. Optic tract lesion = contralateral homonymous hemianopia. Don't confuse them!
  • Don't jump to surgery! Unlike most brain tumors, prolactinomas respond dramatically to medical therapy. Surgery is NOT first-line even for macroadenomas with visual compression.
  • Rule out pregnancy FIRST. Pregnancy is the most common cause of amenorrhea + galactorrhea. Always get β-hCG before attributing symptoms to a prolactinoma.
  • Stalk effect vs. true prolactinoma: Large non-functioning adenomas can cause mild prolactin elevation (<100 ng/mL) via stalk compression. Prolactin >200 ng/mL strongly suggests a true prolactinoma.
rule
Differential Diagnosis Rule-Out Logic
❌

Pregnancy

Ruled out by negative β-hCG. Pregnancy causes physiological prolactin elevation + amenorrhea + possible galactorrhea.

❌

Hypothyroidism

Ruled out by normal TSH and Free T4. Primary hypothyroidism → ↑TRH → stimulates prolactin secretion.

❌

Non-functioning Pituitary Adenoma (Stalk Effect)

Prolactin 250 ng/mL is too high for stalk effect (usually <100 ng/mL). Confirms this is a true secretory prolactinoma.

❌

Drug-induced Hyperprolactinemia

No history of antipsychotics, metoclopramide, or other dopamine-blocking agents. MRI confirms structural lesion.

Localization of the Lesion
expand_more
The lesion is at the optic chiasm / sella turcica.

Visual field: Bitemporal hemianopia → classic optic chiasm compression. Nasal retinal fibers (temporal fields) cross at the chiasm and are selectively damaged by a midline mass pushing from below.

Endocrine: Amenorrhea + galactorrhea + elevated prolactin → anterior pituitary (lactotroph) origin.

Anatomy: The sella turcica (sphenoid bone) houses the pituitary. Suprasellar extension reaches the optic chiasm directly above. No cavernous sinus invasion = intact CN III, IV, V₁, V₂, VI.
2
Most Likely Diagnosis
expand_more
Pituitary Prolactinoma (Macroadenoma)

Supported by the classic triad:
1. Amenorrhea (10 months) — from GnRH suppression
2. Galactorrhea (6 weeks) — direct prolactin effect on mammary glands
3. Bitemporal hemianopia — from optic chiasm compression

Biochemical confirmation: Prolactin 250 ng/mL (10x upper limit). Levels >200 ng/mL are virtually diagnostic of a macroprolactinoma.

Radiological confirmation: 2.8 cm enhancing sellar mass with suprasellar extension compressing the chiasm. Size >1 cm = macroadenoma classification.
3
Initial Investigation Strategy
expand_more
Key investigations ordered:

1. Serum prolactin — Markedly elevated at 250 ng/mL (confirms hypersecretion)
2. β-hCG — Negative (rules out pregnancy as #1 cause of amenorrhea/galactorrhea)
3. LH & FSH — Suppressed (confirms downstream gonadotropin inhibition)
4. TSH + Free T4 — Normal (excludes hypothyroidism-driven hyperprolactinemia)
5. Cortisol — Normal (rules out adrenal insufficiency, important baseline before treatment)
6. Goldmann perimetry — Dense bitemporal hemianopia (quantifies visual compromise)
7. Brain MRI with contrast — 2.8 cm macroadenoma with chiasm compression (definitive imaging)
4
Definitive Management
expand_more
First-line: Cabergoline (Dopamine Agonist)

Mechanism: Stimulates D2 dopamine receptors on anterior pituitary lactotrophs → suppresses prolactin secretion → rapid tumor shrinkage.

Why cabergoline over bromocriptine? Longer half-life → once or twice weekly dosing. Better tolerability with fewer GI side effects.

Expected response: Normalization of prolactin levels, resumption of menses, improvement of visual fields within weeks to months. Tumor shrinkage can be dramatic (>50% in weeks).

If medical therapy fails (resistance): → Transsphenoidal surgical resection of the adenoma.

Follow-up: Serial prolactin levels, repeat visual field testing, and follow-up MRI to monitor tumor response.

🛤️ Diagnostic Algorithm — Prolactinoma
🩺 Clinical Presentation
Amenorrhea + Galactorrhea ± Visual symptoms
Key triad: Amenorrhea, galactorrhea, and headaches. If visual symptoms (peripheral vision loss, bumping into objects), suspect suprasellar extension compressing the optic chiasm. Always ask about menstrual history, fertility, and medications.
arrow_downward First Step
🧪 Exclude Pregnancy (β-hCG)
MUST be done before any further workup
Pregnancy is the #1 cause of amenorrhea + galactorrhea + elevated prolactin. A negative β-hCG is mandatory before proceeding. Also rule out drugs (antipsychotics, metoclopramide) and hypothyroidism (TSH).
arrow_downward β-hCG Negative
📊 Serum Prolactin Level
Check TSH, Free T4, Cortisol, LH, FSH
Prolactin >200 ng/mL: Virtually diagnostic of macroprolactinoma.
Prolactin 25–100 ng/mL: Consider microprolactinoma, drugs, or stalk effect.
Also baseline: TSH/FT4 (exclude hypothyroidism), cortisol (adrenal status), LH/FSH (expect suppressed).
arrow_downward Prolactin ↑↑ (250 ng/mL)
🧲 Brain MRI with Contrast
Confirm sellar mass, measure size, assess extension
Microadenoma: <1 cm. Macroadenoma: ≥1 cm.
Assess: suprasellar extension (chiasm compression?), cavernous sinus invasion (cranial nerve involvement?), hemorrhage/cystic changes.
This case: 2.8 cm macroadenoma, suprasellar extension, chiasm compression, no cavernous sinus invasion.
arrow_downward + Visual field testing
👁️ Goldmann Perimetry
Quantify visual field deficit
Expected finding: Bitemporal hemianopia (loss of both temporal visual fields).
Mechanism: Suprasellar mass compresses crossing nasal fibers at the central chiasm.
Remember: Visual acuity (6/6) may be preserved early — macular sparing due to bilateral cortical representation. Always formally test fields!
arrow_downward Treatment Decision
💊 First-Line: Cabergoline (D2 Agonist)
Medical therapy — even for macroadenomas with visual loss
Cabergoline: D2 dopamine agonist. Once or twice weekly dosing. Suppresses prolactin secretion, shrinks tumor rapidly.
Preferred over bromocriptine: longer half-life, better tolerability.
Monitor: Serial prolactin, visual field testing, follow-up MRI.
arrow_downward If Resistant
🔪 Transsphenoidal Surgery
For dopamine agonist resistance
Indicated when: cabergoline fails to normalize prolactin, tumor doesn't shrink, or visual deterioration despite medical therapy. Approach: through the sphenoid sinus (transsphenoidal).
☢️ Radiation (Adjunct)
Not first-line salvage
Reserved for refractory cases after surgery + medical therapy fail. NOT first-line salvage. Risk of delayed hypopituitarism.
