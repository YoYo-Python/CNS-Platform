Vitals & General
On Arrival
⚠️
Unresponsive
No purposeful response to stimuli
Suggests severe brain injury with depressed consciousness. GCS severely reduced. Correlates with subdural hematoma and cerebral edema on imaging.
⚠️
Bradycardic
Inappropriately low heart rate
In the setting of a high cervical cord injury, bradycardia results from loss of sympathetic outflow (T1-T4) with unopposed vagal tone. May also be part of Cushing response from raised ICP.
🔴
Inadequate Respiratory Effort
Required intubation
C2-C3 injury impairs phrenic nerve roots (C3-C5), which innervate the diaphragm. Loss of diaphragmatic function → ventilator dependence. Critical finding localizing the lesion.
🧠
Neurological Exam
Key Findings
🔴
Flaccid tone, profound hypotonia, areflexia — all 4 limbs
Complete motor & reflex loss below neck
Acute spinal shock phase → flaccidity and areflexia. After resolution, UMN signs (spasticity, hyperreflexia, Babinski) develop below the lesion level.
🔴
Absent anal wink, patulous sphincter
S2-S4 disconnection
Absent anal wink reflex (S2-S4) indicates complete disruption of descending pathways to sacral segments. Patulous sphincter = loss of sphincter tone. Critical exam finding for ASIA Grade A classification.
✅
Pupils equal & reactive; Brisk corneal reflexes
CN II, III, V, VII intact
Preserved pupillary and corneal reflexes indicate intact brainstem circuitry above the lesion. Corneal reflex: CN V (afferent) + CN VII (efferent).
⚠️
Absent gag reflex
CN IX/X dysfunction
Gag reflex requires CN IX (afferent) and CN X (efferent). Absence suggests injury/dysfunction of these lower brainstem pathways, increasing aspiration risk.
💡
Facial grimacing to noxious stimuli — No extremity motor response
Cranial-spinal dissociation
KEY EXAM PEARL: Face grimacing (intact CN V/VII via brainstem) + absent extremity response = complete cervical cord disconnection. The brainstem processes facial sensation, but motor commands cannot reach the spinal cord below C2-C3.

Golden Exam Pearls
Lecturer's Emphasis
⭐ Cranial-Spinal Dissociation
Facial noxious stimuli → grimacing (intact brainstem CN V/VII). Extremity stimuli → no motor response = complete disconnection at high cervical cord. This is the hallmark exam finding proving the lesion is at or above C2-C3, not in the brainstem itself.
⭐ Corneal Reflex = CN V + CN VII
Afferent: CN V (trigeminal — sensory). Efferent: CN VII (facial — motor blink). Brisk bilaterally = brainstem pontine pathways intact.
⭐ Anal Wink Reflex → S2-S4 Localization
Absence of the anal wink = S2-S4 segments disconnected from descending pathways. Combined with areflexia in all limbs, this confirms ASIA Grade A (complete injury).
📌 ASIA Grade A Classification
Complete injury: No motor or sensory function preserved in the sacral segments S4-S5. This patient has absent anal wink, patulous sphincter, no sacral sensation = Grade A at C2-C3.

Red Flags & Common Pitfalls
Dangerous Mistakes
🚨 DO NOT Give High-Dose Methylprednisolone
Major exam trap! Current guidelines: No steroids for pediatric SCI. Increases infection risk, GI bleeding, and complications without proven neurological benefit.
🚨 AVOID Morphine Acutely
Opioids cause respiratory depression — extremely dangerous when respiratory drive is already compromised by C2-C3 cord injury + TBI. This is a classic pharmacology trap question.
🚨 AVOID NSAIDs Acutely
NSAIDs increase bleeding risk (antiplatelet effect) in a patient with active intracranial hemorrhage. Also risk renal impairment in acute trauma with potential hypoperfusion.
⚠️ Don't Confuse Spinal Shock with UMN vs LMN
Acutely: flaccidity + areflexia = spinal shock (NOT a LMN lesion). After resolution → UMN signs emerge: spasticity, hyperreflexia, positive Babinski. The underlying pathology is always UMN for a lesion at C2-C3.
🔄
Rule-Out Logic
Why Other Diagnoses Were Excluded
❌ Isolated Brainstem Injury
Ruled out because pupillary and corneal reflexes are preserved. A brainstem lesion would impair these cranial nerve pathways. The dissociation pattern (intact face, absent limbs) points to cervical cord, not brainstem.
❌ Epidural Hematoma
CT shows a subdural hematoma (bridging vein tear). Epidural hematoma = middle meningeal artery rupture, typically lens-shaped on CT, often with temporal bone fracture. Different mechanism and imaging pattern.
❌ Incomplete Cord Injury (ASIA B-D)
Complete absence of motor, sensory, and reflex function at all levels below the lesion, including sacral segments (absent anal wink/tone), confirms ASIA Grade A = complete injury.

1
Localization & Pathophysiology
expand_more
Two simultaneous injuries:
  • Intracranial: Right acute subdural hematoma → bridging vein tear from deceleration. Causes mass effect (midline shift), cerebral edema, and raised ICP (effaced basal cisterns).
  • Spinal: C2 fracture → spinal canal narrowing → cord contusion/edema at C2-C3. This disconnects all descending motor pathways and ascending sensory pathways below the lesion.
Why cranial-spinal dissociation?
  • Facial sensation (CN V) enters the brainstem directly → processed above the lesion → facial motor output (CN VII) exits above C2 → grimacing preserved.
  • Corticospinal tract for limbs must pass through C2-C3 → completely disrupted → no extremity response.
Respiratory failure: Phrenic nerve (C3-C5) originates below the lesion level → diaphragm paralyzed → ventilator dependent.
2
Most Likely Diagnosis & Classification
expand_more
Dual Diagnosis:
  • Severe Diffuse Traumatic Brain Injury — subdural hematoma, cerebral edema, effaced cisterns, unresponsiveness.
  • Complete High Cervical Spinal Cord Injury — ASIA Grade A at C2-C3
ASIA Grade A criteria met:
  • No motor function below injury level ✓
  • No sensory function below injury level ✓
  • No sacral sparing (absent anal wink, patulous sphincter) ✓
Supporting imaging: C2 fracture + cord contusion/edema on MRI (note: complete functional injury despite no anatomical transection on MRI).
3
Initial Investigations & Steps
expand_more
Immediate priorities (ATLS approach):
  • Airway: Secure airway → intubation (already done due to inadequate respiratory effort)
  • Cervical spine immobilization — maintain until cleared
  • Non-contrast CT head → identified subdural hematoma, edema, midline shift
  • CT cervical spine → identified C2 fracture
  • MRI cervical spine → characterized cord contusion/edema
Acute ICP management:
  • Mannitol or Hypertonic Saline IV → osmotic therapy to reduce cerebral edema prior to surgical intervention
  • Head of bed elevation, avoid hypotension/hypoxia
4
Definitive Management
expand_more
Neurosurgical:
  • Craniotomy for evacuation of subdural hematoma (mass effect with midline shift)
  • ICP monitoring
  • Cervical spine stabilization/fixation for C2 fracture
Critical care:
  • Long-term mechanical ventilation (C2-C3 = ventilator-dependent)
  • Hemodynamic support (neurogenic shock management — IV fluids, vasopressors, atropine for bradycardia)
Medications — what NOT to give:
  • ❌ High-dose methylprednisolone (no benefit, increased complications)
  • ❌ Morphine (respiratory depression)
  • ❌ NSAIDs (bleeding, renal risk)
Long-term rehabilitation:
  • Gabapentin (titrated) → first-line for neuropathic pain/allodynia
  • Baclofen (GABA-B agonist) → for spasticity-related pain when it develops
