🧠 Core Concept: Brain Energy Metabolism
🔴 Must Know
Brain = 2% of body weight but consumes ~20% of total O₂ and glucose. Continuous supply required — minimal energy reserves.
  • Brain depends on glucose + oxygen continuously in the fed state
  • Glycogen stores are extremely small and exist ONLY in astrocytes
  • The brain cannot use fatty acids for energy — lipids are purely structural
  • After prolonged starvation (>2-3 weeks), ketone bodies replace glucose as primary fuel
⚡ Glucose Transport in the CNS
💎 Doctor Pearl
GLUT1 = Blood-Brain Barrier + Glial cells (insulin-insensitive)
GLUT3 = Neurons (also insulin-insensitive)
Both work independently of insulin — brain glucose uptake is always active!
🔬 Pathophysiology Deep Dive
💨 Hypoxia Cascade — Pasteur Effect ▼
Sequence:
Hypoxic event → Drastic slowdown in oxidative phosphorylation → Compensatory increase in glycolysis (Pasteur effect) → Increased lactic acid production → Intracellular acidosis → Gradual increase in spinal fluid lactate

Diagnostic Clue Elevated CSF lactate in hypoxia
🔄 Astrocyte-Neuron Lactate Shuttle ▼
Astrocytes take up glucose via GLUT1 → Convert glucose to lactate → Export lactate → Neurons use lactate for ATP production

This shuttle provides an alternative energy pathway for neurons, supplementing direct glucose uptake.
🔁 Glutamate-Glutamine Cycle ▼
Astrocytes take up glutamate from the synapse → Convert to glutamine (via glutamine synthetase) → Return glutamine to neurons → Neurons use it for neurotransmitter synthesis

Key Enzyme Glutamine synthetase (astrocyte-specific)
🧪 Hyperammonemia in the Brain ▼
Brain LACKS a urea cycle → Detoxifies ammonia by converting it to glutamine

Excess ammonia (hyperammonemia) → Impairs TCA cycle → Decreased ATP production → Neurological dysfunction

Exam Trap Don't confuse with liver's urea cycle — brain uses a completely different mechanism!
🧬 Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB) Structure ▼
Selective barrier controlling CNS entry:
✅ Glucose & amino acids → via active transporters
✅ Lipid-soluble substances → via passive diffusion
❌ Restricts harmful/large molecules

LNAA Competition: Large neutral amino acids share transport across BBB → Competition affects relative brain concentrations (e.g., Tryptophan vs Tyrosine vs Phenylalanine)
🧪 Neurotransmitter Precursors ▼
Tyrosine → Catecholamines (Dopamine, Norepinephrine, Epinephrine)
Tryptophan → Serotonin (5-HT)

Both are large neutral amino acids that compete for the same BBB transporter.
Clinical Pearl High carbohydrate meals → insulin → ↓plasma LNAA (except tryptophan) → relatively more tryptophan enters brain → ↑serotonin

⚠️ Common Pitfalls & Exam Traps
⚠️ Pitfall #1 — Lipid Utilization
Students often assume the brain uses fatty acids for energy. WRONG!
The brain cannot effectively utilize fatty acids. Brain lipids (cholesterol, sphingolipids, phospholipids) are purely structural (myelin sheaths, cell membranes). The brain synthesizes most of its own structural lipids.
⚠️ Pitfall #2 — Ammonia Detoxification
Do NOT confuse liver urea cycle with brain metabolism!
Brain has NO urea cycle → Converts ammonia to glutamine instead.
Hyperammonemia → impairs TCA cycle → decreased ATP.
⚠️ Pitfall #3 — Starvation Timeline
Early fasting (10-14 hrs): Hepatic glycogenolysis maintains glucose
First days of fasting: Hepatic gluconeogenesis (from amino acids & glycerol)
Prolonged starvation (>2-3 wks): Ketone bodies become primary brain fuel

Trap Don't say "ketones" for early fasting — that's glycogenolysis!

🔗 Basic Science Integration
🏗️ BBB — Anatomy & Transport ▼
• Selective barrier controlling CNS molecular entry
• Active transport: Glucose (GLUT1), amino acids (specific carriers)
• Passive diffusion: Lipid-soluble substances, gases (O₂, CO₂)
• Restricted: Large polar molecules, pathogens, toxins
• LNAA competition: Tyr, Trp, Phe, Leu, Ile, Val share the same transporter → intake of one reduces brain entry of others
🧫 Brain Lipids — Structural Role ▼
Brain lipids include:
• Cholesterol — myelin component (brain synthesizes its own)
• Sphingolipids — myelin sheaths
• Phospholipids — neuronal membranes
• Essential fatty acids — must be supplied from circulation (only exception)

Key Point Lipids = STRUCTURE, never energy in the brain

🔀 Flowchart 2: Hypoxia Cascade in the Brain
💨 Cerebral Hypoxic Event
Any cause of reduced O₂ delivery to the brain: near-drowning, cardiac arrest, severe anemia, respiratory failure, etc.
⬇
⬇ Oxidative Phosphorylation Drastically Slows
Without O₂, the electron transport chain cannot function. ATP production from mitochondria drops dramatically.
⬇
⬆ Compensatory Increase in Glycolysis
Pasteur Effect
Pasteur Effect: Anaerobic conditions cause glycolysis to accelerate to compensate for lost mitochondrial ATP. However, this is far less efficient (2 ATP vs 36 ATP).
⬇
⬆ Lactic Acid Production
Pyruvate is converted to lactate (by lactate dehydrogenase) instead of entering the TCA cycle. Lactate accumulates in neurons and interstitial fluid.
⬇
⚠️ Intracellular Acidosis
Accumulation of lactic acid lowers intracellular pH. This disrupts enzyme function, ion channels, and can trigger cell death pathways.
⬇
📈 Gradual Increase in CSF Lactate
Diagnostic Marker
Clinical Finding: Elevated spinal fluid (CSF) lactate is a hallmark finding of cerebral hypoxia. Used diagnostically to assess severity.
🔀 Flowchart 3: Ammonia Detoxification — Brain vs. Liver
🧪 Excess Ammonia (NH₃)
Ammonia is produced from amino acid metabolism, purine/pyrimidine catabolism, and intestinal bacterial activity. It is neurotoxic.
⬇
Which Organ?
The detoxification pathway differs completely between liver and brain. This is a classic exam differentiation point!
⬇
🫀 Liver
⬇
✅ Urea Cycle
NH₃ → Urea → Excreted by kidneys
Liver: Has a complete urea cycle. Converts toxic ammonia to non-toxic urea, which is excreted in urine. This is the major systemic detoxification pathway.
🧠 Brain
⬇
❌ NO Urea Cycle!
NH₃ + Glutamate → Glutamine
(via Glutamine Synthetase)
Brain: Lacks urea cycle enzymes. Astrocytes use glutamine synthetase to convert ammonia + glutamate → glutamine. Excess glutamine causes osmotic swelling (cerebral edema). Hyperammonemia also impairs TCA cycle → ↓ATP.
🔀 Flowchart 4: Astrocyte-Neuron Metabolic Cooperation
🩸 Blood Glucose
Glucose arrives via cerebral blood vessels and crosses the BBB through GLUT1 transporters.
⬇ GLUT1
🔵 Astrocyte Pathway
⬇
Glucose → Glycolysis → Lactate
Astrocytes preferentially metabolize glucose to lactate via aerobic glycolysis, then export the lactate.
⬇ Export
Lactate → Neuron → Acetyl CoA → ATP
Lactate Shuttle: Neurons take up astrocyte-produced lactate, convert it to pyruvate, then Acetyl CoA, and oxidize it in the TCA cycle for ATP.
🟢 Neuron Direct
⬇ GLUT3
Glucose → Glycolysis → TCA → ATP
Neurons also take up glucose directly via GLUT3. Both pathways (direct + shuttle) supply neuronal energy.
🔁 Glutamate-Glutamine Cycle
Parallel to energy metabolism, astrocytes and neurons cooperate in neurotransmitter recycling.
⬇
⬇
Synapse: Glutamate released
Neurons release glutamate into the synaptic cleft for neurotransmission.
→ Astrocyte uptake
Astrocyte: Glutamate → Glutamine
Astrocytes clear glutamate from the synapse (preventing excitotoxicity) and convert it to glutamine via glutamine synthetase.
→ Return to neuron
Neuron: Glutamine → Glutamate (NT)
Neurons receive glutamine, convert it back to glutamate for reuse as a neurotransmitter. This cycle prevents glutamate depletion.
