1. Infancy (0–15 months)
Erikson: Trust vs Mistrust
▾
Definition
Period from birth to 15 months; the foundation of all future social relationships.
Core Task
Formation of an intimate, secure attachment to the primary caregiver (usually the mother).
Key Factor
Physical and emotional presence of the mother is essential.
Deprivation Effect
Prolonged absence → Anaclitic depression, severe developmental retardation, higher death rates, and indiscriminate attachment to strangers.
Significance
Quality of primary attachment dictates the shape of ALL future social relationships.
⚠️ EXAM POINTS
  • "The most important psychological task of a child between birth and 15 months is the development of an intimate attachment to the mother."
  • "Maternal deprivation leads to anaclitic depression and poor physical development."
  • Piaget: Sensorimotor stage ends with Object Permanence (understanding objects exist when out of sight).
📖 Source: Mansoura University — Developmental Psychology & Psychiatry Lecture
👦
2. Toddler Years (15–30 months)
Erikson: Autonomy vs Shame
▾
Definition
Developmental stage from 1 to 3 years old.
Core Task
Separation and individuation from the primary caregiver; completed by age 3.
Key Mechanism
The child must establish an independent identity without losing the secure base (the mother as anchor).
Significance
Allows the child to eventually attend day care without severe separation anxiety.
⚠️ EXAM POINTS
  • "The toddler has no understanding of death and sees the death of a close family member as separation."
  • A child who CANNOT separate by age 3 → Separation Anxiety Disorder (not normal).
  • Toilet training typically occurs at age 3; it is often delayed by genetics or perfectionist parents.
📖 Source: Mansoura University — Developmental Psychology & Psychiatry Lecture
🎨
3. Preschool Child (3–6 Years)
Erikson: Initiative vs Guilt
▾
Definition
Children aged 3 to 6 years; Piaget's Pre-operational Stage.
Core Task
Development of superego (conscience), empathy, and theory of mind (realizing others exist and have independent thoughts).
Cognitive Features
Egocentric, animism (giving life to objects), magical thinking, and death is perceived as temporary and reversible.
Stressor Response
Life stressors (divorce, new sibling) can trigger regression (e.g., bed-wetting in a trained child).
⚠️ EXAM POINTS
  • "A child whose pet died and thinks it will come back to life is typically 4 years old." (Under 5 → death is temporary/reversible.)
  • Toilet training at age 3; enuresis is ONLY diagnosed after age 5.
  • Regression is a defense mechanism: stress → revert to baby-like behaviors for comfort.
📖 Source: Mansoura University — Developmental Psychology & Psychiatry Lecture
🏫
4. School Age (7–11 Years)
Erikson: Industry vs Inferiority
▾
Definition
The period from 7 to 11 years old; Piaget's Concrete Operational Stage.
Core Task
Internalizing moral right/wrong; interacting predominantly with same-gender peers; identifying with same-gender parent.
Cognitive Skills
Conservation (quantity unchanged by container shape) and Seriation (arranging objects by size/quality).
Death Concept
After age 11 → child FINALLY understands death is universal, permanent, and inevitable.
⚠️ EXAM POINTS
  • Conservation: Pour water from a short wide glass into a tall thin glass → quantity is the same (school-age child understands this).
  • Children ages 7–11 avoid the opposite sex and identify exclusively with same-sex peers and the same-gender parent.
📖 Source: Mansoura University — Developmental Psychology & Psychiatry Lecture
🧑
5. Adolescence (11–20 Years)
Erikson: Identity vs Role Confusion
▾
Definition
The transition period bridging childhood to adulthood; marked biologically by puberty.
Core Task
Developing a stable identity (Erikson's identity crisis) and cognitive maturation toward abstract reasoning.
Sub-phases
Early (obedient, peer-opinion sensitive), Middle (risk-taking, omnipotence), Late (abstract reasoning, identity consolidation).
Brain Basis
Prefrontal cortex (behavior/emotional regulation) matures; Limbic system (raw emotions) was already active — this mismatch drives impulsivity.
Puberty Markers
Females: Menarche (age 11–14). Males: First ejaculation.
⚠️ EXAM POINTS
  • "Education about obvious SHORT-TERM benefits is more effective than long-term consequences to stop teenage risk-taking."
  • Unresolved identity crisis → Role confusion → societal displacement, criminality.
  • Some individuals remain concrete thinkers for life; abstract reasoning is NOT universal.

Frequently Confused Concepts
Concept AConcept BKey Difference
GrowthDevelopmentGrowth = physical increase in size. Development = maturation in function.
PsychologyPsychiatryPsychology studies normal mental processes/behavior. Psychiatry diagnoses and treats mental diseases.
FearAnxietyFear = specific, known trigger (e.g., a lion). Anxiety = apprehension about an unknown/undefined threat.
EmpathySympathyEmpathy = understanding and validating suffering while maintaining healthy boundaries. Sympathy = merely feeling pity (can be harmful).
SexGenderSex = biological/chromosomal (XX, XY). Gender = psychological/social self-identity.
Concrete ThinkingAbstract ThinkingConcrete = literal/physical interpretation. Abstract = understands symbolism, metaphors, and underlying logic.
ShameGuiltShame = seeing yourself negatively through others' eyes (social). Guilt = internal feeling of personal remorse.
