Topic 1 — Perception Process
🔵 Sensation
First awareness of an outside stimulus via sense organs — produces meaningless bits of information.
Foundation
Mechanism: Stimuli activate sensory receptors → electrical signals → brain → raw data.
Key point: The signals at this stage are MEANINGLESS — meaning is assigned later during interpretation.
Regulation: Stimulus intensity and perceiver factors determine which stimuli are received.
🟣 Perception
Analysis, organization, and meaningful interpretation of sensory input.
High Yield
Steps: Selection → Organization (Gestalt) → Interpretation (association areas) → Action.
External factors: Size, intensity, movement, color, contrast of stimulus.
Internal factors: Age, learning, interest, emotions, drive, set (expectation).
🟢 Interpretation
Assigning meaning to raw data in sensory association areas of the brain.
Process
Location: Sensory association areas (beyond primary sensory cortex).
Dependence: Strongly shaped by past experience and cognitive sets.
Significance: Bridges raw sensation and adaptive behavioral output.
Topic 2 — Gestalt Organization
🔲 Figure-Ground
Brain separates a focal object (figure) from its background (ground).
Exam Fav
Reversible: Ambiguous images (vase/faces) allow figure and ground to alternate.
Cannot perceive both simultaneously — only one at a time.
Example: Rubin's vase — vase OR two faces, never both at once.
📐 Continuity
Tendency to continue patterns in their established direction without breaking.
Gestalt
The brain resists breaking lines or sequences and continues them in their established direction. Makes it hard to see overlapping shapes as separate.
📍 Proximity
Items close together are perceived as a group.
Gestalt
Spatial closeness is a powerful grouping signal. The brain automatically clusters nearby objects even if they differ in shape, color, or size.
🎨 Similarity
Items sharing color or shape are grouped together.
Gestalt
The brain groups elements that share visual characteristics (shape, color, size, orientation), even if they are not close to each other spatially.
⭕ Closure
Brain fills in missing information to perceive complete familiar objects.
MCQ Fav
Example: Dotted circle → perceived as full circle.
Key feature: Automatic and inborn — does not require effort.
Clinical relevance: Related to why we "see" faces in random patterns (pareidolia).
Topic 3 — Depth Perception
👁️ Monocular Cues (6)
Depth cues available to one eye alone.
High Yield
1. Texture Gradient: Fine textures = distant; coarse = near.
2. Linear Perspective: Parallel lines converge in distance (railroad tracks).
3. Superposition: Near object partially hides far object (chicken in front of farmer).
4. Shadowing: Light & shadow convey 3D shape.
5. Speed of Movement: Near objects cross field faster.
6. Aerial Perspective: Distant objects appear bluish/hazy (water vapor scatter).
👀 Binocular Cues (2)
Depth cues requiring both eyes working together.
High Yield
1. Convergence: Extraocular muscles angle both eyes inward for near objects. More angle = closer object.
2. Retinal Disparity: Eyes ~6.5 cm apart see slightly different images. Brain uses this difference to triangulate precise depth — especially effective for near objects.
🔄 Perceptual Constancy
Stable perception despite changing retinal input.
Pearl
Brightness: White paper looks white in dim or bright light.
Color: Apple looks red under incandescent or LED light.
Size: Friend walking away doesn't seem to shrink.
Shape: Round pot looks round even viewed at an angle.
Topic 4 — Pitfalls & Faulty Perception
👻 Hallucination
Vivid sensory experience with NO external stimulus present.
⚠️ #1 Exam
Types: Auditory (most common in psychosis), Visual, Tactile, Olfactory, Gustatory.
Hypnagogic: Hallucination at sleep onset (normal variant).
Hypnopompic: At waking (normal variant).
Clinical sign: Key indicator for psychiatric evaluation — no stimulus, yet perception exists.
🎭 Illusion
Misinterpretation of a real external stimulus.
⚠️ #1 Exam
Causes: Physical (mirrors, light), Habit/familiarity, Set/expectation (waiting for a call → doorbell sounds like phone), Camouflage, Emotional state.
Key distinction: A real stimulus EXISTS but is misinterpreted. Unlike hallucination where nothing exists.
😶 Depersonalization
Perceiving oneself as changed or different.
Know Def
The person feels detached from their own thoughts, feelings, or body — like an outside observer. No stimulus is fabricated; reality testing is intact.
🌫️ Derealization
Perceiving the external world as changed or unreal.
Know Def
The person experiences their surroundings as dreamlike, foggy, or unreal. Contrast with depersonalization (self) — derealization affects the perception of the external world.
⭐ Halo Effect
One positive dominant trait → assume all traits are positive.
MCQ
Example: Attractive person assumed to be also intelligent, kind, and honest.
Mechanism: Cognitive shortcut bypassing objective evaluation of each trait.
😈 Devil Effect
One negative dominant trait → assume all traits are negative.
MCQ
Example: Unattractive person assumed to be dumb or dishonest.
Opposite: Halo effect (positive). Devil effect → horns effect in some texts.
