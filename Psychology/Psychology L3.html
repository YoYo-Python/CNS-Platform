Classical Conditioning
Pavlov • Watson
Learning by association — a neutral stimulus is paired with an unconditioned stimulus to produce a conditioned response. Involves involuntary, autonomic responses. Does not create novel behavior.
⚡ High-Yield 📝 Exam Favorite
⚠️ Classical conditioning involves pairing two stimuli during acquisition and does NOT create novel behavior.
🐀
Operant Conditioning
Skinner • Thorndike
Learning through rewards and punishments — behavior is regulated by its consequences. Uses voluntary, cranio-spinal system. Creates novel behavior. Uses Skinner Box.
⚡ High-Yield 📝 Exam Favorite
⚠️ The reinforcement vs punishment slide is "extremely important" for both written and MCQ exams.
🔄
Non-Associative Learning
Habituation & Sensitization
Habituation: Progressive diminution of response when stimulus is neither rewarding nor harmful.
Sensitization: Progressive amplification of response to a stimulus.
📖 Core Concept
🏥
Behavioral Therapies
Clinical Applications
Based on learning theory to relieve symptoms by unlearning maladaptive behaviors. Includes Systematic Desensitization, Flooding, Implosion, Aversive Conditioning, Token Economy, and Biofeedback.
🏥 Clinical Pearl 📝 Exam Favorite

Observational Learning
Mirror Neurons • Modeling
Learning by imitating another's behavior, critically relying on mirror neurons. Key to social learning and the basis of Modeling therapy.
📖 Core Concept
😔
Learned Helplessness
Animal Model of Depression
Animals receiving inescapable pain become apathetic and hopeless, even when escape is later possible. Primary animal model for human depression. Antidepressants increase escape attempts.

📅 Learning Types Timeline

Progression of learning theory concepts

🔄 Non-Associative Learning

The simplest forms: Habituation (decreased response to repeated benign stimuli) and Sensitization (increased response). No pairing of stimuli required.

🔔 Classical Conditioning (Pavlov)

Learning by association. Neutral stimulus + UCS → CR. Key: involuntary responses, autonomic nervous system, does NOT create novel behavior.

🧩 Trial & Error (Thorndike)

Puzzle box experiments. Established the Law of Effect: responses producing satisfaction are strengthened; responses producing discomfort are weakened.

🐀 Operant Conditioning (Skinner)

Skinner Box. Behavior determined by consequences. Reinforcement (positive/negative) increases behavior; Punishment (positive/negative) decreases behavior. Creates novel behavior.

👀 Observational Learning

Learning through imitation. Mirror neurons play a critical role. Foundation for Modeling therapy.

🏥 Clinical Applications

Systematic Desensitization, Flooding, Implosion, Aversive Conditioning, Token Economy, Biofeedback, and CBT applied to treat phobias, addictions, paraphilias, and psychiatric disorders.

🎯 High-Yield Signs & Phenomena

Key learning phenomena you must know

🔻 Extinction

CS presented without UCS → CR disappears over time.

📝 Testable
🔄 Spontaneous Recovery

Temporary return of extinguished CR after passage of time.

⚡ High-Yield
🌐 Stimulus Generalization

Responding similarly to stimuli resembling the original CS (e.g., rat → cat).

📝 Testable
🎯 Stimulus Discrimination

Learned ability to differentiate between stimuli, responding only to specific CS.

⚡ Disinhibition

Temporary increase of extinguished response due to an unrelated external stimulus.

⚡ High-Yield
📈 Shaping

Reinforcing successive approximations toward the desired behavior (e.g., teaching writing).

🧘 Reciprocal Inhibition

Cannot be fearful and relaxed simultaneously—key to Systematic Desensitization.

🏥 Clinical
🐣 Imprinting

Rapid, early-life learning creating strong attachment (e.g., ducklings following first moving object).

🔔 Classical Conditioning Process

1
Before Conditioning

Neutral Stimulus (NS) e.g., bell → No relevant response
Unconditioned Stimulus (UCS) e.g., food → Unconditioned Response (UCR) e.g., salivation

2
During Conditioning (Acquisition)

NS + UCS presented together repeatedly → UCR is elicited. The NS is being paired with the UCS.

3
After Conditioning

Conditioned Stimulus (CS) (formerly NS, e.g., bell alone) → Conditioned Response (CR) (e.g., salivation). Learning complete!

4
Extinction (if applicable)

CS presented without UCS repeatedly → CR gradually disappears. May show Spontaneous Recovery later.

🧘 Systematic Desensitization Steps

1
Teach Relaxation Skills

Muscle relaxation, diaphragmatic breathing, guided imagery. Patient must master these first.

2
Construct Fear Hierarchy

List fear-provoking stimuli from mildly anxious to intensely fearful.

3
Begin Desensitization

Vividly imagine mild fear scenes while maintaining relaxation. Based on reciprocal inhibition.

4
Real-Life Confrontation

Therapist encourages facing fears in real life, starting with milder stimuli and progressing up the hierarchy.

🐀 Operant Conditioning Decision Tree

?
Does the behavior INCREASE or DECREASE?

If behavior increases → It's Reinforcement
If behavior decreases → It's Punishment

+
Is something ADDED or REMOVED?

Added (Positive): Something is presented/given.
Removed (Negative): Something is taken away.

✓
Combine to Classify

• Behavior ↑ + Add favorable = Positive Reinforcement (praise)
• Behavior ↑ + Remove unfavorable = Negative Reinforcement (end detention)
• Behavior ↓ + Add unfavorable = Positive Punishment (yelling)
• Behavior ↓ + Remove favorable = Negative Punishment (take privileges)

💊 Behavioral Therapies

Clinical applications of learning principles

🔵 Based on Classical Conditioning

🧘 Systematic Desensitization

Gradual exposure to feared stimulus hierarchy while maintaining relaxation. Based on reciprocal inhibition — cannot be anxious and relaxed simultaneously.

Steps: 1) Teach relaxation → 2) Build fear hierarchy → 3) Imagine mild fears while relaxed → 4) Real-life exposure
Used for: Phobias

🌊 Flooding

Exposure to actual overwhelming dose of feared stimulus until fear subsides through habituation.

Key: Real exposure, no hierarchy
Used for: Phobias

💭 Implosion

Exposure to imagined overwhelming dose of feared stimulus. Similar to flooding but uses imagination.

Key: Imagined exposure only
Used for: Phobias

⚡ Aversive Conditioning

Pairs a pleasurable but maladaptive stimulus with an aversive/painful one (electric shock, nausea-inducing drug).

Used for: Paraphilias (e.g., pedophilia), alcohol addiction
Example: Disulfiram (Antabuse) for alcoholism
🟢 Based on Operant Conditioning

🏅 Token Economy

Desirable behaviors are reinforced with tokens. Based on positive reinforcement principles.

Used for: Psychotic, autistic, mentally retarded patients
Example: Token for combing hair, shaving

📊 Biofeedback

Provides ongoing physiologic information to reinforce patient's use of relaxation techniques for visceral control.

Used for: Hypertension, migraine, chronic pain
Mechanism: Patient monitors own blood pressure/readings as reinforcement
🟡 Cognitive-Based

🧠 Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

Identifies distorted, negative thoughts and replaces them with positive, self-assuring ones.

Used for: Depression, anxiety, and various psychiatric conditions
Key: Addresses cognitive distortions, not just behaviors

👀 Modeling

Patient learns new behavior by observing and imitating someone performing the desired behavior.

Based on: Observational learning, mirror neurons
Used for: Social skills, phobias
⚠️ Dangers of Punishment: Can lead to increased aggression, physical harm, emotional trauma, decreased self-esteem, and mistrust. Should be immediate, non-physical, and clearly communicated.
