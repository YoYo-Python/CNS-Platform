Topographic Theory

3 levels: Conscious (logical/secondary process), Preconscious (easily recalled), Unconscious (repressed/primary process). Iceberg analogy.

High Yield

Structural Theory

Id (birth, instincts, unconscious), Ego (just after birth, reality testing, defenses), Superego (+6yrs, morals). Ego mediates between Id & Superego.

⚠️ EXAM
🛡️

Defense Mechanisms

Unconscious Ego techniques keeping conflict out of conscious mind → ↓anxiety, maintain equilibrium & self-esteem. Repression is foundational.

⚠️ EXAMHigh Yield
🔄

Transference

Patient → Doctor (positive/negative). Countertransference: Doctor → Patient. Both unconscious. Negative → poor adherence; Counter → impaired judgment.

Other Personality Theories

📅 Structural Development Timeline

At Birth

🔴 Id Development

Entirely unconscious. Contains instinctive sexual & aggressive drives. Primary process thinking. No logic, no time concept. Pleasure principle only.

Just After Birth

🟢 Ego Development Begins

Controls Id expression. Adapts to external reality via reality testing. Spans conscious, preconscious & unconscious. Uses defense mechanisms. Problem solver.

+6 Years

🔵 Superego Development

Moral values, rules, conscience from parents & society. Constantly suppresses Id's unacceptable drives. Causes guilt. Spans all 3 topographic levels.

Ongoing (Epigenetics)

🟡 Personality Consolidation

Continuous interaction of genetics + environment. Epigenetics modifies gene expression. Personality remains relatively stable but modifiable.

📝 Essay Questions

Q1: Describe the interaction between Id, Ego, and Superego when experiencing a sudden aggressive impulse, including topographical levels.

Sources: PDF Slides & Lecture Audio

The Id operates entirely in the Unconscious, pushing for immediate primitive gratification of the aggressive impulse (primary process thinking). The Superego, operating across the Unconscious, Preconscious, and Conscious, applies internalized moral rules to block this impulse. The Ego, which also spans all three topographic levels, mediates this conflict by utilizing reality testing and triggering unconscious Defense Mechanisms (e.g., Repression or Displacement) to prevent the conflict from entering the conscious mind, thereby reducing anxiety.

Q2: Compare Mature vs. Immature Defense Mechanisms with examples and impact on equilibrium.

Sources: PDF Slides

Mature defense mechanisms (e.g., Altruism — volunteering to avoid negative self-image; Humor — joking about one's own insecurities) assist the patient or others directly in dealing with conflict, maintaining equilibrium while remaining functional and socially adaptive.

Immature defense mechanisms (e.g., Regression — reverting to childlike behavior under stress; Denial — refusing to accept liver cirrhosis diagnosis) are manifestations of childlike or disturbed behavior. While they successfully keep acute anxiety out of the conscious mind, excessive reliance on them distorts external reality and becomes a barrier to adapting to life situations or adhering to medical care.

🏥 Clinical Scenarios

Applied exam-style vignettes with structured answers

🧩 Long Integrative Scenario
A 17-year-old female student was subjected to severe public punishment by her school principal. She had to stand by the wall with her hands raised in the schoolyard while other girls watched and mocked her. Suddenly, the girl fell to the ground as if she lost consciousness. When awakened by teachers, she began crying and talking in the exact manner of a 3-year-old child.
  1. Identify: Patient exhibits Immature Defense Mechanisms — specifically Regression (talking like a 3-year-old) and likely Dissociation (loss of consciousness) — in response to unbearable psychosocial stress.
  2. Mechanism: External intolerable stimulus (public shame) → Ego could not resolve the conflict consciously → Ego utilized unconscious defense mechanisms → Reverted behavioral patterns to an earlier, safer developmental stage (Regression) to escape present reality.
  3. Alternatives: Behaviorists might argue it is a learned escape behavior. Strictly psychoanalytically, it is an unconscious Ego defense.
  4. Investigation: Psychoanalysis (Freud's couch method) to explore unconscious repressions — though not used in acute settings.
  5. If defense fails: Conscious mind is overwhelmed with severe anxiety and unbearable shame.
  6. Broader principle: Topographic Theory (unconscious drives taking over conscious behavior) + Structural Theory (Ego failing to satisfy external reality).
  7. Compensatory: The defense mechanism itself is the compensatory mechanism protecting the psyche from complete breakdown.

🧭 Algorithm: Identifying a Defense Mechanism in Exam Questions

1

Identify the Stimulus

What is the unbearable/unacceptable situation or drive? (Trauma? Shame? Aggressive impulse? Diagnosis?)

2

Identify the Behavioral Response

How is the person reacting? (Denying? Blaming others? Acting childlike? Intellectualizing? Joking?)

3

Ask: Is it Conscious or Unconscious?

If deliberately avoiding → Suppression. Everything else → unconscious defense mechanism.

4

Match the Pattern

Use the defense mechanism definitions: Projection = attributing to others; Displacement = redirecting to safer target; Regression = younger behavior; etc.

5

Classify: Mature vs. Immature

Mature (Altruism, Humor, Suppression, Sublimation) vs. Immature (all others). Mature = functional/adaptive.

💊 Defense Mechanisms — Complete Catalog

All defense mechanisms organized by Mature vs. Immature with definitions and clinical examples

✅ Mature Defense Mechanisms Adaptive

  • 🌟
    Altruism: Assisting others to manage own negative personal feelings. Ex: Man with poor self-image volunteers extensively at charity.
  • 😄
    Humor: Expressing uncomfortable feelings without causing emotional discomfort to self or others. Ex: Short man jokes about short people.
  • 🤚
    Suppression: Deliberately & consciously pushing unacceptable emotions out of awareness. ONLY defense with conscious aspect. Ex: Teen deliberately changes topic from painful love stories.
  • 🎨
    Sublimation: Transforming unacceptable drives into socially acceptable, constructive activities. Ex: Aggressive person becomes a successful boxer.

⚠️ Immature Defense Mechanisms Maladaptive if excessive

  • 🔒
    Repression: Foundational defense. Unconsciously pushing unacceptable emotions into the unconscious. Ex: Completely forgetting severe childhood trauma.
  • 🙈
    Denial: Unconsciously not accepting unbearable aspects of reality. Ex: Man with liver cirrhosis insists he's just a "social drinker" and liver is fine.
  • 👉
    Projection: Unconsciously attributing own threatening beliefs/emotions to another person. Ex: Jealous person accuses colleague of jealousy.
  • 🔄
    Displacement: Moving negative emotions from intolerable subject to less threatening, tolerable target. Ex: Doctor angry at wife → harsh to female residents.
  • 🔃
    Reaction Formation: Repressing threatening beliefs & unconsciously replacing them with their opposites. Ex: Mother who resents children → buys expensive gifts.
  • 👶
    Regression: Reverting to behavior patterns of a younger developmental age. Ex: Pregnant woman becomes frightened toddler demanding husband's constant presence.
  • 🧩
    Dissociation: Mentally separating consciousness from real-life traumatic events. Ex: Teen in fatal car accident has NO memory of it.
  • 📖
    Intellectualization: Heavy emphasis on thinking/technicalities to avoid experiencing associated emotion. Ex: Surgeon whose patient died gives emotionless technical lecture on why.
  • 🎭
    Identification (Introjection): Unconsciously patterning behavior after someone more powerful (often the aggressor). Ex: Abused child becomes punitive, critical teacher.
  • ↩️
    Undoing: Performing an action to symbolically "cancel out" a previous unacceptable action or thought.
  • 💭
    Fantasy: Retreating into an imaginary world to escape reality.

🎯 Key Exam Distinctions

  • ⚡
    Repression vs. Suppression: Repression = unconscious (foundational). Suppression = conscious & deliberate (mature).
  • ⚡
    Denial vs. Repression: Denial = not accepting external reality. Repression = pushing internal emotions into the unconscious.
  • ⚡
    Projection vs. Displacement: Projection = attributing own feelings to others. Displacement = redirecting feelings to a different target.
  • ⚡
    Intellectualization vs. Dissociation: Intellectualization = overly technical thinking about the event. Dissociation = complete memory gap / separation from the event.
  • ⚡
    Transference vs. Projection: Transference = specific clinical patient-doctor dynamic. Projection = general defense mechanism (attributing to anyone).
