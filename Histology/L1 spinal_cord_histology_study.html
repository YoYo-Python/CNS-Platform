📋 Overview & Key Concepts

🎓 Start here! These cards cover the core distinctions your lecturer emphasized. Gray vs White Matter and the BBB are the most frequently tested concepts. Let's go! 💪

⚪ White Matter

Outer peripheral region. Composed of myelinated ascending/descending axons, oligodendrocytes, and blood capillaries. Appears white due to myelin lipids. No nerve cell bodies. Function: Rapid long-distance signal conduction via tracts.

⭐ High-Yield🔴 Exam

🔘 Gray Matter

Central H-shaped core. Contains multipolar nerve cell bodies, unmyelinated fibers, protoplasmic astrocytes, and capillaries. Appears gray due to absence of myelin. Divided into anterior, posterior, and lateral horns.

⭐ High-Yield🔴 Exam

🛡️ Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB)

Three layers: (1) Continuous non-fenestrated capillary endothelium → (2) Thick basement membrane → (3) Astrocyte feet. Protects sensitive CNS from toxins, bacteria, and drugs.

🔴 Exam📝 MCQ💎 Pearl

🧫 Anterior Horn Cells (LM)

5 key features (5-point question!): Multipolar shape · Rounded euchromatic nucleus · Prominent Nissl granules · Well-developed Golgi apparatus · Neurofibrils (silver stain only).

🔴 Exam⭐ High-Yield

🔲 Ependymal Cells

Cuboidal/columnar cells lining the central canal. Apically: cilia (CSF movement) + long microvilli (absorption). Basally: NO basement membrane — instead, fine processes extend into nervous tissue.

📝 MCQ💎 Pearl

🌀 Oligodendrocytes

CNS myelin-forming cells. One oligodendrocyte wraps multiple axons simultaneously (vs. Schwann cells in PNS = one segment of one axon). Loss → Multiple Sclerosis.

⭐ High-Yield🔴 Exam

🔵 Microglia

The "policeman" of the CNS. Immune cells — activated by injury or infection. Perform phagocytosis to clear debris. Respond acutely to trauma and chronic disease.

🔬 Glial

🔀 Golgi Type I vs II

Type I: Long axons, grouped in nuclei, form tracts for long-distance projection. Type II: Short axons, act as interneurons, associative or commissural. Both are multipolar neurons of the gray matter.

🔴 Exam
🗂️ Gray Matter Horns — Functional Overview

🔼 Anterior (Ventral) Horn — Motor

Contains multipolar motor neuron cell bodies. Thick in cervical (upper limb supply) and lumbar enlargements. Thin in thoracic (trunk only). Golgi Type I cells project long axons out via ventral roots.

🔽 Posterior (Dorsal) Horn — Sensory

Receives incoming sensory fibers from dorsal root ganglia. Processes pain, temperature, touch. Governs the ascending sensory tracts in the posterior funiculus.

↔️ Lateral Horn — Autonomic (T1–L2/3 & S2–S4)

Only present at specific segments. Contains preganglionic sympathetic (T1–L2/3) and parasympathetic (S2–S4) neurons. Key identification feature for thoracic cross-sections.

🔗 Gray Commissure — Bridge

Transversely connects the right and left halves of the gray matter around the central canal. Allows communication between both sides of the cord.

📐 White Matter Funiculi

⬆️ Dorsal Funiculus

Ascending only. Carries fine touch, vibration, proprioception to brain.

↕️ Lateral Funiculus

Contains both ascending and descending tracts. Includes corticospinal and spinocerebellar.

↕️ Anterior Funiculus

Both ascending and descending tracts. Contains reticulospinal, tectospinal tracts.

Case 1 — The Failing Insulation
A demyelination scenario
A 32-year-old presents with progressive weakness and sensory loss in both legs over 6 months. An MRI of the spinal cord shows multiple plaques in the lateral and posterior funiculi. A histological biopsy of the white matter reveals loss of the insulating lipid-rich sheaths around axons, with relative preservation of axons themselves.
❓ Q1: What cells are responsible for forming the destroyed structures in the white matter?
❓ Q2: How does one of these cells differ from its PNS counterpart in terms of the number of axons it can serve?
❓ Q3: What is the clinical diagnosis?
1
Oligodendrocytes form the myelin sheaths in the CNS. Their myelin is composed of lipid-rich membrane, giving white matter its characteristic color and insulating function.
2
One oligodendrocyte wraps multiple axons simultaneously via its many processes. In contrast, one Schwann cell (PNS) myelinates only a single internode of a single axon.
3
The clinical diagnosis is Multiple Sclerosis — a CNS demyelinating disease characterized by plaques in white matter tracts.
🔬
Case 2 — The Impenetrable Wall
Blood-Brain Barrier physiology
A researcher injects Evans Blue dye (a large molecule that binds albumin) intravenously into a rat. After 30 minutes, organs throughout the body are stained blue. However, a histological cross-section of the spinal cord shows no blue staining in the gray matter, even though blood capillaries are present.
❓ Q1: Name the THREE structural layers that prevented dye entry into the spinal cord tissue.
❓ Q2: What is the crucial type of capillary endothelium in this barrier and why does it matter?
❓ Q3: What would happen clinically if the astrocyte feet component of this barrier were selectively destroyed?
1
The three lines of defense: (1) Continuous (non-fenestrated) capillary endothelium — no gaps for large molecules. (2) Thick basement membrane — physical barrier around the capillary. (3) Astrocyte feet (neuroglial processes) — tightly enwrap the basement membrane.
2
The endothelium is continuous and non-fenestrated. This is critical — fenestrations would allow substances to leak into the tissue. ⚠️ This is a classic MCQ trap: the answer is CONTINUOUS, not fenestrated.
3
Loss of astrocyte feet would compromise the third layer of the BBB, allowing toxins, bacteria, and drugs to enter the highly sensitive CNS tissue, disrupting the constant metabolic environment neurons require.
⚡
Case 3 — The Silver Mystery
Neuronal LM structure
A histology slide of the spinal cord anterior horn is stained with a silver impregnation technique. Under the microscope, a network of thin, black, thread-like fibrils is visible within the cytoplasm of large multipolar cells. A neighboring slide stained with H&E shows the same cells with a rounded, pale nucleus and dark blue granular material throughout the cytoplasm.
❓ Q1: Identify the two specific intracellular structures highlighted by each stain.
❓ Q2: What does the rounded, pale-staining nucleus indicate about the metabolic state of these cells?
❓ Q3: What type of neuron (Golgi classification) are these large anterior horn cells?
1
Silver stain → highlights Neurofibrils (the cytoskeletal network maintaining neuronal shape). H&E stain → highlights Nissl granules (rough ER; responsible for protein synthesis).
2
The rounded, pale (euchromatic) nucleus indicates HIGH metabolic and synthetic activity. Euchromatin = uncoiled DNA = actively transcribing genes. This makes sense as motor neurons have very high metabolic demands.
3
These are Golgi Type I neurons — large cells with long axons grouped in nuclei, projecting long distances out of the spinal cord via ventral roots to innervate muscles.
💧
Case 4 — Still Waters
CSF circulation failure
A patient presents with headaches and signs of raised intracranial pressure. An MRI reveals dilation of the central canal (syringomyelia). A histological biopsy of the central canal lining shows cells with a columnar morphology. Electron microscopy reveals that the apical surface of these cells lacks the normal hair-like projections seen in a healthy specimen.
❓ Q1: What cells line the central canal and what is absent on their apical surface?
❓ Q2: What other apical structure remains and what is its function?
❓ Q3: What is unique about the BASAL aspect of these cells compared to all other epithelial cells?
1
Ependymal cells line the central canal. The missing apical structures are cilia, which normally beat rhythmically to propel and circulate CSF through the canal. Loss of cilia → CSF stagnation.
2
The remaining apical structures are long microvilli, which function in absorption and modification of CSF composition (not propulsion — that's the cilia's job).
3
Ependymal cells are unique in that they do NOT rest on a basement membrane — a key MCQ fact! Instead, their basal surface has fine processes that extend directly into the underlying nervous tissue for anchoring.
🩻
Case 5 — The Spinal Cross-Section Puzzle
Regional identification
A pathologist examining two cross-sections of spinal cord from an unknown level. Section A: oval outline, very thick ventral horns, thin dorsal horns, large amount of white matter, NO lateral horns. Section B: rounded outline, thin ventral horns, thin dorsal horns, a distinct small lateral horn present between the ventral and dorsal horns.
❓ Q1: Identify the spinal cord level of each section (A and B).
❓ Q2: Why does Section A have thicker ventral horns than Section B?
❓ Q3: What neurons populate the lateral horn in Section B and what are their functional levels?
1
Section A = Cervical (oval shape, thick ventral horns for upper limb motor supply, no lateral horn). Section B = Thoracic (rounded, thin horns, distinct lateral horn for sympathetic outflow).
2
The cervical cord supplies motor innervation to the upper limbs, which requires a large number of motor neurons → thick ventral (anterior) horns. The thoracic cord only supplies the trunk muscles → fewer motor neurons needed → thin ventral horns.
3
The lateral horn contains preganglionic sympathetic neurons at levels T1–L2/3 and preganglionic parasympathetic neurons at S2–S4. Section B (thoracic) would contain sympathetic preganglionic cells.
🚨
Case 6 — Integrative: T4 Spinal Trauma
Long integrative scenario from lecture
A patient suffers traumatic spinal cord injury at T4 after a motor vehicle accident. The initial impact physically disrupts the cord's white and gray matter. Over the next 48 hours, the wound becomes infected. Within weeks, the patient develops complete paralysis below the level of injury. A histological specimen shows loss of Nissl bodies in anterior horn cells, activated microglial cells, and a developing glial scar.
❓ Q1: Which barrier was disrupted by the trauma, allowing infection to enter?
❓ Q2: Which cell is the CNS's first responder to the infection and what is its mechanism of action?
❓ Q3: What does loss of Nissl bodies in anterior horn cells indicate?
❓ Q4: What do activated astrocytes form in response to chronic damage and what is its consequence?
1
The Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB) was disrupted — specifically the continuous endothelium and astrocyte feet. Trauma physically tears these structures, opening the CNS to circulating pathogens.
2
Microglia — the "policeman" of the CNS. They are activated by injury/infection and perform phagocytosis to clear cellular debris, pathogens, and myelin breakdown products.
3
Loss of Nissl bodies (chromatolysis) indicates severe neuronal stress or injury. Since Nissl granules = rough ER for protein synthesis, their loss means the motor neuron is metabolically compromised and may be undergoing degeneration → hence the paralysis.
4
Activated astrocytes proliferate and form a glial scar. While initially protective (containing the damage), it ultimately prevents axon regeneration, contributing to the permanence of CNS injuries — a key reason the CNS heals poorly compared to the PNS.

🔘 Gray Matter — Full Detail

The central H-shaped core of the spinal cord. Components: multipolar nerve cell bodies (the defining feature), unmyelinated fibers (beginning/terminations of axons), protoplasmic astrocytes, and blood capillaries. Appears gray due to absence of myelin. Divided into: Anterior horn (motor, Golgi I neurons), Posterior horn (sensory), Lateral horn (autonomic, only T1–L2/3 and S2–S4), and Gray Commissure (connects both halves around central canal).

🔴 Key Exam Topic⭐ High-Yield

⚪ White Matter — Full Detail

Peripheral region of the spinal cord. Contains myelinated ascending and descending axons, oligodendrocytes, and blood capillaries. Appears white due to myelin lipids. Divided into: Dorsal funiculus (ascending only), Lateral funiculus (ascending + descending), Anterior funiculus (ascending + descending). Also contains the anterior white commissure connecting both sides.

🛡️ Blood-Brain Barrier — Full Detail

Layer 1: Continuous (non-fenestrated) capillary endothelium with tight junctions — prevents bulk flow. Layer 2: Thick basement membrane — physical molecular sieve. Layer 3: Astrocyte feet (neuroglial processes) wrapping the basement membrane. Function: maintains constant metabolic environment; blocks toxins, bacteria, and most drugs. ⚠️ CRITICAL MCQ: endothelium is CONTINUOUS, not fenestrated.

🔴 Short Note Question📝 MCQ Trap

🌀 Oligodendrocytes — Full Detail

Myelin-forming cells of the CNS. One oligodendrocyte extends multiple processes, each wrapping a different axon segment — so one cell myelinates multiple axons simultaneously. Compare to PNS: one Schwann cell = one internode of one axon. Clinical relevance: destruction of oligodendrocytes → demyelination → Multiple Sclerosis. Also responsible for the white color of white matter.

🔲 Ependymal Cells — Full Detail

Cuboidal-to-columnar cells lining the central canal. Apical border: cilia (move CSF) + long microvilli (absorb CSF). Basal border: ⚠️ NO basement membrane — instead, fine processes extend into nervous tissue for anchoring. They circulate and modify CSF. Loss of cilia → CSF stagnation → raised ICP (syringomyelia).

📝 No Basement Membrane — Classic MCQ!

🔵 Microglia — Full Detail

The "policeman" of the CNS. Small cells with reactive morphology. Function: immune defense via phagocytosis. Activated by injury, infection, or ischemia. In trauma: they clear myelin debris and cellular remnants. Contribute to glial scar formation. Equivalent to macrophages in the periphery. Found throughout both gray and white matter.

⭐ Astrocytes — Full Detail

Protoplasmic astrocytes (in gray matter): provide metabolic support to neurons. Fibrous astrocytes (in white matter): structural support. Key role: their feet enwrap capillaries forming the third layer of the BBB. In injury: they proliferate and form a glial scar that prevents regeneration. Also regulate the ionic and metabolic environment.

⚡ Anterior Horn Motor Neuron — LM Features (5-point!)

1. Multipolar shape — multiple dendrites + one axon. 2. Rounded euchromatic nucleus — pale, large, indicating HIGH synthetic activity (open chromatin = active transcription). 3. Prominent Nissl granules — = rough ER, responsible for protein synthesis. 4. Well-developed Golgi apparatus — for packaging and secretion. 5. Neurofibrils — cytoskeletal filaments maintaining shape; requires SILVER STAIN to visualize. ⚠️ Memorize all 5!

🧪 Diagnosis & Investigations

How to identify spinal cord histological structures and their associated pathologies

🔬 Histological Staining — Key Findings
H&E → Nissl granules (blue)
Silver stain → Neurofibrils (black)
Osmium → Myelin sheaths
Luxol Fast Blue → Myelin (blue)
Golgi stain → Full neuron shape
No stain for BBB endothelium type → Need EM or special immunostains
🗺️ Diagnostic Algorithm: Identify the Spinal Level
1

Check overall shape

Oval → Cervical. Rounded → Thoracic. Oval/round with large horns → Lumbar/Sacral.

2

Assess ventral horn size

Thick ventral horns = Cervical (upper limb) or Lumbar (lower limb). Thin = Thoracic (trunk only).

3

Look for lateral horn

Present = Thoracic (T1–L2/3) or Sacral (S2–S4). Absent = Cervical below C8, or lower lumbar.

4

Assess white-to-gray ratio

More white matter relative to gray = Higher levels (more descending fibers yet to synapse). Less relative white = Lower levels (most tracts already exited).

💊 Clinical Management

Conditions arising from spinal cord histology pathology — management overview

📚 Note: This section is based on histological principles from the lecture. Management is contextualized from the pathological mechanisms described. For full pharmacology, refer to dedicated pharmacology notes.

🚨 Acute Spinal Trauma — Emergency

  • Immobilize spine immediately
  • IV methylprednisolone (reduce inflammation, protect BBB)
  • Surgical decompression if indicated
  • ICU monitoring for autonomic dysreflexia
  • Prevent secondary BBB breakdown

🦠 BBB Breach + CNS Infection

  • IV antibiotics (BBB-penetrating: e.g., cephalosporins)
  • Dexamethasone to reduce neuroinflammation
  • Target: restore astrocyte barrier function
  • Monitor CSF via lumbar puncture

🔵 Multiple Sclerosis (Demyelination)

  • Interferon-beta / glatiramer acetate (disease-modifying)
  • Natalizumab (blocks lymphocyte entry through BBB)
  • Acute relapse: IV corticosteroids
  • Goal: protect remaining oligodendrocytes
  • Remyelination research: stem cell therapy

⚡ ALS — Motor Neuron Disease

  • Riluzole (reduces glutamate excitotoxicity)
  • Edaravone (antioxidant, slows decline)
  • Multidisciplinary: physio, speech, respiratory
  • No cure — supportive management focus

💧 Syringomyelia (Ependymal Dysfunction)

  • Treat underlying cause (e.g., Chiari malformation)
  • Surgical: syrinx shunting or posterior fossa decompression
  • Physiotherapy for motor deficits
  • Pain management (central neuropathic pain)

🔧 General Supportive — CNS Injury

  • Neuroprotection: minimize secondary injury (hypoxia, hypotension)
  • Rehabilitation: promote neural plasticity
  • Bladder/bowel management if autonomic involvement
  • Prevent pressure sores and DVT

🔬 Research / Future Directions

  • Oligodendrocyte precursor transplantation for MS
  • BBB-targeted drug delivery systems
  • Nanoparticles to cross BBB for CNS drug delivery
  • Glial scar dissolution (chondroitinase ABC)
  • Exosome therapy for axonal regeneration

🧫 Histological Investigation Tools

  • H&E stain → Nissl bodies (neuronal health)
  • Silver stain → Neurofibrils (structural integrity)
  • Luxol Fast Blue → Myelin integrity
  • Immunohistochemistry → Specific glial markers (GFAP for astrocytes, MBP for myelin)
