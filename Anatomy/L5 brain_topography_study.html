Sulci, Gyri & Cortical Areas — Mansoura University Anatomy
📐 Hemisphere Basics
Each cerebral hemisphere has 3 Poles (Frontal, Occipital, Temporal), 3 Borders (Superior, Inferomedial, Inferolateral), and 3 Surfaces (Superolateral, Medial, Inferior). The inferolateral border features a preoccipital notch 4 cm from the occipital pole — key for demarcating lobes.
✂️ Major Sulci — The Dividers
4 sulci divide the hemisphere into 4 lobes: Central, Lateral, Calcarine, Parieto-occipital.
The lateral sulcus has a short stem + 3 rami (anterior, ascending, posterior).
The Insula is buried at the bottom of the lateral sulcus.

🔥 High-Yield Key Facts

⬆️
Homunculus is Upside-DownLower 1/3 of pre/postcentral gyri = head. Upper 2/3 = body. Paracentral lobule = leg/foot/perineum.
📏
Size ≠ FunctionCortical area size is proportional to innervation density, NOT anatomical body part size.
👁️
Insula HiddenThe Insula is buried at the bottom of the lateral sulcus. Has gustatory & emotional functions. Must pull lobes apart to see it.
🏛️
Dominant HemisphereBroca's (44/45), Wernicke's (22), Angular gyrus (39) only cause aphasia in DOMINANT hemisphere lesions.
👃
Uncus TrickyDestructive lesion → Anosmia. Irritative lesion → Olfactory hallucinations (uncinate fits).
👁️‍🗨️
Area 8 Eye DeviationArea 8 moves eyes to OPPOSITE side. Lesion → ipsilateral eye deviation (eyes look toward lesion).
🎨
Non-DominantNon-dominant equivalent of speech areas → disturbances in drawing, spatial tasks, not aphasia.
🖐️
Astereognosis TestPlace object in closed hand. Can feel it but can't identify it? → Areas 5,7,40 lesion (supramarginal gyrus).

Diagnostic Approach 🔬
Deficit-to-lesion mapping and diagnostic algorithm
🗺️ Deficit → Lesion Localization Algorithm
1
Motor deficit? Contralateral hemiplegia (whole body) → Area 4 — contralateral hemiplegia (leg only) → Paracentral lobule
↓
2
Motor without paralysis? Can't perform learned movements → Area 6 (Premotor) → Apraxia. Eyes deviate toward lesion side → Area 8
↓
3
Speech/language deficit? Can't produce speech but understands → Broca's 44/45 Motor aphasia. Can't understand speech → Wernicke's 22 Sensory aphasia. Can't read/write → Angular gyrus 39
↓
4
Sensory deficit? Can't identify objects by touch (astereognosis) → Areas 5,7,40. Ignores one body side → Area 40 Unilateral neglect. Can't feel anything → Areas 3,1,2
↓
5
Visual deficit? Half visual field loss (macular sparing) → Area 17. Can see but can't recognize → Areas 18/19 Visual agnosia. Can't recognize faces → Area 37 Prosopagnosia
↓
6
Personality/behavior change? Poor judgment, socially inappropriate, unkempt → Prefrontal Areas 9-12

Deficits & Management 💊
Cortical lesion syndromes, clinical presentations, and management principles
⚡ Emergency Note: Acute cortical lesions (stroke) require immediate neuroimaging (CT/MRI), airway management, and neurology referral. The deficit pattern localizes the lesion to guide intervention.
🟠 Frontal — Motor
Area 4 Lesion
  • Contralateral hemiplegia (UMN pattern)
  • Whole body if full area 4 infarcted
  • Leg only if paracentral lobule alone
  • Spasticity, hyperreflexia, upgoing plantar
🟠 Frontal — Premotor
Area 6 Lesion
  • Apraxia — can't perform learned motor acts
  • Muscle strength intact
  • No paralysis
  • Ideomotor or ideational apraxia
🟠 Frontal — Eye Field
Area 8 Lesion
  • Ipsilateral conjugate eye deviation
  • "Eyes look toward the lesion"
  • Horizontal gaze palsy
🟠 Frontal — Broca's
Areas 44/45 Lesion
  • Motor (Expressive) Aphasia
  • Understands perfectly
  • Can't articulate words
  • Frustrated, aware of deficit
  • Only in DOMINANT hemisphere
🟠 Frontal — Prefrontal
Areas 9-12 Lesion
  • Personality change
  • Poor judgment & social inhibition
  • Unkempt appearance
  • Phineas Gage syndrome
🟣 Parietal — Primary Sensory
Areas 3,1,2 Lesion
  • Contralateral hemianesthesia
  • Loss of all modalities contralateral side
  • Upside-down somatotopic loss
🟣 Parietal — Association
Areas 5,7,40 Lesion
  • Astereognosis (tactile agnosia)
  • Can feel but can't identify by touch
  • Area 40 alone → unilateral neglect
🟣 Parietal — Angular Gyrus
Area 39 Lesion (Dominant)
  • Alexia — can't read
  • Agraphia — can't write
  • Non-dominant → drawing disturbances
🟢 Temporal — Wernicke's
Area 22 Lesion (Dominant)
  • Sensory (Receptive) Aphasia
  • Fluent speech — but nonsensical
  • Can't understand spoken language
  • Auditory agnosia
🟢 Temporal — Face Area
Area 37 Lesion
  • Prosopagnosia
  • Can't recognize familiar faces
  • Vision intact, identity failed
🩷 Occipital — Primary Visual
Area 17 Lesion
  • Contralateral homonymous hemianopia
  • With MACULAR SPARING
  • (Macular cortex has dual blood supply)
🩷 Occipital — Visual Assoc.
Areas 18/19 Lesion
  • Visual agnosia
  • Sees clearly — can't recognize
  • Objects & scenes meaningless

Speech Aphasia Types — Who CAN do what?
B-ROCA = BROKE OUTPUT

Broca's is BROKEN OUTPUT — can't speak. Wernicke's is WRONG WORDS — can't understand.

  • B
    Broca's (44/45) — Output BROKEN. Understands ✅ | Speaks ❌ | Frustrated
  • W
    Wernicke's (22) — Wrong Words Everywhere. Speaks ✅ | Understands ❌ | Unaware
  • A
    Angular gyrus (39) — Alexia + Agraphia. Can't read or write (Area 39 of 3-9)
Homunculus Body Map — "HEAD DOWN LEGS UP"
HLM = HEAD LOW, MEDIAL LEGS

The brain's body map is upside-down! Head is lowest, legs go medial.

  • H
    Head — Lower 1/3 of pre/postcentral gyrus (superolateral surface)
  • A
    Arm & trunk — Upper 2/3 of pre/postcentral gyrus
  • L
    Leg, foot, perineum — Paracentral lobule (MEDIAL surface)
  • S
    Size = Function — Size ∝ innervation density, NOT anatomical size
