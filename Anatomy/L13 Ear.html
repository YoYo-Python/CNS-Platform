🗺️ Anatomical Pathway Timeline

1. Auricle (Pinna)
Collects sound waves. Supplied by lesser occipital (C2), great auricular (C2,C3), auriculotemporal (V3), and auricular branch of vagus. Blood supply: anterior & posterior auricular arteries.
2. External Auditory Meatus
Outer 1/3 cartilaginous (hair + wax glands, directed upward); Inner 2/3 bony (thin skin, no glands, directed downward & forward). S-shaped canal ~2.5cm long.
3. Tympanic Membrane
Pars flaccida (between malleolar folds) & pars tensa. Umbo = point of max convexity from malleus handle. Cone of light anteroinferiorly. Lateral surface: skin; Medial: mucous membrane.
4. Middle Ear (Tympanic Cavity)
6 walls. Contains 3 ossicles (malleus→incus→stapes), 2 muscles (tensor tympani, stapedius), chorda tympani, tympanic plexus. Connects to nasopharynx via Eustachian tube.
5. Oval Window → Inner Ear
Stapes footplate transmits vibrations through the oval window into perilymph of bony labyrinth → fluid waves stimulate receptors in membranous labyrinth.
6. Inner Ear Receptors
Organ of Corti (hearing, in cochlear duct), Macula (balance, in utricle & saccule), Crista Ampullaris (rotational balance, in semicircular ducts).

⚡ Key Clinical Signs & Concepts

🔊 Hyperacusis
Painful sensitivity to loud sounds due to stapedius paralysis (facial nerve lesion). Stapes vibrates excessively without dampening.

👶 Pediatric Otitis Media
Short, horizontal Eustachian tube in children allows easy spread of pharyngeal infections to middle ear.

🛡️ Acoustic Reflex
Tensor tympani (V3) + stapedius (VII) contract reflexly to loud sounds, protecting the inner ear by dampening ossicular vibrations.
⚠️ High-YieldExam Favorite
📡 Tympanic Plexus
On promontory. From CN IX (tympanic branch) + sympathetic fibers. Gives off lesser petrosal nerve → otic ganglion → parotid.
Exam FavoriteAnatomy
🧱 Promontory
Bulge on medial wall of middle ear produced by the basal coil of the cochlea. Houses the tympanic plexus on its surface.

🔎 Diagnostic Approaches to Ear Pathology

👁️ Otoscopic Examination Findings
✅ Normal cone of light 🔵 Bulging TM = effusion 🔴 Hemotympanum 🟡 Retracted TM = negative pressure 🟣 Perforated TM

The cone of light is a normal anteroinferior triangular reflection. Its absence or displacement indicates pathology (effusion, perforation, retraction). A red/blue TM may indicate hemotympanum (blood behind TM from skull base fracture).

💊 Management of Common Ear Conditions

🚨 Emergency: Sudden sensorineural hearing loss, complicated otitis media (mastoiditis, intracranial abscess), CSF otorrhea from temporal bone fracture require urgent specialist referral.
🔴 Acute Otitis Media (AOM)
⏱️ Acute Management
Analgesics (paracetamol/ibuprofen) for pain. Antibiotics (amoxicillin first-line) if severe, bilateral, or persisting >48-72h. Consider watchful waiting in mild cases (children >2yo).
🔄 Recurrent AOM
Investigate Eustachian tube dysfunction. Consider adenoidectomy if adenoid hypertrophy. Tympanostomy tubes (grommets) for ventilation if recurrent (≥3 episodes in 6 months).
🛡️ Prevention
Breastfeeding, pneumococcal vaccination, avoid passive smoke exposure, treat allergic rhinitis. Eustachian tube exercises.
🟡 Otitis Media with Effusion (OME / Glue Ear)
👀 Watchful Waiting
Most cases resolve spontaneously within 3 months. Active observation with hearing assessment. No role for antibiotics/decongestants.
🔧 Surgical
Grommet insertion if persistent >3 months with bilateral hearing loss. Adenoidectomy may help in older children.
📣 Autoinflation
Otovent balloon or Valsalva maneuver to open Eustachian tube. Non-invasive first-line approach.
🟢 Hyperacusis (from Facial Nerve Palsy)
🧠 Treat Underlying Cause
Bell's palsy: corticosteroids (prednisolone) within 72h of onset. Protect eye (lubricants, tape). Antiviral if herpes suspected (Ramsay Hunt).
🎵 Symptomatic Management
Sound therapy / desensitization for hyperacusis. Ear protection from loud sounds while stapedius is non-functional. Physiotherapy for facial muscles.
