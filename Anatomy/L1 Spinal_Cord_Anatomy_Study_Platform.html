Developmental Timeline
Spinal cord termination levels across life stages
🤰 Intrauterine Life
The spinal cord completely fills the entire vertebral canal. Cord and vertebral column are equal in length.
👶 At Birth
Cord terminates at L3 vertebra. Differential growth has already begun — bone grows faster than neural tissue.
🧑 Adulthood (lifelong)
Cord terminates at the lower border of L1 (conus medullaris). This level remains constant throughout adult life.

🏥 Clinical Cases

Exam-style clinical vignettes with structured answers

Case 1: CSF Sampling
Procedure
A 35-year-old man presents with severe headache, neck stiffness, and fever for 2 days. The physician suspects meningitis and needs to sample CSF to check for elevated proteins and white cells.
❓ What anatomical space should the physician target, and why is it safe?
The physician should target the lumbar cistern (L2-S2). This is the subarachnoid space below the termination of the spinal cord (L1 in adults). It contains CSF, the cauda equina, and filum terminale interna — but NO solid spinal cord. A needle inserted between L3-L4 or L4-L5 safely avoids the cord.
❓ What layers does the needle pass through?
Skin → Subcutaneous fascia → Supraspinous ligament → Interspinous ligament → Ligamentum flavum → Epidural space → Dura mater → Arachnoid mater → Subarachnoid space (Lumbar cistern).
❓ What would happen if the puncture were performed at T12-L1?
This risks directly piercing the conus medullaris (the terminal end of the spinal cord), potentially causing permanent neurological damage including paralysis and loss of bladder/bowel control.
Case 2: Anterior Spinal Artery Syndrome
Vascular
A 60-year-old woman develops sudden bilateral leg weakness and loss of pain/temperature sensation below the waist after aortic surgery. She retains proprioception and vibration sense.
❓ Which vessel is most likely occluded?
The anterior spinal artery, which is a single artery supplying the anterior 2/3 of the spinal cord. Occlusion causes ischemia of the ventral horns (motor neurons) and the anterolateral white matter (spinothalamic tracts carrying pain/temperature).
❓ Why is proprioception preserved?
The posterior 1/3 of the cord (dorsal columns carrying proprioception and vibration) is supplied by the two posterior spinal arteries, which remain patent in this scenario.
❓ What is the role of radicular arteries in this context?
The lower two-thirds of the cord depend on radicular arteries (from intercostal, lumbar, lateral sacral arteries) for supplementary blood supply. Aortic surgery can compromise these feeder vessels, predisposing to anterior spinal artery syndrome.
Case 3: Lamina II Destruction
Histology
A research lab observes selective destruction of Lamina II neurons in the dorsal horn of the spinal cord in experimental animals. The animals demonstrate altered pain responses.
❓ What nucleus is located in Lamina II?
Substantia Gelatinosa of Rolandi — a major sensory relay station in the dorsal horn that processes pain signals.
❓ Using Rexed's classification, what nuclei are in the adjacent laminae?
Lamina I = Posteromarginal nucleus. Laminae III & IV = Nucleus Proprius. Lamina VII (medial) = Clarke's nucleus.
Case 4: Autonomic Dysfunction
Autonomic
A 28-year-old man sustains a traumatic spinal cord injury at the T6 level. Post-injury, he exhibits loss of sympathetic regulation to the abdominal viscera and blood vessels below the level of injury, leading to hypotension.
❓ Which specific structure in the spinal cord is responsible for sympathetic outflow?
The lateral horn (intermediolateral cell column) in the T1-L2 segments contains the cell bodies of preganglionic sympathetic neurons.
❓ Why would a T6 injury cause loss of sympathetic function below?
The sympathetic neurons below T6 (T7-L2) are disconnected from supraspinal control. While the neurons are intact, they no longer receive descending commands from the brain, resulting in loss of vascular tone, sweating, and visceral regulation.
❓ What about parasympathetic function?
Sacral parasympathetic neurons (S2-S4) are anatomically below the injury but also disconnected from brain control. This can cause bladder and bowel dysfunction.
Case 5: The Long Integrative Case
Integration
Mr. Lewis, 45, is admitted after a car accident with numbness in his left leg and difficulty walking. He reports inability to feel temperature changes in the affected area. MRI reveals a lesion at the L1 vertebral level.
❓ What spinal structures could be involved at L1?
At L1, the conus medullaris (terminal end of cord) is present, along with the beginning of the cauda equina. The lumbar enlargement (L1-S2) contains neurons for the lumbosacral plexus (lower limb innervation).
❓ Trace the normal sensory pathway from leg to cord.
Receptor in leg skin → peripheral process → pseudounipolar cell in DRG → central process (dorsal root) → enters dorsal horn → synapses at Laminae I-IV (e.g., Substantia Gelatinosa for pain/temperature) → ascending tracts → brain.
❓ Trace the normal motor pathway from cord to leg muscles.
Motor commands from brain → descend in white matter tracts → synapse on ventral horn motor neurons (Lamina IX) → axons exit via ventral root → join dorsal root to form spinal nerve → peripheral nerve → muscle.
❓ Why does the MRI show vertebral level L1 but the cord segment affected may be different?
Due to differential growth, spinal cord segments do NOT align perfectly with their corresponding vertebrae. The lumbar enlargement (cord segments L1-S2) is located at vertebral levels T9-T12/L1. The cord terminates at L1 vertebra, so a lesion at L1 vertebra may affect sacral cord segments or the beginning of the cauda equina.

🔬 Spinal Cord Structure

Click any structure for detailed explanation

🧠
Grey Matter (H-shaped)
Internal structure housing neuronal cell bodies
The grey matter forms an H-shape inside the spinal cord. It consists of neuronal cell bodies organized into nuclei. The dorsal horn receives sensory input via the dorsal root, the ventral horn contains motor neuron cell bodies whose axons exit via the ventral root, and the lateral horn (present only in T1-L2 and S2-S4) houses autonomic neurons. The grey commissure connects both sides across the midline.
⬜
White Matter
Peripheral myelinated axon tracts
White matter surrounds the grey matter peripherally and consists of myelinated axon bundles forming ascending tracts (sensory, carrying information to the brain) and descending tracts (motor, carrying commands from the brain). The white myelin sheaths give it its characteristic white appearance.
🔻
Conus Medullaris
Tapered terminal end of cord
The cone-shaped lower end of the spinal cord. In intrauterine life it fills the whole canal, at birth it ends at L3, and in adults it terminates at the lower border of L1. This differential is caused by the vertebral column growing faster than the cord.
🐴
Cauda Equina
Trailing nerve roots below cord termination
Latin for 'horse's tail.' After the cord ends at L1, the lower lumbar and sacral nerve roots must travel downward through the lumbar cistern to reach their respective intervertebral foramina. These trailing roots form the cauda equina, bathed in CSF.
🧵
Filum Terminale
Pia mater extension anchoring the cord
Filum terminale interna: runs from conus medullaris through the lumbar cistern to S2 (within CSF). Filum terminale externa: continues from S2 to the back of the coccyx (outside the dural sac). Both are pia mater extensions providing longitudinal stabilization.
⚓
Denticulate Ligaments
21 lateral processes stabilizing the cord
Formed by the pia mater. They project laterally as 21 tooth-like processes on each side. Each process pierces the arachnoid mater to attach firmly to the inner surface of the dura mater. They provide crucial lateral suspension of the spinal cord within the dural sac.
🛡️
Spinal Meninges
Three protective layers around the cord
From outer to inner: Dura mater (tough, terminates S2), Arachnoid mater (delicate, terminates S2), Pia mater (adherent, continues as filum terminale). The epidural space (dura-bone) contains venous plexus. The subarachnoid space (arachnoid-pia) contains CSF.
💪
Cervical Enlargement
C5-T1 for brachial plexus
A widening of the spinal cord in the lower cervical region (C5-T1). It houses the increased number of neuronal cell bodies needed to form the brachial plexus, which provides all motor and sensory innervation to the upper limbs.
🦵
Lumbar Enlargement
L1-S2 for lumbosacral plexus
A widening in the lower thoracic/upper lumbar cord segments (L1-S2). Contains the neurons for the lumbosacral plexus, innervating the lower limbs. Note: these cord segments are located at vertebral levels T9-T12/L1 due to differential growth.
🔴
Anterior Spinal Artery
Single artery, anterior 2/3 of cord
A single artery arising from the vertebral artery. Runs along the anterior median fissure for the full length of the cord. Supplies the anterior 2/3 including the ventral horns (motor neurons) and the anterolateral tracts. Occlusion causes anterior spinal artery syndrome.
🔵
Posterior Spinal Arteries
Two arteries, posterior 1/3 of cord
Two arteries arising from the vertebral artery or PICA. Run posterolaterally. Supply the posterior 1/3 of the cord including the dorsal horns and dorsal columns (proprioception, vibration).
🌿
Radicular Arteries
Regional feeder arteries to the cord
Enter through intervertebral foramina to supplement the spinal arteries, particularly critical for the lower 2/3 of the cord. Sources vary by region: ascending cervical (neck), intercostal (thorax), lumbar (abdomen), lateral sacral (pelvis).

💊 Clinical Applications

Clinically relevant anatomy organized by urgency and importance

🚨 Critical Knowledge (Exam & Clinical)

⚠️ Lumbar Puncture Safety Zone
  • NEVER insert above L1 in adults (risk of cord damage)
  • Safe zone: L3-L4 or L4-L5 interspace
  • In neonates: cord ends at L3 → adjust accordingly
  • Access the lumbar cistern (L2-S2)
🚫 Anterior Spinal Artery Syndrome
  • Occlusion → ischemia of anterior 2/3 of cord
  • Loss of motor function (ventral horns)
  • Loss of pain/temperature (spinothalamic tracts)
  • Preserved proprioception (dorsal columns intact)

⚡ Important Anatomical Applications

📌 Epidural Anesthesia
  • Injected into the epidural space (between bone & dura)
  • Contains internal vertebral venous plexus
  • Drug bathes nerve roots as they exit
  • Common in labor/delivery & surgery
🔗 Tethered Cord Syndrome
  • Filum terminale may be abnormally thick/tight
  • Restricts upward migration of cord during growth
  • Cord ends lower than L1 → neurological deficits
  • Treatment: surgical release of filum terminale
🐴 Cauda Equina Syndrome
  • Compression of nerve roots in lumbar cistern
  • LMN signs (flaccid paralysis, areflexia)
  • Bladder/bowel dysfunction (S2-S4 parasympathetic)
  • Surgical emergency

📗 Functional Anatomy Applications

🔴 Sympathetic Outflow (T1-L2)
  • Lateral horn neurons ONLY in thoracolumbar segments
  • Controls fight-or-flight responses
  • Damage → loss of vascular tone, sweating
🔵 Parasympathetic Outflow (S2-S4)
  • Lateral horn neurons in sacral segments
  • Controls bladder, bowel, sexual function
  • Damage → urinary retention, impotence
💪 Enlargements & Plexuses
  • Cervical enlargement (C5-T1) → Brachial plexus → Upper limbs
  • Lumbar enlargement (L1-S2) → Lumbosacral plexus → Lower limbs
  • More neurons = more grey matter = enlargement

🗣️ Viva Practice Questions

12 oral exam questions — click to reveal model answers

V1. What is the defining difference in the level of termination of the spinal cord between a newborn and an adult?
At birth, the cord ends at the level of the L3 vertebra. In an adult, due to the faster growth of the vertebral column, it ends at the lower border of L1.
V2. Describe the origin and attachments of the filum terminale.
It is an extension of the pia mater originating from the conus medullaris. It extends downward to anchor the spinal cord to the back of the coccyx. The interna portion runs through CSF to S2; the externa continues to the coccyx.
V3. What is the clinical significance of the lumbar cistern?
It is an enlarged subarachnoid space containing CSF and nerve roots but NO solid spinal cord. This makes it the safe and ideal location for performing a lumbar puncture.
V4. Where does the spinal cord officially begin?
It begins as a direct continuation of the medulla oblongata immediately below the foramen magnum.
V5. Why does the spinal cord feature cervical and lumbar enlargements?
These segments house an increased number of neuronal cell bodies necessary to give origin to the massive brachial plexus (C5-T1) and lumbosacral plexus (L1-S2) that supply the upper and lower limbs.
V6. What functional type of neurons are located in the ventral horn?
The ventral horn exclusively contains the cell bodies of motor neurons (LMNs), whose axons exit via the ventral roots to stimulate effector muscles.
V7. Identify the location and contents of the epidural space.
Located between the dura mater and the surrounding vertebral bone. Contains the internal vertebral venous plexus and fat.
V8. Which specific nucleus is located in Lamina II of Rexed?
Lamina II contains the Substantia Gelatinosa of Rolandi, a major sensory nucleus involved in pain processing.
V9. Name the sources of the radicular arteries supplying the cord in the abdomen and pelvis.
In the abdomen: lumbar arteries. In the pelvis: lateral sacral arteries.
V10. Structurally, what forms a spinal nerve?
A spinal nerve is formed by the union of a ventral (motor) root and a dorsal (sensory) root at the intervertebral foramen.
V11. What is the cauda equina?
The collection of lumbar and sacral nerve roots that trail downwards through the lumbar cistern after the spinal cord terminates at L1.
V12. How many processes do the denticulate ligaments have, and what is their function?
They have 21 processes on each side. They pierce the arachnoid to attach to the dura, providing lateral physical support to the spinal cord.
