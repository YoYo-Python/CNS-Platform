Overview
Quick-reference summary of CN VII & CN VIII
🧠
CN VII — Facial Nerve
Contains 4 nuclei: Motor nucleus, Superior salivary nucleus, Nucleus solitarius, Spinal trigeminal nucleus. Supplies muscles of facial expression, parasympathetic to glands, taste from anterior ⅔ of tongue, and somatic sensation from external ear.
⚡ High-Yield 📝 Exam Favorite
👂
CN VIII — Vestibulocochlear Nerve
Two components: Vestibular (balance, from Scarpa's ganglion) and Cochlear (hearing, from Spiral ganglion). Both enter the brainstem at the pontomedullary junction via internal auditory meatus.
⚡ High-Yield
😐
LMN Facial Palsy (Bell's)
Lesion at nucleus or nerve → entire ipsilateral face paralyzed. Cannot close eye, raise eyebrow, whistle, or smile on that side. Forehead wrinkles lost.
📝 Exam Favorite 🩺 Clinical Pearl
🔝
UMN Facial Palsy
Lesion in cortex or corticobulbar tract → contralateral lower face paralyzed, forehead & eye closure spared. The upper face nucleus receives bilateral cortical fibers.
📝 Exam Favorite ⚡ High-Yield
⚠️
Facial Colliculus — Exam Trap!
The facial colliculus is NOT the facial nerve nucleus. It is the abducent nerve (CN VI) nucleus surrounded by the internal genu (loop) of facial nerve fibers. A classic viva pitfall!
📝 Exam Favorite
🗺️
Facial Nerve Course
Brainstem → Internal auditory meatus → Facial canal (1st genu + geniculate ganglion, 2nd genu) → Stylomastoid foramen → Parotid gland → 5 terminal branches: Temporal, Zygomatic, Buccal, Mandibular, Cervical.
🔬 Anatomy
⚠️ HIGH-YIELD EXAM POINT
UMN vs LMN facial palsy distinction is one of the most frequently tested clinical topics. Remember: LMN = entire ipsilateral face (including forehead and eye), UMN = contralateral lower face only (forehead spared due to bilateral cortical supply to upper facial nucleus).
🩺
Key Clinical Signs
Essential signs to recognize in exam scenarios
🔊 Hyperacusis
Hypersensitivity to loud sounds → paralysis of stapedius muscle (nerve to stapedius, CN VII in facial canal)
🩺 Clinical Pearl
🍔 Food Accumulation
Food collects in vestibule of mouth → buccinator paralysis (buccal branch, CN VII)
📝 Exam
👁️ Cannot Close Eye
Orbicularis oculi paralysis → eye remains open → risk of corneal drying (LMN palsy)
⚡ High-Yield
👅 Loss of Taste
Anterior ⅔ of tongue → chorda tympani damaged within facial canal
📝 Exam
💧 Dry Eye
Loss of lacrimation → lesion proximal to greater superficial petrosal nerve
🩺 Clinical Pearl
🎵 Can't Whistle
Orbicularis oris paralysis → inability to pucker lips for whistling or blowing
📝 Exam
🌀 Vertigo
Dizziness, nystagmus, loss of balance → vestibular nerve lesion (CN VIII)
🩺 Clinical Pearl
🔇 Ipsilateral Deafness
Complete unilateral deafness → cochlear nerve or cochlea lesion (before bilateral crossover)

Topographic Diagnosis
Localize the lesion using clinical features
⚠️ KEY DIAGNOSTIC PRINCIPLE
Identifying which specific branches are affected (greater petrosal, nerve to stapedius, chorda tympani) allows you to pinpoint the exact level of the lesion along the facial nerve's course. This is called topographical diagnosis.

📐 Diagnostic Algorithm: Where is the Facial Nerve Lesion?

1
Is there facial muscle paralysis?
If the entire ipsilateral face is paralyzed (including forehead) → LMN lesion confirmed. If only contralateral lower face → UMN lesion.
2
Is lacrimation affected (dry eye)?
Yes → Lesion is proximal to the greater superficial petrosal nerve (near the geniculate ganglion or more proximal). This is the most proximal intra-canal branch.
3
Is there hyperacusis?
Yes → Lesion is proximal to the nerve to stapedius within the facial canal. Stapedius is paralyzed → sounds are abnormally loud.
4
Is taste lost on the anterior ⅔ of tongue?
Yes → Lesion is proximal to the chorda tympani branch. The chorda tympani branches off near the 2nd genu of the facial canal.
5
Is there ONLY facial muscle paralysis (no taste loss, no hyperacusis, no dry eye)?
Yes → Lesion is at or distal to the stylomastoid foramen. All intracranial branches were already given off.
6
Is there combined facial nerve AND hearing loss/vertigo?
Yes → Consider a lesion at the cerebellopontine angle (e.g., vestibular schwannoma / acoustic neuroma) affecting both CN VII and CN VIII where they travel together.

Management & Consequences
Clinical management of facial & vestibulocochlear lesions
ℹ️ Source Note
Treatment details are limited in the provided source material. The content below covers clinically relevant consequences and symptomatic management based on the anatomical principles taught.
🚨 URGENT — Eye Protection (LMN Palsy)
Preventing Corneal Damage
  • The eye cannot close → risk of corneal drying and ulceration
  • Artificial tears / eye lubricants
  • Eye patching at night
  • Moisture chamber glasses
  • Taping the eyelid shut during sleep
📋 Consequences of Motor Loss
Functional Deficits from Facial Paralysis
  • Orbicularis oculi: Can't close eye → dry eye
  • Orbicularis oris: Can't whistle or blow
  • Buccinator: Food accumulates in vestibule
  • Frontalis: Loss of forehead wrinkles
  • Nasolabial fold: Flattened on affected side
  • Mouth angle: Droops on affected side
🔊 Symptomatic Management
Managing Hyperacusis & Taste Loss
  • Hyperacusis: Ear protection in loud environments
  • Taste loss: Usually recovers with nerve regeneration
  • Dry eye: Lubricant eye drops essential
  • Salivation issues: Manage with oral care
🌀 Vestibular Lesion Management
Managing Vertigo & Balance Issues
  • Fall prevention and safety measures
  • Vestibular rehabilitation exercises
  • Anti-emetics for nausea
  • Identify and treat underlying cause
🔇 Hearing Loss Management
Cochlear Nerve Lesion Consequences
  • Peripheral lesion → complete ipsilateral deafness
  • Cortical lesion → bilateral diminution (not total deafness)
  • Hearing aids for partial loss
  • Audiometric testing for diagnosis
⚡ Key Anatomical Principle
Topographical Diagnosis Guides Management
  • Proximal lesion = more symptoms to manage
  • Distal (stylomastoid) lesion = motor loss only
  • UMN lesion: eye safe → focus on lower face rehabilitation
  • LMN lesion: eye at risk → urgent eye protection needed
