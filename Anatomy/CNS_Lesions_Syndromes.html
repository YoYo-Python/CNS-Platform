05

Corpus Callosum Lesion

Callosal Apraxia / Split-Brain

The corpus callosum is important for the interhemispheric transfer of information (such as memory and language) between the dominant left and the nondominant (right) hemisphere.

Signs
  • Severing the connection between the two cerebral hemispheres leads to a complete division of their functions — known as split-brain.
  • Memory and language functions become inaccessible to the non-dominant hemisphere, leading to disorders on the left side.
  • The patient cannot execute verbal commands with their left hand — e.g., being asked to salute with it. (Callosal apraxia)

Basal Ganglia Diseases

1 — Parkinsonism
Parkinsonism
Pathology: Degeneration of dopamine-producing cells in substantia nigra → depletion of dopamine in striatum.
Tremor
Resting tremors (pill rolling).
Tone
Rigidity.
Movement
Bradykinesia — slowness of movement.
Absent
No paralysis, sensory loss, ataxia.
2 — Chorea
Chorea

A neurological disorder characterized by jerky involuntary movements affecting especially the shoulders, hips, and face.

Hemiballismus
Cause: Damage to the sub-thalamic nucleus or its connection with lentiform nucleus, often by a stroke.
Laterality
Contralateral side of the body.
Character
Flailing movements of one arm and leg.

Syndromes of Medulla

Medial Medullary Syndrome
Cause: Occlusion of anterior spinal artery.
Clinical Signs

HYPOGLOSSAL NERVE & NUCLEUS

  • LMN paralysis of the same side of the tongue (hemiparalysis).
  • Deviation of the protruded tongue toward the affected side.

MEDIAL LEMNISCUS

  • Contralateral loss of kinaesthesia, discriminative touch and sense of vibration.

CORTICOSPINAL TRACT

  • Contralateral hemiplegia.
Lateral Medullary Syndrome (Wallenberg / PICA)
Cause: Occlusion of the PICA.
Clinical Signs
  • 1. Spinal trigeminal nucleus: Loss of pain & temperature from the same side of face.
  • 2. Lateral spinothalamic tract: Loss of pain & temperature from the opposite side of body.
  • 3. Nucleus ambiguus:
    Ipsilateral PARALYSIS of Muscles
    • Larynx → hoarseness of voice.
    • Pharynx → dysphagia & loss of pharyngeal gag reflex.
    • Palate → nasal regurgitation of food & deviation of uvula to normal side.
  • 4. Inferior cerebellar peduncle: Ataxia.
  • 5. Vestibular nuclei: Vertigo, nausea, vomiting and nystagmus.
  • 6. Descending sympathetic fibres: Ipsilateral Horner's syndrome.

Syndromes of Pons

Caudal Basal Pontine Syndrome
Cause: Occlusion of paramedian branches of basilar artery.
Affected Structures
  • Corticospinal tract
  • Facial nerve (CN VII)
  • Abducent nerve (CN VI)
Signs
  • Contralateral hemiplegia.
  • LMN Ipsilateral facial nerve palsy.
  • Ipsilateral paralysis of the lateral rectus → internal strabismus.
Rostral Basal Pontine Syndrome
Cause: Occlusion of short circumferential artery.
Affected Structures
  • Corticospinal tract
  • Trigeminal nerve (CN V)
Signs
  • Contralateral UMNL hemiplegia.
  • Ipsilateral paralysis of muscles of mastication.
  • Ipsilateral loss of sensation from the face.

Syndromes of Midbrain

Weber's Syndrome
Cause: Occlusion of a branch of posterior cerebral artery.
Affected Structures
  • Oculomotor nerve (CN III)
  • Crus cerebri (pyramidal tract)
Signs
  • LMNL of CN III on the same side of the lesion.
  • UMNL Hemiplegia on the opposite side of the body.
Benedikt Syndrome
Cause: Lesion in tegmentum of the midbrain — occlusion of posterior cerebral artery.
Affected Structures
  • Oculomotor nucleus or its emerging fibres
  • Red nucleus
Signs
  • LMNL of CN III on the same side of the lesion.
  • Extrapyramidal manifestations — rigidity and tremors on the opposite side.
Parinaud Syndrome — Dorsal Midbrain
Cause: Posterior commissure compression — tumor (pineal gland), hemorrhage, or infection.
Sign
Paralysis of upward gaze.

CN III / IV / VI — Oculomotor, Trochlear, Abducent

Oculomotor — CN III
Ptosis
Dropping of the upper eyelid due to paralysis of levator palpebrae.
Mydriasis
Due to paralysis of constrictor pupillae muscle.
Eye deviation
Deviation of the eye downward & laterally — by unopposed action of lateral rectus & superior oblique.
Trochlear — CN IV
In the nucleus
Paralysis of contralateral superior oblique.
Nerve (outside brainstem)
Paralysis of ipsilateral muscle.
Sign 1
Inability to depress the adducted eye.
Sign 2
Diplopia in looking downward — causing difficulty in walking downstairs.
Abducent — CN VI
Lesion
Paralysis of the lateral rectus muscle → internal strabismus (squint) by unopposed medial rectus muscle.

CN VII — Facial Nerve Lesions

Upper Motor Neuron Lesion
Cause: Lesion in corticobulbar tract above the level of facial nucleus, or lesion in the motor cortex.
Result

Paralysis of facial muscles of the lower ½ of the opposite side of the face.

Signs of Lesion
  • 1 Paralysis of buccinator muscle — accumulation of food in vestibule of mouth & dribbling of saliva.
  • 2 Paralysis of levators of mouth angle — dropping of the angle of the mouth & loss of nasolabial fold.
  • 3 Paralysis of orbicularis oris — patient can't whistle or blow.
Lower Motor Neuron Lesion
Cause: Lesion of the facial nerve or in the facial nucleus — may be located at any level.

Levels:

  • 1. Facial colliculus: accompanied by abducent nucleus lesion.
  • 2. Cerebellopontine angle: accompanied by CN VIII lesion.
  • 3. Facial canal: may affect greater superficial petrosal nerve, chorda tympani & nerve to stapedius.
  • 4. Stylomastoid foramen: ipsilateral paralysis of facial muscles only — without loss of lacrimation, salivation or taste.
Result

Paralysis of all muscles of facial expression on the same side of the face.

Signs
  • 1 Paralysis of orbicularis oculi — patient cannot close eye → remains open → dryness of the eye.
  • 2 Paralysis of frontal belly of occipitofrontalis — inability to raise the eyebrow & loss of wrinkles of the forehead.
  • 3 Paralysis of buccinator muscle — accumulation of food in the vestibule of mouth & dribbling of saliva.
  • 4 Paralysis of levators of mouth angle — dropping of the angle of the mouth & loss of nasolabial fold.
  • 5 Paralysis of orbicularis oris — patient can't whistle or blow.
  • 6 Paralysis of stapedius — Hyperacusis (hypersensitivity to sounds; can't tolerate loud sounds).
  • 7 Affection of chorda tympani — loss of taste of the anterior 2/3 of the tongue & decreased salivation.
  • 8 Affection of great superficial petrosal — loss of lacrimation.

CN XI — Accessory Nerve Lesion

Paralysis of Sternomastoid
Causes inability to rotate the face to the opposite side. Tested clinically by turning the face to the opposite side.
Paralysis of Trapezius
Causes inability to elevate the shoulder. Tested clinically by elevating shoulder against resistance.
Irritation of CN XI
May produce Torticollis.

CN XII — Hypoglossal Nerve Lesion

Clinical test: Ask the patient to protrude the tongue outwards.
Type of LesionTongue DeviationWasting?
LMNL Deviated to the same side — by the effect of the unopposed contralateral genioglossus muscle. Yes — wasting of the tongue.
UMNL of CN XII Deviated to the contralateral side. No wasting.
Bilateral CN XII lesion Tongue lies motionless. Slow articulation & difficult swallowing.
