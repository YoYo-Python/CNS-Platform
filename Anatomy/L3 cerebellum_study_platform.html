📋 Topic Overview

The Cerebellum is a hindbrain structure in the posterior cranial fossa responsible for equilibrium, muscle tone & coordination, and planning of movement. The Fourth Ventricle is a diamond-shaped CSF-filled cavity lying between the cerebellum (posteriorly) and the pons/upper medulla (anteriorly).

⚡ Key Facts Grid
🔷

4th Ventricle Shape

Diamond-shaped (rhomboid fossa). Floor = anterior wall, Roof = posterior wall. Divided by stria medullaris into upper pontine + lower medullary parts.

High-Yield
🚨

Facial Colliculus Trap

The facial colliculus is formed by facial nerve fibers wrapping around the abducent nucleus. It does NOT contain the facial nucleus — classic exam trick!

⚠️ EXAM FAV
💧

CSF Exits the 4th Ventricle Via

• Foramen of Magendie (median) → subarachnoid space
• Foramina of Luschka (×2 lateral) → subarachnoid space
• Central canal → spinal cord

High-Yield
🩸

PICA = 4th Ventricle Artery

Posterior Inferior Cerebellar Artery (PICA) — branch of vertebral artery — supplies choroid plexus of 4th ventricle + posterior inferior cerebellum.

Clinical Pearl
🎭

Intention vs Resting Tremor

Cerebellar lesions → intention tremors (appear during movement, absent at rest). Parkinson's → resting tremors (opposite).

⚠️ EXAM
🎯

Dysmetria

Inability to judge distance/stop movement at the right point. Tested by finger-to-nose test. Sign of cerebellar (especially neocerebellum) damage.

Clinical Sign
🏗️

Cerebellar Peduncles

Superior → midbrain (mainly efferent)
Middle → pons (mainly afferent)
Inferior → medulla (mixed)

High-Yield
⚖️

Tentorium Cerebelli

Fold of dura mater that separates the cerebellum from the cerebrum above. The cerebellum sits in the posterior cranial fossa below it.

🕒 Timeline: Cerebellum Anatomy Progression

1️⃣ 4th Ventricle Boundaries

Anterior = rhomboid fossa (floor). Posterior = roof (superior + inferior medullary velum + cerebellum). Lateral borders = cerebellar peduncles (SCP superiorly, ICP+gracile+cuneate tubercles inferiorly).

2️⃣ Floor Landmarks

Stria medullaris divides floor into upper pontine part (contains facial colliculus, locus coeruleus, superior fovea) and lower medullary part (hypoglossal, vagal, vestibular trigones).

3️⃣ Cerebellar Lobes

Primary fissure separates anterior from posterior lobe. Posterolateral fissure separates posterior lobe from flocculonodular lobe.

4️⃣ Functional Connections

Archi → vestibular nuclei (equilibrium). Paleo → spinal cord (tone). Neo → cerebrum (planning). All mediated via respective deep cerebellar nuclei.

5️⃣ Vascular Supply

PICA (vertebral artery branch) → inferior cerebellum + 4th ventricle choroid plexus. SCA and AICA from basilar artery → superior/anterior cerebellum.

🔎 Diagnostic Approach to Cerebellar Lesions
🧭 Diagnostic Algorithm
1

History & Neurological Exam

Look for: onset, associated symptoms (vertigo, diplopia, headache), risk factors. Test: finger-to-nose, heel-to-shin, rapid alternating movements, gait assessment.

2

Identify the Type of Cerebellar Sign

Dysmetria → neocerebellum. Muscle tone/coordination loss → paleocerebellum. Ataxia/balance loss → archicerebellum/flocculonodular lobe.

3

Tremorr Differentiation

Intention tremor (cerebellar) — appears during goal-directed movement, absent at rest.
Resting tremor (Parkinson's) — appears at rest, suppressed by movement.

4

Localise the Lesion

4th ventricle floor lesion → CN palsy (Abducent from facial colliculus, Hypoglossal, Vagal). Aqueduct blockage → hydrocephalus of 3rd + lateral ventricles only (4th intact).

5

Vascular Considerations

PICA occlusion → posterior inferior cerebellar infarct + 4th ventricle choroid plexus ischemia. SCA occlusion → superior cerebellar infarct.

💊 Clinical Management of Cerebellar & 4th Ventricle Pathologies
🚨 URGENT Hydrocephalus (Aqueductal Blockage)

🧠 Identify the Block

Aqueduct of Sylvius obstruction → dilation of 3rd + lateral ventricles only. 4th ventricle remains normal. This pattern is pathognomonic.

⚡ Raised ICP Management

Head elevation, osmotic therapy (mannitol), and urgent neurosurgical referral. Ventriculoperitoneal (VP) shunt may be required.

🔍 Investigate Cause

MRI brain for aqueductal stenosis, compressing tumor, or inflammatory cause. Tumor compression (e.g., pineal region) must be excluded.

⚠️ IMPORTANT PICA Stroke (Posterior Inferior Cerebellar Artery)

🩺 Clinical Features

Cerebellar dysfunction (ipsilateral), impaired CSF secretion (choroid plexus ischemia), and possible Wallenberg syndrome if lateral medulla involved.

💊 Acute Management

IV tPA within 4.5 hours if ischemic. Aspirin/anticoagulation depending on etiology. Neurology intensive care for monitoring.

🔄 Rehabilitation

Vestibular rehabilitation for balance. Physiotherapy for coordination retraining. Occupational therapy for daily function.

✅ STANDARD Flocculonodular Lobe Tumor (Archicerebellum)

🔬 Presentation

Truncal ataxia and equilibrium loss — disruption of vestibular connections. Common in medulloblastoma (children). MRI is key imaging.

⚕️ Surgical Resection

Surgical excision is first-line. Posterior fossa craniotomy. Care to preserve peduncles and brainstem structures during surgery.

☢️ Adjuvant Therapy

Craniospinal radiation ± chemotherapy for malignant tumors (e.g., medulloblastoma). Follow-up MRI for recurrence monitoring.

🩺 Symptomatic Management of Cerebellar Signs

🎯 Intention Tremor

Clonazepam, propranolol, or primidone may reduce tremor. Deep brain stimulation (DBS) of thalamus in refractory cases. Weighted utensils for daily living.

🚶 Ataxia / Dysmetria

Physiotherapy-guided coordination exercises. Balance training and vestibular exercises for equilibrium deficits. Occupational therapy for independence.

👁️ CN VI Palsy (Abducent)

From facial colliculus lesion. Prism glasses for diplopia. Patching for monocular use. Botulinum toxin injection if persistent. Surgical correction if stable.
