📌 What is Meningitis?
🦠

Acute Purulent (Bacterial)

Acute inflammatory infection with marked purulent exudates. Most dangerous type. Caused by bacteria like N. meningitidis & S. pneumoniae.

High-YieldExam Favorite
🐢

Chronic Meningitis

Caused by slow-growing agents (Mycobacteria, Cryptococcus). Produces granulomatous inflammation rather than acute purulence. TB & fungi typical.

Granulomatous
🦟

Aseptic (Viral)

Most common type overall. Non-purulent, predominantly lymphocytic. Echovirus & Coxsackievirus are leading causes. Usually self-limiting.

⚡ High-Yield Clinical Signs
🌡️

High Fever

Sudden onset >39°C. Endotoxin-mediated cytokine release (IL-1, TNF).

Classic Triad
🤕

Nuchal Rigidity

Stiff neck — meningeal irritation from inflammation. Kernig & Brudzinski signs positive.

Classic TriadExam Favorite
😵

Photophobia

Sensitivity to light. Part of meningeal irritation syndrome. Classic triad component.

Classic Triad
🔴

Petechiae / Ecchymosis

PATHOGNOMONIC for meningococcemia. LOS endotoxin → vascular damage → intravascular clotting.

⚠️ Danger SignPearl
💉

Low BP + High HR

Septic shock from meningococcemia. Cytokine storm causes vascular collapse.

Emergency
🤢

Vomiting / Lethargy

Due to raised intracranial pressure and meningeal inflammation.

🗺️ Disease Progression Timeline

🫁 Inhalation of respiratory droplets

Colonization of nasopharynx — most carriers never develop disease

🔗 Pili-mediated adhesion to mucosa

IgA protease cleaves mucosal antibodies → immune evasion

🩸 Bacteremia develops

Endotoxin (LOS) released → fever, cytokine storm begins

💥 Meningococcemia (severe)

Vascular damage, petechiae, shock — can kill in <24h

🧠 BBB penetration → Meningitis

Purulent CSF, PMN influx, glucose depletion, raised ICP

⚕️ Without treatment → death

Immediate empirical antibiotics are life-saving

🧬 CSF Glucose — Why Is It Low?
Normal CSF glucose = ~60-70% of serum glucose
↓
Bacteria directly consume glucose (metabolic activity)
↓
Massive PMN influx → PMNs perform rapid glycolysis
↓
Severe vascular inflammation shifts glucose to blood
↓
CSF glucose <0.3 serum ratio = ⚠️ Bacterial meningitis

🧫 Lab Diagnosis of N. meningitidis — Step by Step
1️⃣ Lumbar puncture — 3-10 ml CSF under strict aseptic conditions
↓
2️⃣ Physical exam: turbid fluid under tension
↓
3️⃣ Direct Gram stain: Gram-neg kidney-shaped diplococci (intra + extracellular)
↓
4️⃣ Culture: Chocolate agar (CSF) or Thayer-Martin (swab), aerobic, 37°C, 10% CO₂
↓
5️⃣ Biochemistry: oxidase positive, ferments glucose + maltose (acid only)
↓
6️⃣ Definitive ID: agglutination with specific antisera
↓
7️⃣ Rapid alternatives: Antigen detection (latex agglutination), PCR (especially post-antibiotics)

⚠️ In meningococcemia — EVERY MINUTE COUNTS. Give antibiotics before LP if patient is unstable!

🚨 Acute Treatment Emergency

  • Drug of choice (confirmed N. meningitidis): Penicillin G IV
  • Why Penicillin works: Strong anti-meningococcal activity + good CSF penetration when meninges are INFLAMED
  • Empirical (before culture): 3rd-gen cephalosporins (e.g., Cefotaxime, Ceftriaxone) — broad coverage
  • If penicillin allergic: Chloramphenicol (historical use)
  • H. influenzae: Ampicillin or 3rd-gen cephalosporin

💊 Chemoprophylaxis Contacts

  • Drugs used: Rifampicin or Ciprofloxacin
  • Why NOT Penicillin: Poor penetration of UNINFLAMED CSF and mucosa → cannot eradicate carrier state
  • Goal: Eradicate nasopharyngeal carriage in close contacts
  • Who gets it: Household contacts, dormitory roommates, close contacts within 7 days

💉 Vaccination Prevention

  • Quadrivalent vaccine: Covers serogroups A, C, W-135, Y
  • Target: Polysaccharide capsule antigens
  • High-risk groups: Military, Hajj pilgrims, asplenic patients
  • Limitation: DOES NOT cover serogroup B
  • Group B problem: Capsule mimics human brain sialic acid → poorly immunogenic + autoimmune risk
  • Vaccine is ineffective in infants: Pure polysaccharide vaccines are T-independent

⚠️ Sulfonamides — Historical Note
🚫

Sulfonamides — Now Obsolete

Previously used for N. meningitidis treatment and prophylaxis. Now rendered ineffective due to widespread resistance development. No longer used clinically for this purpose.
