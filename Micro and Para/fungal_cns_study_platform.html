⚡ Exam Pearls

🌡️
Temperature trick: Cryptococcus grows at 37°C. Pigeons (40-42°C body temperature) are immune carriers — NOT infected — because the high temperature prevents fungal replication inside them.
🔒
"Crypto" = Hidden: The word means "hidden" — the polysaccharide capsule hides it from the immune system. Macrophages cannot destroy it without CD4+ T-cell help → explains AIDS susceptibility.
⚙️
DKA + Free Iron: Acidosis in DKA reduces iron binding to transport proteins (transferrin). Free iron is essential for Mucor spores to convert to invasive filamentous hyphae — this is the CRITICAL mechanistic link.
🔬
India Ink Mechanism: The ink CANNOT penetrate the thick capsule → appears as a clear "halo" ring around the dark background. It's a negative stain, not a true stain.
📊
CSF Profile: High protein + Low glucose (fungus consumes it) + 90% lymphocytes. In severely immunocompromised patients, inflammatory response may be blunted.
🧫
SDA Culture Rule: Both fungi are cultured on SDA WITHOUT cycloheximide (cycloheximide inhibits their growth). Cryptococcus = creamy white. Mucor = dirty snow gray-black.
🫁
Primary Pulmonary Cryptococcosis: Often ASYMPTOMATIC in immunocompetent hosts — walled off as granuloma. Reactivates when immunity fails (e.g. HIV).

🟣 Cryptococcus — Pathogenesis

Step 1 · Environment
🐦 Inhalation of pigeon excreta dust
Cryptococcus neoformans lives in dry pigeon droppings. Spores become airborne and are inhaled. Pigeons themselves are unaffected because their body temperature (40–42°C) exceeds the fungus's upper growth limit of ~37°C.
↓
Step 2 · Primary Lung
🫁 Asymptomatic pulmonary granuloma
In immunocompetent hosts, alveolar macrophages phagocytose spores and form granulomas, walling off the infection. No systemic disease develops. The fungus can remain dormant for years.
↓
Step 3 · Immune Evasion
🛡️ Capsule blocks macrophage phagocytosis
The thick polysaccharide capsule is the primary virulence factor. It physically prevents opsonisation and phagocytosis. CD4+ T-cells are ESSENTIAL to activate macrophages to destroy the yeast. When CD4 count falls (HIV/AIDS), this protection collapses.
↓
Step 4 · Dissemination
🩸 Haematogenous spread to the CNS
The yeast crosses the blood-brain barrier by "Trojan horse" transport inside macrophages, or directly through the endothelium. The CNS microenvironment is particularly supportive of Cryptococcal growth.
↓
Step 5 · CNS Disease
🧠 Subacute meningoencephalitis
Causes progressive inflammation of the meninges and brain parenchyma → headache, fever, confusion, stiff neck, photophobia. Rising intracranial pressure (from impaired CSF drainage) can cause coma and death if untreated.

🟠 Mucormycosis — Pathogenesis

Step 1 · Trigger (DKA)
⚡ DKA creates free iron pool
In diabetic ketoacidosis, metabolic acidosis reduces the binding capacity of iron transport proteins (transferrin). This releases "free iron" into serum. Mucor species require abundant free iron to germinate from spore to hyphal form — DKA essentially sets the table for them.
↓
Step 2 · Inhalation
👃 Spores inhaled into nasal passages
Mucor spores are ubiquitous in the environment (soil, decaying organic matter). In immunocompetent hosts, they are rapidly cleared. In DKA or neutropenic patients, they germinate in the nasal mucosa/sinuses.
↓
Step 3 · Germination
🌿 Spores → invasive hyphae (free iron)
Free iron drives the conversion from dormant spore to invasive filamentous hyphae. This is the critical, exam-tested step. Without free iron (or with iron chelation therapy), this conversion is impaired.
↓
Step 4 · Angioinvasion
🩸 Hyphae invade blood vessel walls
Mucor hyphae are uniquely angiotrophic — they specifically invade blood vessel walls. This is the defining virulence mechanism. Hyphae penetrate the endothelium and media of vessels, causing luminal occlusion.
↓
Step 5 · Necrosis
💀 Thrombosis → Ischemic necrosis
Vascular invasion causes thrombosis → ischemia → massive tissue infarction. Clinically: black necrotic tissue in the nasal cavity, orbit, and eventually the brain. The classic presentation is facial pain, periorbital swelling, and black eschar in a DKA patient.

🔬 Diagnosis

Tests, stains, cultures, and diagnostic algorithms

🟣 Diagnosing Cryptococcosis

1

India Ink Preparation (CSF)

Capsule blocks ink particles → clear "halo" around budding yeast on dark background. Pathognomonic. Rapid bedside test.

2

Latex Agglutination (CSF/Serum)

Rapid antigen detection test. Detects cryptococcal polysaccharide antigen. Most sensitive and specific diagnostic test.

3

CSF Analysis

↑ Protein · ↓ Glucose (fungus consumes it) · Lymphocytic pleocytosis (90% lymphocytes) · ↑ Opening pressure

4

Culture on SDA

Without cycloheximide. Creamy white, mucoid colonies in 1–2 days at 37°C. Urease positive on Christensen's urea agar.

🟠 Diagnosing Mucormycosis

1

10% KOH Mount

Applied to nasal discharge, sputum, or tissue. KOH dissolves host cellular material, revealing broad (>10µm), non-septate, ribbon-like hyphae with right-angle branching.

2

Tissue Biopsy + Histology

GMS or H&E stain. Shows broad non-septate hyphae invading blood vessel walls. Surrounding tissue shows ischemic necrosis and thrombosis.

3

Culture on SDA

Without cycloheximide. Rapid growth. Gray-to-black, cottony "dirty snow" appearance within 24–48 hours. Fills the Petri dish rapidly.

4

CT Imaging

CT sinuses/brain: Shows sinus opacification, bony erosion, orbital/intracranial extension. Not specific but guides surgical planning.

💊 Treatment

Antifungal regimens, surgical management, and supportive care

🚨 Urgent Management Principles

• Cryptococcal meningitis: Induction with IV Amphotericin B + Flucytosine → Consolidation with Fluconazole → Maintenance (suppression) in HIV patients
• Mucormycosis: EARLY surgical debridement of all necrotic tissue is CRITICAL — antifungals alone are insufficient
• Reverse the predisposing condition: Treat DKA aggressively · Optimise ART in HIV · Reduce immunosuppression if possible
• Iron chelation: Deferoxamine is CONTRAINDICATED in mucormycosis (provides iron to the fungus) — use only when strongly indicated otherwise
🟣 Amphotericin B First-line Cryptococcosis Induction
Polyene antifungal
Binds to ergosterol in the fungal cell membrane → forms pores → loss of cellular contents → fungal cell death. Used IV for severe CNS cryptococcosis.
Ergosterol binding Nephrotoxicity Infusion reactions Hypokalaemia Induction phase (2 weeks)
🟣 Flucytosine (5-FC) Synergistic with AmB
Pyrimidine antifungal
Converted intracellularly to 5-Fluorouracil → inhibits DNA/RNA synthesis. Used with Amphotericin B for synergy in Cryptococcal meningitis induction. NOT used as monotherapy (resistance develops rapidly).
DNA/RNA synthesis inhibition Bone marrow suppression Hepatotoxicity Combination induction
🟣 Fluconazole Consolidation & Maintenance
Azole antifungal
Inhibits lanosterol 14α-demethylase (cytochrome P450 enzyme) → blocks ergosterol synthesis → disrupts fungal membrane integrity. Used orally for consolidation (8 weeks) and long-term maintenance in HIV patients.
Ergosterol synthesis inhibition Hepatotoxicity Drug interactions (CYP450) Consolidation + Maintenance
🟠 Amphotericin B (Liposomal) First-line Mucormycosis
Polyene antifungal — Liposomal formulation preferred (less nephrotoxic)
Same mechanism as conventional AmB but liposomal formulation allows higher doses with less toxicity. High doses required for mucormycosis. Must be combined with surgical debridement.
Ergosterol binding First-line for Mucormycosis Nephrotoxicity (less than deoxycholate)
🟠 Posaconazole / Isavuconazole Mucormycosis — Step-down / Salvage
Triazole antifungals — Active against Mucorales
Inhibit ergosterol synthesis via 14α-demethylase. Active against Mucorales (unlike fluconazole and voriconazole which have NO activity). Used as step-down oral therapy or salvage when AmB fails.
Ergosterol synthesis inhibition Step-down oral therapy Salvage therapy

⚔️ Surgical Management — Mucormycosis

Surgical debridement is the cornerstone of treatment for mucormycosis — antifungals alone cannot penetrate necrotic tissue. All black necrotic tissue must be excised. This may require:

  • Endoscopic sinus surgery (rhinosinusitis cases)
  • Orbital exenteration (if orbit involved)
  • Repeat surgical debridement as disease progresses
  • Concurrent management of DKA/underlying immunosuppression
