📋 Overview — Key Organisms at a Glance
🦠 Entamoeba histolytica
HIGH-YIELD EXAM-FAVORITE
Parasitic amoeba causing amoebic dysentery (flask-shaped ulcers in the colon) and extra-intestinal disease (liver abscess, lung, brain). Infective stage: mature quadri-nucleate cyst. Only trophozoites are found in tissues. Brain involvement = Secondary Amoebic Meningoencephalitis.
🧠 Naegleria fowleri
HIGH-YIELD EXAM-FAVORITE
Free-living "brain-eating" amoeba. Enters via nasal route during swimming → olfactory nerve → brain. Causes Primary Amoebic Meningoencephalitis (PAM). Rapidly fatal (3–6 days). Only trophozoites found in CSF/tissue. Thrives in warm stagnant water.
👁️ Acanthamoeba castellani
HIGH-YIELD CLINICAL PEARL
Free-living amoeba causing Granulomatous Amoebic Encephalitis (GAE) in immunocompromised hosts and Amoebic Keratitis (contact lens users). Both trophozoites AND cysts found in tissues. Spiky pseudopodia = acanthopodia. Chronic course.
⚡ Key Clinical Signs & Exam Facts
🩸
Flask-shaped Ulcer
EXAM
E. histolytica: Trophozoites erode the mucosa → narrow neck ulcer opens into broad base undermining the submucosa. Found primarily in the caecum.
🍫
Anchovy-Sauce Pus
PEARL
Amoebic Liver Abscess: Thick, chocolate-colored, odorless pus. Composed of lysed hepatocytes, bile & fat. Trophozoites at the abscess wall, not center.
🏊
Swimming → Death in Days
HIGH-YIELD
Naegleria fowleri: Healthy child swims in warm lake → headache, fever, stiff neck → coma → death in 3–6 days. PAM.
👓
Contact Lens Blindness
PEARL
Acanthamoeba keratitis: Homemade/non-sterile lens solution → corneal invasion → can cause blindness within a month.
🧬
Cysts in Tissue?
MCQ TRAP
Only Acanthamoeba has both trophozoites AND cysts in tissues. E. histolytica & Naegleria have only trophozoites in tissues.
🔬
Galactose Lectin
EXAM
E. histolytica adheres to colonic mucosa via a surface lectin (galactose), then secretes cytotoxins and proteolytic enzymes.
🧫
Acanthopodia
EXAM
Acanthamoeba has small, spiky pseudopodia called acanthopodia. Size 20–40 µm. Double-walled cysts are characteristic.
🌡️
Warm Water Risk
HIGH-YIELD
Naegleria thrives in warm stagnant freshwater (lakes, hot springs). Hyper-chlorination required to kill cysts.
📍 Disease Progression — E. histolytica (Intestinal → Brain)

1. Cyst Ingestion

Mature quadri-nucleate cyst in contaminated food/water passes stomach acid (cyst wall protects).

2. Excystation in Large Intestine

Cyst wall breaks down → releases trophozoites → multiply by binary fission in the lumen.

3. Mucosal Adherence & Invasion

Galactose lectin adhesion → secretion of cytolytic enzymes → flask-shaped ulcers form.

4. Sub-mucosal Blood Vessel Invasion

Trophozoites enter blood vessels → travel via hepatic portal vein.

5. Liver Abscess

Trophozoites destroy hepatocytes → anchovy-sauce pus → abscess grows.

6. Secondary Brain Abscess

Further hematogenous spread → brain → secondary amoebic meningoencephalitis. Fatal if untreated.

🏥 Clinical Case Vignettes
🔬 Pathophysiology & Key Structures
🧬 Key Structures — Click to Explore
🔵
Trophozoite (E. histolytica)
20–40 µm, single vesicular nucleus with central karyosome & regular peripheral chromatin. Moves via broad pseudopodia. Contains ingested RBCs (pathognomonic). Produces cytolytic enzymes, cysteine proteinases, and pore-forming proteins (amoebapore). Destroys tissue; never forms cysts in tissue.
🟢
Cyst (E. histolytica)
Mature: 4 nuclei (quadri-nucleate), 10–15 µm, rounded, refractile wall. Resistant to gastric acid and chlorination at standard levels. Survives outside host. Infective stage. Immature: 1–2 nuclei with chromatoid bars. Found only in stool, NEVER in tissues.
🟠
Trophozoite (Naegleria)
Amoeboid form: 10–35 µm, single broad pseudopodium (slug-like). Flagellate form: develops in water (pear-shaped, 2 flagella). Found only in CSF/brain tissue. Thrives at 40–45°C. Cannot form cysts in human tissue.
🟣
Trophozoite (Acanthamoeba)
20–40 µm. Characteristic spiky pseudopodia (acanthopodia). No flagellate form (2 stages only: trophozoite & cyst). Found in soil, water, contact lens fluid. Can invade cornea, skin, lungs, and brain. Both stages found in tissue.
⭕
Cyst (Acanthamoeba)
Double-walled cyst — outer wrinkled wall (ectocyst) + inner wall (endocyst). Highly resistant. Found in tissues (brain, cornea) alongside trophozoites. This is a key differentiator from Naegleria.
🫀
Galactose/GalNAc Lectin
Surface protein of E. histolytica trophozoite. Mediates adherence to colonic epithelium by binding host galactose/N-acetyl-D-galactosamine residues. Crucial first step in pathogenesis. Antibodies against this lectin are protective.

📊 Diagnostic Algorithm — CNS Amoebic Infection

🤒 Patient with Meningoencephalitis

Headache, fever, altered mental status, stiff neck

⬇️

📋 History

Recent swimming in warm water? → think Naegleria
Immunocompromised? → think Acanthamoeba
Prior dysentery / liver abscess? → think E. histolytica

⬇️

🧪 Lumbar Puncture + CSF Analysis

Purulent + motile amoebae + no bacteria = PAM (Naegleria)
Lymphocytic + chronic = consider GAE (Acanthamoeba)

⬇️

🖥️ Brain Imaging (CT/MRI)

Diffuse edema → PAM | Ring-enhancing granulomas → GAE | Abscess + liver history → E. histolytica

⬇️

🔬 Confirm with Culture / PCR / Biopsy

Non-nutrient agar + E. coli | PCR for species | Brain biopsy: cysts? → Acanthamoeba

💊 Treatment
🚨 EMERGENCY — Naegleria fowleri (PAM)
Drug of choice: Amphotericin B (IV + intrathecal) — the only drug with proven activity.

• Initiated immediately upon suspicion — delays are fatal.
• Miltefosine (oral) may be added as adjunct.
• Supportive: control cerebral edema (mannitol, dexamethasone).
• Prognosis: Extremely poor (>95% mortality). Very rare survivors reported with early, aggressive treatment.

⚠️ Prevention is key: Avoid swimming in warm stagnant freshwater. Use nose clips. Hyper-chlorination of swimming pools required (standard chlorination does NOT destroy Naegleria cysts).
🚨 EMERGENCY — E. histolytica Brain Abscess
Metronidazole (tissue amoebicide) — first-line for invasive disease (dysentery, liver abscess, brain abscess).

• Must follow with luminal agent (diloxanide furoate, paromomycin, iodoquinol) to eradicate cysts in the gut lumen.
• Large liver abscesses: Closed aspiration / surgical drainage + Metronidazole.
• Brain abscess: Neurosurgical consultation + Metronidazole.
• Asymptomatic cyst passers: Luminal agents ONLY.
💊 Acanthamoeba — GAE & Keratitis
GAE: Combination therapy — Pentamidine + Sulfadiazine ± Flucytosine ± Fluconazole. Miltefosine may help. Prognosis poor in immunocompromised.

Keratitis: Topical — PHMB (polyhexamethylene biguanide) or chlorhexidine + propamidine isethionate (Brolene). Prolonged therapy (months). May need corneal transplant.

⚠️ Prevention: Use commercial sterile contact lens solutions. Never use homemade saline or tap water. Remove lenses before swimming.

🩹 Supportive & Symptomatic Management
💧
Fluid & Electrolyte Replacement
Essential in amoebic dysentery with severe diarrhea. Oral or IV rehydration depending on severity.
🧊
Cerebral Edema Management
Mannitol, dexamethasone, elevation of head. Critical in PAM and brain abscess.
🔪
Surgical Intervention
Closed aspiration of liver abscess. Neurosurgical drainage of brain abscess. Corneal transplant for failed keratitis treatment.
🧠 Mnemonics
🔤 "FLASK" — E. histolytica Pathogenesis
Remember the steps to flask-shaped ulcer formation
Food/water — cyst ingestion
Large intestine — excystation site
Adherence — galactose lectin
Secretion — cytolytic enzymes
Killing — necrosis → flask ulcer
Full breakdown: The trophozoite adheres to the colonic mucosa using a galactose lectin, then secretes cytotoxins and proteolytic enzymes that kill epithelial cells, forming the classic flask-shaped ulcer with a narrow mucosal opening and broad submucosal base.
🔤 "NOSE → BRAIN" — Naegleria Entry
N.O.S.E. = Naegleria's Only Safe Entry
Nasal route (swimming/sniffing)
Olfactory nerve migration
Six days to death
Eating brain (PAM)
Naegleria fowleri enters ONLY via the nose, never orally. It travels the olfactory nerve through the cribriform plate to the olfactory bulb, then destroys brain tissue. Death typically occurs within 3–6 days. The nasal route = Primary (P)AM.
🔤 "ACE" — Acanthamoeba Characteristics
Remember the 3 key features
Acanthopodia (spiky pseudopodia)
Cysts AND trophozoites in tissue
Eye (keratitis) + Encephalitis (GAE)
Acanthamoeba is unique because BOTH stages (trophozoites with acanthopodia + double-walled cysts) are found in tissues. It causes TWO major diseases: keratitis (contact lens users) and GAE (immunocompromised). Remember: ACE = Acanthamoeba Cysts Everywhere.
🔤 "ANCHOR" — Liver Abscess
Remember the anchovy-sauce pus features
Anchovy-sauce appearance
No bacteria (sterile pus)
Chocolate-colored
Hepatocytes lysed
Odorless
Right lobe most common
Amoebic liver abscess contains thick, chocolate-colored, odorless pus (anchovy-sauce) made up of lysed liver cells, bile & fat. It's usually in the right lobe. It's NOT a true abscess (no bacterial pus) — trophozoites are found at the wall, not the center.
🔤 "TROPIC" — Tissue Stage Rule
Which stages are found in tissue?
TRophozoites — E. histolytica (tissue) ✅
Only trophozoites — Naegleria (tissue) ✅
Plus cysts — Acanthamoeba (tissue) ✅
In gut only — Cysts pass in stool
Cysts NEVER in E.h./N.f. tissue ❌
⚠️ CLASSIC MCQ: "Which amoeba has cysts found in tissue?" → ONLY Acanthamoeba. E. histolytica cysts are ONLY in stool. Naegleria cysts are ONLY in soil/water — never in CSF or brain tissue.
🔤 "4-C-Q" — E. histolytica Infective Cyst
Remember: 4 nuclei Cyst = only infective stage
4 = Quadri-nucleate
C = Cyst (mature)
Q = ingested (oral route)
Only the mature quadri-nucleate cyst is infective. Immature cysts (1–2 nuclei) are not infective. Trophozoites, if ingested, are destroyed by gastric acid. The cyst wall protects against stomach acid, standard chlorination, and drying.
🔤 "PRIMARY vs SECONDARY" — Brain Infections
Never confuse them on the exam!
Primary = PAM = direct nose → brain (Naegleria)
Secondary = Spread from gut/liver → brain (E. histolytica)
Naegleria goes PRIMARILY to the brain (no intestinal or systemic phase first) = PRIMARY amoebic meningoencephalitis.
E. histolytica goes SECONDARILY to the brain (primary site is the intestine/liver, brain is secondary metastasis) = SECONDARY amoebic meningoencephalitis.
