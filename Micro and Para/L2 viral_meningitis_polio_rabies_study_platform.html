🧠 Overview
High-yield summary of all three topics
🦠 Viral Meningitis

Most common type of meningitis. Self-limiting (7–10 days). Two main CNS entry routes:

  • Hematogenous — most common overall
  • Neural penetration — retrograde (HSV, Rabies)
⚠️ Most common cause: Enteroviruses 💡 Gold standard: PCR (RNA)
🧫 Poliovirus

Picornaviridae family · Enterovirus genus · Positive-sense ssRNA · Naked icosahedral · 3 serotypes

  • Entry: fecal-oral route
  • Destroys lower motor neurons → flaccid paralysis
⚠️ Sabin = mucosal IgA (Salk does NOT)
🦇 Rabies Virus

Rhabdoviridae · Lyssavirus · Negative-sense ssRNA · Bullet-shaped · Enveloped with glycoprotein spikes

  • Entry: animal bite (saliva), scratch, inhalation
  • Binds nicotinic ACh receptors at NMJ
⚠️ Gold standard: Fluorescent antibody test

⚡ Key Clinical Signs
🤒 Meningeal Signs

Nuchal rigidity (stiff neck), positive Kernig's sign, positive Brudzinski's sign, photophobia, headache, nausea/vomiting

High-yield
💧 ADH Complication

Inappropriate ADH secretion occurs in 80% of childhood meningitis cases → water retention → ↑ ICP

Exam-favorite
😱 Furious Rabies

Anxiety, agitation, hydrophobia, painful laryngeal spasms on seeing/swallowing water. Most common form.

Clinical pearl
😴 Paralytic Rabies

Flaccid paralysis, ascending weakness, milder sensory disorders. Less dramatic but equally fatal.

High-yield
🦵 Polio Paralysis

Flaccid paralysis (asymmetric, affects lower limbs). Destruction of lower motor neurons in anterior horn of spinal cord.

Exam-favorite
🍎 Apple-Green Fluorescence

Seen in direct immunofluorescence for Rabies antigen (skin biopsy from nape of neck). Gold standard.

🔬 Diagnosis
Tests, gold standards & diagnostic algorithms
🧪 Viral Meningitis
Gold Standard: PCR — detects viral RNA in CSF
  • CSF: Lymphocytosis, Normal glucose, ↑ Protein
  • PCR for RNA viruses (Enteroviruses)
  • Viral culture on monkey kidney cells (slower)
  • Serology for IgM antibodies
⚠️ PCR = gold standard for viral RNA detection
🦇 Rabies Virus
Gold Standard: Direct Immunofluorescence (apple-green color)
  • Skin biopsy (nape of neck) + fluorescein-labeled antibody
  • View under UV microscope → apple-green fluorescence
  • Dog observation for 10 days (if alive)
  • Post-mortem brain biopsy for direct IF
⚠️ Fluorescent antibody test detects antigens

💊 Treatment
Management protocols for all three conditions
🚨 Viral Meningitis — usually self-limiting (7–10 days). No specific antiviral needed for most enteroviruses. Supportive care.
🚨 Rabies Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP)

🩸 Step 1 — Wound Care

  • Wash wound with soap + running water (vigorously)
  • Apply antiseptic (iodine)
  • ⛔ DO NOT SUTURE the wound
  • Suturing exposes nerve endings → viral spread

🛡️ Step 2 — Passive Immunization

  • Human Rabies Immunoglobulin (HRIG) 20 IU/kg
  • ½ dose: infiltrated around the wound
  • ½ dose: distant anterolateral thigh (IM)
  • Neutralizes virus locally + delays CNS fixation
  • Must be at DIFFERENT site from vaccine

💉 Step 3 — Active Vaccination (PEP)

  • 5 doses inactivated Rabies vaccine
  • IM in deltoid (NOT gluteal)
  • Schedule: Days 0, 3, 7, 14, 28
  • Induces endogenous humoral immunity

💊 Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis

  • For vets, lab workers, travelers to endemic areas
  • 3 doses HDCV on days 0, 7, 21
  • Booster doses based on titer levels
💉 Polio Vaccination

💉 Salk (IPV) — Inactivated

  • Formalin-inactivated virus
  • Injection (IM/SC)
  • Induces IgG (humoral) only
  • Protects CNS; does NOT prevent gut replication
  • Safer (no reversion risk)

🍬 Sabin (OPV) — Live Attenuated

  • Live attenuated virus, oral route
  • Induces IgG + Secretory IgA
  • Prevents gut replication + fecal shedding
  • Crucial for global eradication campaigns
  • ⚠️ Rare risk of vaccine-derived polio
⚠️ Key Exam Point: Mucosal (intestinal) IgA immunity is provided by Sabin (OPV) — NOT by Salk (IPV).
