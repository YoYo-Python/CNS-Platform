📋 Overview — CNS Parasitic Infections

Five major parasitic infections affecting the CNS and special senses. Each represents a unique mechanism of tissue invasion, immune evasion, and organ damage.

🦟 Cerebral Malaria

Plasmodium falciparum

Life-threatening complication with iRBC sequestration to brain endothelium, causing DIC, BBB disruption, coma, and seizures.

⚡ HIGH-YIELD 📝 EXAM FAVORITE

🪰 African Trypanosomiasis

T. b. gambiense / T. b. rhodesiense

Sleeping sickness: hemolymphatic stage → meningoencephalitis. Transmitted by Glossina (tsetse) fly. Metacyclic trypomastigote is infective.

⚡ HIGH-YIELD 📝 EXAM FAVORITE

🧫 Secondary Amoebic Abscess

Entamoeba histolytica

Hematogenous spread from large intestine → liver → brain. ONLY trophozoites invade tissue. Cysts are NEVER found in tissues.

📝 EXAM FAVORITE 💎 CLINICAL PEARL

🪱 Neurocysticercosis

Taenia solium (larval stage)

Humans as intermediate host. Ingestion of eggs → larval migration to CNS → space-occupying cysts. Internal autoinfection via antiperistalsis.

⚡ HIGH-YIELD 💎 CLINICAL PEARL

👁️ Onchocerciasis (River Blindness)

Onchocerca volvulus

Simulium (blackfly) transmits L3 larvae → adults in subcutaneous nodules → microfilariae migrate to eyes → hypersensitivity → blindness.

📝 EXAM FAVORITE 💎 CLINICAL PEARL

🔑 Key Clinical Signs & Exam Pearls

🩸 Rosette Formation

iRBCs bind to uninfected RBCs, protecting parasite from splenic clearance and immune response.

HIGH-YIELD

🫁 Winterbottom's Sign

Posterior cervical lymphadenopathy — hallmark of African trypanosomiasis hemolymphatic stage.

EXAM

🔴 Chancre

Painful red nodule at site of tsetse fly bite where metacyclic trypomastigotes multiply locally.

PEARL

👁️ Conjunctivitis + Photophobia

Earliest ocular finding in Onchocerciasis. Precedes keratitis, uveitis, and optic atrophy.

EXAM

🧠 Space-Occupying Lesion

Both neurocysticercosis and secondary amoebic abscess present as brain tumors.

HIGH-YIELD

🧬 DIC

Disseminated intravascular coagulation accompanies cerebral malaria pathogenesis.

HIGH-YIELD

💊 BBB Penetrance

Melarsoprol needed for late-stage trypanosomiasis because it crosses the BBB; Suramin does not.

PEARL

🔁 Autoinfection

Internal (antiperistalsis) and external (fecal-oral) autoinfection unique to T. solium neurocysticercosis.

EXAM

⚠️ Critical Exam Warnings

⚠️ Cerebral Malaria is caused by Plasmodium falciparum ONLY. It is called "malignant" malaria because it is life-threatening. Never attribute cerebral malaria to other Plasmodium species.
⚠️ The infective stage of T. brucei is the METACYCLIC trypomastigote. Writing just "trypanosome" will NOT be counted as correct.
⚠️ The trophozoite is the ONLY form of E. histolytica that causes pathology and is found in tissues. Cysts are NEVER found in tissues.
⚠️ T. b. gambiense = chronic (patient sleeps on side). T. b. rhodesiense = acute (strikes like a stick, rapidly fatal).
💡 Earliest ocular sign in Onchocerciasis = conjunctivitis with photophobia.

🧬 Key Structures & Processes (Click to Expand)

🔴 Rosette Formation

iRBCs binding to uninfected RBCs

Function: Protects the parasite from host immune response and splenic clearance. The rosettes of cells also contribute to microvascular obstruction in the brain, exacerbating cerebral ischemia. This is unique to P. falciparum.

🧪 Nitric Oxide (NO)

Essential for endothelial cell function

In CM: NO levels are significantly reduced, contributing to endothelial dysfunction. Normal NO maintains vascular tone, prevents platelet aggregation, and reduces leukocyte adhesion. Its depletion is central to BBB breakdown.

🧬 Metacyclic Trypomastigote

Infective stage of T. brucei

Life cycle stages: Metacyclic (fly salivary glands, infective) → Long slender bloodstream form → Short stumpy form (→ fly ingests) → Procyclic (fly midgut) → Epimastigote (fly salivary glands) → Metacyclic. Exam demands you specify "metacyclic."

🧫 Trophozoite vs Cyst

E. histolytica tissue-invasive form

Critical distinction: Only the trophozoite invades tissue (has pseudopodia, lytic capabilities). The quadrinucleated mature cyst is the infective/transmissible stage via fecal-oral route but CANNOT survive or form in tissues. Cysts excyst in the intestine to release trophozoites.

🪱 Cysticercus cellulosae

Larval stage of T. solium in brain

Mechanism: When humans ingest T. solium eggs (NOT cysticerci), the eggs hatch into hexacanth embryos/oncospheres that penetrate the intestinal wall and migrate hematogenously. In the brain, they develop into cysticerci, causing a local immune response with neutrophils, eosinophils, and lymphocytes.

👁️ Microfilariae in Eye

O. volvulus → ocular pathology

Mechanism of blindness: Microfilariae released by adult worms in subcutaneous nodules migrate to eyes. Both living and dead microfilariae trigger hypersensitivity reactions. Mechanical damage + toxin release → conjunctivitis → keratitis → uveitis → chorioretinitis → optic atrophy → permanent blindness.

Diagnostic Decision Algorithm
Step-by-step approach for CNS parasitic infections

Step 1: Clinical Presentation

Coma/seizures? Lymphadenopathy? Visual changes? Travel to endemic area?

⬇️

Step 2: Peripheral Blood Analysis

Thick/thin smear for malaria parasites; buffy coat for trypanosomes

⬇️

Step 3: Neuroimaging (CT/MRI)

Space-occupying lesion → NCC or amoebic abscess? Diffuse edema → CM or trypanosomiasis?

⬇️

Step 4: CSF Analysis (if safe)

Trypomastigotes → Sleeping sickness. Trophozoites → Amoebic. Elevated eosinophils → NCC.

⬇️

Step 5: Serology & Molecular

PCR, ELISA, EITB as confirmatory. Skin snip for Onchocerciasis.

🧪 Special Diagnostic Tests

🩹 Patch Skin Test (Onchocerciasis)

A piece of skin is excised and placed in saline. After incubation, microfilariae migrate out of the skin sample and can be seen microscopically. Detects the presence of O. volvulus microfilariae in the dermis.

🧪 Mazzotti Test (Onchocerciasis)

Administration of a small dose of DEC (diethylcarbamazine) causes an intense pruritic reaction if microfilariae are present. The dying microfilariae trigger a hypersensitivity response. Rarely used now due to risk.
