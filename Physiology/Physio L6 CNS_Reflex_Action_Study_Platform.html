⚠️ High-Yield Exam Pearls
EXAM ⭐

Only Monosynaptic Reflex

The stretch reflex is the ONLY monosynaptic reflex in the body. All others are polysynaptic (have interneurons).

EXAM ⭐

Renshaw Cells → Glycine

Renshaw cells release glycine (inhibitory) to limit α-motor neuron discharge — both the original and neighboring neurons.

High Yield

Divergence → Irradiation

Divergence = one interneuron → many efferents → irradiation (multi-segmental reflex, e.g., limb withdrawal from pinprick).

High Yield

Convergence → Spatial Summation

Convergence = many interneurons → fewer efferents → enables spatial summation of signals.

Clinical Pearl

α vs ɣ Motor Neurons

α-motor neurons: large, 70% of AHCs, extrafusal fibers. ɣ-motor neurons: small, 30% of AHCs, intrafusal (spindle) fibers.

Clinical Pearl

RAS Uses Reverberating Circuits

The RAS maintains wakefulness for 16–18 hours via closed-chain (reverberating) circuits. Respiratory center uses them for life.

Key Concept

Afferent Neurons = Pseudo-unipolar

Cell bodies in Dorsal Root Ganglion (DRG). Peripheral branch → receptors; central branch → spinal cord via dorsal roots.

Key Concept

Lateral Inhibition → Sharpens Sense

Active neurons inhibit adjacent less-active neurons → focuses sensory input → sharpens perception of location.

📍 Motor Unit vs. Motor Pool

🔌 Motor Unit

One anterior horn cell (AHC) + its efferent terminals + ALL muscle fibers it supplies.

Distal muscles: fewer fibers/unit → finer control

🏊 Motor Neuron Pool

The TOTAL number of AHCs that supply a single entire muscle.

Lateral AHCs → distal | Medial AHCs → axial

⚡ Clinical Applications of Reflex Mechanisms
🏥 Integrative Case

Spinal Cord Injury at T5–8

A 24-year-old male sustains a car accident with complete spinal cord transaction at T5–8. Examination shows loss of all motor activity and all spinal reflexes below the injury.
1

Structure Involved

Complete transaction of spinal cord at T5–8 — severs all ascending & descending tracts and destroys local reflex arcs.

2

Mechanism

Loss of higher center modulation + local structural damage → all spinal reflexes below injury level fail immediately (spinal shock).

3

Investigation

CT scan reveals fracture/dislocation at T5–8.

4

Consequence

Intact reflex arc pathway (Receptor→Afferent→Interneuron→Efferent→Effector) within the cord is destroyed → no reflex possible.

🔬 Spinal Reflex Classification by Effector

🦴 Somatic Spinal Reflexes

  • Stretch reflex (monosynaptic)
  • Flexion withdrawal reflex
  • Crossed extensor reflex
  • Scratch reflex
  • Plantar reflex

Efferent: α & ɣ motor neurons → skeletal muscle

🫀 Autonomic Spinal Reflexes

  • Micturition reflex
  • Defecation reflex
  • Erection reflex
  • Reflex vasoconstriction of blood vessels

Efferent: Autonomic (sympathetic/parasympathetic) → smooth muscle/glands

💡 Physiological Examples per Circuit
🌊 Divergence → Irradiation

A pinprick to the foot → simultaneous contraction of thigh, leg, and hip flexors (multi-segmental withdrawal). Signal spreads through multiple spinal levels via interneuronal divergence.

🔁 Reverberating → RAS

One waking stimulus in the morning activates the Reticular Activating System, which sustains wakefulness for 16–18 hours via closed-chain reverberating circuits in the brainstem.

🫁 Reverberating → Breathing

The medullary respiratory center maintains rhythmic breathing for life using reverberating circuits. These stop only by severe synaptic fatigue or inhibition.

🎯 Lateral Inhibition → Touch Localization

When you touch a point on the skin, lateral inhibitory circuits suppress surrounding neurons → sharpens the perceived location of the stimulus.

🍕 Conditioned → Salivation

Seeing/smelling food → salivation response via cortical conditioned reflex pathways (requires intact cerebral cortex and previous experience of the food).

⚡ Stretch Reflex → Knee Jerk

Only monosynaptic reflex: muscle stretch → 1a afferent → directly synapses on α-motor neuron → muscle contraction. No interneuron involved.
