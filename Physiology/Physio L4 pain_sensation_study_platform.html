Pain Sensation

Cutaneous & Deep Pain — nociceptors, neural pathways, hyperalgesia, ischemic pain, and their clinical correlates. Covers fast vs. slow pain, autonomic reactions, and the gate-control principles relevant for Mansoura Physiology exams.

⚠️ High Yield MCQ-Heavy Written Qs Viva Favorite

🔑 Core Concepts

  • Pain receptors = free nerve endings (no capsule)
  • Absent in: liver parenchyma, lung alveoli, brain, bone ⚠️
  • Slowly adapting / non-adapting (protective!) ⚠️
  • Stimulation is chemical: Direct stimulators vs. Sensitizers ⚠️
  • Sensitizers (Substance P, PGE) lower threshold
  • Direct stimulators (K+, bradykinin, serotonin, histamine)
  • TRPV1 / TRPA1 channels → Na⁺ & Ca²⁺ influx ⚠️
  • Thermal threshold = 45°C ⚠️

⚡ Neural Pathways

  • Fast pain: A-delta myelinated → neospinothalamic → Glutamate ⚠️
  • Slow pain: C non-myelinated → paleospinothalamic → Substance-P ⚠️
  • Fast pain: felt 0.1s, laminae I & IV, VPLN thalamus → cortex
  • Slow pain: felt 1s, laminae II & III (SGR) → RF & intralaminar nuclei
  • Slow pain causes RAS arousal, emotional reactions ⚠️
  • Cortex: intensity, locality, modality discrimination ⚠️
  • Thalamus: perceives crude diffuse pain without cortex
  • Tickling & itching = C-fibers (anterolateral system)

🔴 Hyperalgesia Types

  • Primary (Allodynia): flare area, non-painful stimuli → pain ⚠️
  • Mechanism: local axon reflex → antidromic Substance-P release
  • Secondary: healthy skin, mild painful stimuli → more pain
  • Mechanism: convergence-facilitation at dorsal horn cells
  • Primary develops in 30–60 min, lasts hours/days
  • Secondary has shorter duration than primary

💔 Deep & Ischemic Pain

  • Deep pain: C-fibers only, poorly localized, dull/burning ⚠️
  • Causes reflex muscle spasm → immobilizes injured part
  • Deep pain autonomics: parasympathetic (↓HR, nausea)
  • Ischemic pain: K⁺ + lactic acid accumulation ⚠️
  • Intermittent claudication: calf pain on walking, relieved by rest ⚠️
  • Mild pain → sympathetic pressor (↑HR, ↑BP, pupil dilation)
  • Severe pain → parasympathetic depressor (↓HR, ↓BP, nausea)

🕒 Timeline: Nail Injury Integrative Scenario

Stepwise events following a nail puncture — the classic integrative example from the lecture

⚡ 0 – 0.1 seconds: Fast Pricking Pain

A-delta myelinated fibers activate. Neospinothalamic tract carries signal to primary sensory cortex. Rapid localization + withdrawal reflex initiated. Glutamate released at laminae I & IV.

🔥 1 second: Slow Burning Pain Begins

C non-myelinated fibers take over. Paleospinothalamic tract to reticular formation & intralaminar nuclei. Substance-P released. Strong arousal, anxiety, and emotional distress (RAS activation).

🌸 30–60 minutes: Flare & Primary Hyperalgesia

Local axon reflex releases Substance-P antidromically. Surrounding skin (flare area) becomes hypersensitive — clothes touching the area cause pain (Allodynia). Receptor threshold drops significantly.

🔵 Hours later: Secondary Hyperalgesia

Convergence-facilitation at dorsal horn cells. Healthy skin around injury becomes more sensitive to mild painful stimuli. Shorter duration than primary hyperalgesia.

🤢 Ongoing: Autonomic Reactions

If pain is severe: parasympathetic depressor response — ↓HR, ↓BP, nausea/vomiting. If mild: sympathetic pressor — ↑HR, ↑BP, pupillary dilatation, ↓GIT motility.

🔬 Key Structures & Mechanisms

⚡ A-delta Fibers

Myelinated, fast-conducting. Carry fast, pricking cutaneous pain. Signal reaches cortex in 0.1s. Release Glutamate at laminae I & IV. Responsible for rapid withdrawal reflexes. Part of neospinothalamic tract.

🐢 C Fibers

Non-myelinated, slow-conducting. Carry slow burning cutaneous AND all deep pain. Signal reaches cortex in 1s. Release Substance-P at laminae II & III (SGR). Part of paleospinothalamic tract. Also carry tickling and itching.

🎯 TRPV1 Channel

Transient Receptor Potential Vanilloid 1. A non-selective cation channel on nociceptors. Activated by direct chemical stimulators (K⁺, bradykinin, heat >43°C). Allows Na⁺ and Ca²⁺ influx → generator potential → action potential.

🔷 Laminae I & IV

Dorsal horn termination of first-order neurons of fast pain (neospinothalamic). Where Glutamate is released. Second-order neurons then cross and ascend in the lateral spinothalamic tract to VPLN of thalamus.

🟢 Substantia Gelatinosa (II & III)

Dorsal horn laminae II & III = "Substantia Gelatinosa of Rolandi (SGR)." Termination of slow C-fiber afferents. Where Substance-P is released. Second-order neurons then relay extensively in the reticular formation and intralaminar nuclei.

🧠 VPLN Thalamus

Ventral Posterolateral Nucleus — relay for fast pain. 2nd-order neurons of neospinothalamic tract synapse here. 3rd-order neurons project to primary somatosensory cortex for precise localization and modality discrimination.

🌀 Reticular Formation

Paleospinothalamic pathway has HEAVY relays here. Activation of the Reticular Activating System (RAS) causes strong arousal, emotional reactions, and somatic responses. Explains why slow pain causes distress, nausea, and behavioral changes.

🔮 Substance P (Triple Role)

1. SENSITIZER: released by peripheral tissue → lowers nociceptor threshold. 2. NEUROTRANSMITTER: slow pain 1st-order neuron → releases in SGR. 3. PRIMARY HYPERALGESIA MEDIATOR: antidromic release in flare area → decreases receptor threshold.

🦿 Dorsal Horn Cells (DHCs)

Central to secondary hyperalgesia via convergence-facilitation. Afferent fibers from the original injury AND from surrounding healthy skin BOTH converge on same DHCs. Continuous barrage from injury site facilitates these neurons → amplified response to healthy skin stimuli.

🗺️ Diagnostic Algorithm: Type of Pain

1

Onset timing?

Immediate (<0.1s) → Fast pain (A-delta). Delayed (~1s) → Slow pain (C-fibers)

2

Pain quality?

Sharp/pricking/well-localized → Cutaneous fast pain. Dull/burning/poorly localized → Deep pain or slow cutaneous

3

Location relative to injury?

At injury site (flare) + non-painful stimulus → Primary hyperalgesia. Surrounding healthy skin + amplified pain → Secondary hyperalgesia

4

Autonomic response?

↑HR, ↑BP → mild pain (sympathetic). ↓HR, nausea → severe pain or deep pain (parasympathetic)

5

Relationship to activity?

Pain during exertion, relieved by rest → Ischemic pain (claudication / angina)

6

Associated muscle spasm?

Reflex rigidity of surrounding muscles → Deep pain origin (muscle, joint, periosteum)

💊 Management & Interventions

Targeting pain mechanisms at each physiological level

🚨 Targeting Sensitizers (Immediate Priority)

🔮 Anti-Substance-P Strategies

  • Block Substance-P release → reduces sensitization and primary hyperalgesia
  • Capsaicin depletes Substance-P from C-fiber terminals
  • Tachykinin receptor antagonists (experimental)

💊 Anti-Prostaglandin (PGE)

  • NSAIDs (ibuprofen, aspirin) inhibit COX enzymes → ↓PGE production
  • Reduce sensitization of nociceptors
  • Most effective when given BEFORE tissue damage (pre-emptive)

🚫 TRPV1 Channel Blockers

  • Block the gateway for Na⁺/Ca²⁺ influx → no action potential
  • Local anesthetics (lidocaine) block Na⁺ channels
  • Heat avoidance to stay below 45°C threshold

🛤️ Targeting Pain Pathways

⚡ Fast Pathway (Neospinothalamic)

  • NMDA antagonists (ketamine) block Glutamate receptors
  • A-delta fiber block with local anesthetics
  • Transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS)

🐢 Slow Pathway (Paleospinothalamic)

  • Opioids (morphine) act at mu receptors in SGR → inhibit Substance-P
  • Opioids also modulate intralaminar nuclei and RAS arousal
  • Tricyclic antidepressants for chronic slow pain management

🧠 Central Modulation

  • Opioids at periaqueductal gray (PAG) — endogenous analgesia system
  • Antidepressants (SNRIs) modulate descending inhibitory pathways
  • Cortical processing modification through cognitive-behavioral therapy

🩸 Ischemic Pain Management

🦵 Intermittent Claudication

  • Rest immediately → allows blood to wash away K⁺ and lactic acid
  • Antiplatelet agents (aspirin, clopidogrel) to prevent thrombosis
  • Statins to slow arteriosclerosis progression
  • Vasodilators to improve peripheral blood supply
  • Supervised exercise rehabilitation programs

💗 Anginal (Cardiac Ischemic) Pain

  • Nitrates → vasodilate coronary vessels → restore blood flow
  • Beta-blockers → ↓myocardial O₂ demand → ↓metabolite accumulation
  • Antiplatelet / anticoagulant therapy
  • Sublingual GTN for acute attacks

💪 Muscle Cramp / Spasm

  • Stretching and massage to physically disperse metabolites
  • Hydration to maintain K⁺ balance
  • Muscle relaxants (baclofen, diazepam) for reflex spasm with deep pain
  • Electrolyte replacement (Mg²⁺, K⁺ correction)

🌸 Hyperalgesia Management

Primary Hyperalgesia

  • Ice packs to limit local axon reflex and reduce Substance-P release
  • NSAIDs reduce PGE sensitization in flare area
  • Topical lidocaine to block antidromic impulses
  • Capsaicin to deplete Substance-P (long-term)

Secondary Hyperalgesia

  • Treat primary lesion to reduce afferent barrage to DHCs
  • NMDA antagonists to reduce central sensitization (ketamine)
  • Gabapentin/pregabalin to stabilize dorsal horn excitability
  • Spinal cord stimulation for chronic cases
