Sensory System
Detects environmental changes (internal/external) → transmits via afferent fibers → informs specific CNS centers to adopt adequate responses.
Core Concept

Sensory Receptors
Specialized microscopic structures that act as detectors & transducers — converting all forms of energy into electrical nerve impulses conducted to CNS.
High Yield

Sensory Unit
A single afferent neuron and ALL the sensory receptor endings attached to it. The fundamental building block of sensory perception.
Core Concept
🗺️
Receptive Field
The area of the body (e.g., skin) covered by a single sensory unit. Stimulating anywhere within this area activates the corresponding sensory neuron.

Muller's Law (Specificity)
Each receptor is highly sensitive to ONE specific stimulus type (adequate stimulus) → yields one sensation regardless of how it is stimulated.

Receptor Potential
Local, graded potential change upon stimulation. Usually partial depolarization (Na+ influx) — EXCEPT photoreceptors which hyperpolarize. NOT blocked by local anesthetics.
High Yield⚠️ Exam

Weber-Fechner Law
R = log S × K. Rate of discharge ∝ log of stimulus intensity. Doubling discharge: 100× intensity. Tripling: 1000× intensity. Compression function.
High Yield⚠️ Exam

Receptor Adaptation
Decrease in discharge despite constant stimulus. Phasic = rapidly adapting (touch). Tonic = slowly adapting (pain, baroreceptors, muscle spindles).
High YieldClinical Pearl
📊
Recruitment
Increasing stimulus intensity activates MORE neighboring receptors. Two encoding mechanisms: ↑ individual discharge rate + ↑ number of excited receptors.
Core Concept
🔬
Pacinian Corpuscle
Large, encapsulated mechanoreceptor ideal for study — easily dissected and stimulated. Concentric lamellae compress, deforming nerve terminal. Classic model for receptor potential studies.
Exam FavoriteClinical Pearl
👁️
Photoreceptor Exception
Unlike all other receptors, photoreceptors respond to their adequate stimulus (light) with HYPERPOLARIZATION — not depolarization. Key exam exception!
High Yield⚠️ Exam
💉
Local Anesthetics & Receptors
Local anesthetics block voltage-gated Na+ channels (blocking APs) but do NOT block receptor potentials (which use mechanically-gated channels). Critical distinction!

💊 Receptor Properties — Deep Dive
Understanding every property of receptors is essential for exam success. These are the four cardinal properties you must know.
🎯 Property 1: Specificity (Muller's Law)
Definition: Each receptor is highly sensitive to ONE specific type of stimulus (adequate stimulus).

Key Point: Stimulation gives rise to one specific type of sensation REGARDLESS of how it is stimulated.

Example: Pressing on the eye produces a sensation of light (phosphene) — because retinal photoreceptors always interpret stimulation as light.

Clinical: Ice on skin → only cold receptors respond, even though touch/pressure receptors are also present.
⚡ Property 2: Excitability (Receptor Potential)
Mechanism: Adequate stimulus → mechanical deformation → opens Na⁺ channels → Na⁺ influx → partial depolarization.

⚠️ EXCEPTION: Photoreceptors → HYPERPOLARIZE.

Key Properties:
• Graded amplitude (up to ~100 mV)
• ~5 msec longer than AP
• Can be summated (spatial + temporal)
• Localized spread
• ⚠️ NOT blocked by local anesthetics
• Does NOT obey all-or-none rule
📈 Property 3: Rate of Discharge (Weber-Fechner Law)
Formula: R = log S × K

What it means:
• Stimulus 10 → Rate 1
• Stimulus 100 → Rate 2
• Stimulus 1000 → Rate 3
• To DOUBLE rate: 100× intensity
• To TRIPLE rate: 1000× intensity

Compression function: Prevents CNS overload by compressing massive intensity changes into small frequency changes.

Recruitment: Also ↑ number of excited receptors as intensity increases.
🔄 Property 4: Adaptation
Definition: Decrease in discharge rate despite prolonged, constant stimulation.

Phasic (Rapid): Touch/tactile receptors — clothes sensation disappears. Prevents CNS overload.

Tonic (Slow): Pain, baroreceptors, muscle spindles — must keep firing for vital functions (posture, BP, pain protection).

Mechanism: Na⁺ channel inactivation at 1st node of Ranvier (accommodation).
🔑 Key Distinctions for Exams
⚠️ Mechanically-Gated vs. Voltage-Gated Na⁺ Channels
Mechanically-gated: Found at unmyelinated nerve terminal. Opened by physical deformation. Generate RECEPTOR potential. NOT blocked by local anesthetics.

Voltage-gated: Found at 1st node of Ranvier and along axon. Opened by change in voltage. Generate ACTION potential. BLOCKED by local anesthetics.
⚠️ Depolarization vs. Hyperpolarization
Most receptors: Adequate stimulus → Na⁺ influx → partial DEPOLARIZATION (membrane becomes less negative).

Photoreceptors (EXCEPTION): Light stimulus → HYPERPOLARIZATION (membrane becomes MORE negative). This is a critical exam point!
📐 Spatial vs. Temporal Summation
Spatial summation: Multiple receptor potentials from DIFFERENT locations simultaneously combine to reach threshold.

Temporal summation: Multiple receptor potentials from the SAME location in rapid succession combine to reach threshold.
🧠 Why Study Pacinian Corpuscles?
Three reasons they're the ideal model:
• Large size — visible, easy to isolate
• Easy to dissect — well-encapsulated
• Easy to stimulate — microglass rod compression

These make them the gold standard for studying receptor/generator potentials.
