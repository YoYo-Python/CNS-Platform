⚡ Quick Overview
⚠️ EXAM ALERT: The stretch reflex is the ONLY monosynaptic reflex in the body. This gives it the shortest latent period, high localization, and NO after-discharge/recruitment.
⚠️ EXAM ALERT: Muscle tone = static stretch reflex in a dynamic state (NOT static state). It is sub-tetanic, continuous partial contraction in all skeletal muscles at rest.
⚡ Dynamic Stretch Reflex
Stimulus: Sudden stretch (change in length)
Receptor: Nuclear bag fibers
Afferent: A-α nerve fibers
Duration: Short (fraction of a second)
Result: Rapid contraction then relaxation
Clinical: Tendon jerks (e.g., knee jerk)
γ regulation: γ-dynamic fibers
⭐ High Yield 📋 Exam Fav
🔄 Static Stretch Reflex
Stimulus: Maintained stretch
Receptor: Nuclear chain fibers
Afferent: A-β nerve fibers
Duration: Continuous (as long as stretched)
Result: Continuous contraction = muscle tone
Clinical: Muscle tone maintenance
γ regulation: γ-static fibers
⭐ High Yield 💎 Pearl
🛑 Inverse Stretch Reflex (Lengthening Reaction)
Stimulus: Excessive tension in tendon
Receptor: Golgi Tendon Organs (GTO)
Afferent: A-α (Ib) fibers
Synapse: Di-synaptic (via interneurons)
Result: Muscle RELAXATION (protective)
Clinical: Clasp Knife Rigidity in UMNL
Protects against: Muscle tearing, tendon avulsion
⭐ High Yield 📋 Exam Fav
🏋️ Load Reflex (Servo-assist)
Definition: Reflex that keeps limb position when load is applied
Mechanism: Load → muscle elongates → stretches spindles → ↑spindle discharge → reflex contraction
Role of γ-MNs: Brain co-activates α & γ-MNs simultaneously
Clinical: Holding a cup when more tea is added
📋 Written Q 💎 Pearl
🏃 Key Properties of Stretch Reflex
⚡ Shortest Latent Period
ONLY monosynaptic reflex = single synapse delay + fast-conducting Aα/Aβ fibers
Monosynaptic
🎯 Highly Localized
No interneurons = No divergence = Contraction ONLY in the stretched muscle
📋 MCQ
💪 No Fatigue
Asynchronous motor unit contraction + high number of slow (tonic) fibers in antigravity muscles
🚫 No Recruitment/After-discharge
No interneurons = No reverberating circuits = No after-discharge
📋 MCQ
↔️ Reciprocal Innervation
Stretched muscle contracts while its antagonist reflexively relaxes → smooth coordinated movement
📊 Graded Response
Response is proportional to the strength of the stimulus (stretch intensity)
🔄 Functions of the Stretch Reflex
🦾 Smoothing (Damping / Signal Averaging)
Brain sends unsmooth signals → muscle spindle provides indirect reflex contraction that averages/summates with direct contraction → prevents oscillation & jerky movements

⚠️ EXAM: "Very crucial function - making movement smooth"
🧍 Muscle Tone Maintenance
Continuous sub-tetanic contraction of skeletal muscles at rest → maintains posture, body temperature, venous return, and viscera in position

⚠️ EXAM: "Static stretch reflex in a DYNAMIC state" (can change with posture)

🔬 Receptor Comparison
🌀 Muscle Spindle (Intrafusal Fibers)
Location: Within the muscle belly (parallel to extrafusal fibers)
Detects: Changes in muscle LENGTH
Types:
→ Nuclear bag fibers → Dynamic (A-α)
→ Nuclear chain fibers → Static (A-β)
γ-MN control: γ-dynamic & γ-static fibers
Result: Muscle CONTRACTION (excitatory)
🔗 Golgi Tendon Organ (GTO)
Location: At the musculotendinous junction
Detects: Changes in muscle TENSION
Afferent: A-α (Ib) fibers
Synapse: Di-synaptic (via inhibitory interneurons)
Result: Muscle RELAXATION (inhibitory)
Clinical: Clasp knife rigidity in UMNL

🩺 Clinical Signs Algorithm
↑ Muscle Tone (Spasticity/Rigidity)
Suggests: UMNL (Area 4 & 6 lesion)
Mechanism: Net cortical inhibition removed
Sign: Clasp Knife Rigidity = Inverse stretch reflex triggered
Reflexes: Hyperreflexia (↑ tendon jerks)
↓ Muscle Tone (Hypotonia/Flaccidity)
Suggests: LMNL or spinal shock (cord transection)
Mechanism: Supraspinal facilitation removed
Sign: Areflexia (↓/absent tendon jerks)
Reflexes: Hyporeflexia
🧬 Supraspinal Control of Stretch Reflex
⚠️ EXAM: "Supraspinal control of stretch reflex — مهم جدا (Very important)" — understand facilitatory vs inhibitory areas, and the net cortical effect.
⬆️ FACILITATORY Areas
  • Area 4 (Primary motor cortex)
  • Facilitatory reticular formation (Pons/Midbrain)
  • Neocerebellum (lateral cerebellum)
  • Vestibular nucleus
⬇️ INHIBITORY Areas
  • Area 6 (Premotor cortex)
  • Inhibitory reticular formation (Medulla)
  • Paleocerebellum (vermal cerebellum)
  • Basal ganglia
⚡ NET EFFECT
  • Normal: Net facilitatory (tone maintained)
  • Area 4+6 net: INHIBITORY
  • UMNL: Remove inhibition → ↑ tone (spasticity)
  • Spinal shock: Remove facilitation → ↓ tone (flaccid)
🔄 γ-MN Control
  • Brain co-activates α & γ-MNs
  • γ-MNs set spindle sensitivity
  • Adjusts response to load
  • Pain → ↑ γ-MN discharge

⚙️ Regulation of Muscle Tone
🦵 Antigravity Muscles (More Tone)
Lower limb extensors, jaw elevators, back extensors are continuously stretched by gravity → more static stretch reflex activity → more prominent tone.

Key example: Jaw elevators keep mouth closed against gravity even at rest.
😴 Factors Modifying Muscle Tone
↑ Tone: Pain stimuli, emotional excitement, anxiety
↑ Tone: UMNL, Decerebrate state
↓ Tone: Sleep, fatigue
↓ Tone: Spinal shock, LMNL, anaesthesia
↓ Tone: Cerebellar lesion
