🏠 Overview
Core concepts at a glance — the essential framework of vestibular physiology
💡 Remember: The key exam exception — vestibular hair cell depolarization occurs via K⁺ influx (not Na⁺!), because endolymph is uniquely rich in potassium.
📍 Location

Non-auditory part of the inner ear labyrinth, housed in the petrous portion of the temporal bone at the skull base.

🏗️ Components

Two otolith organs (utricle & saccule) containing Maculae, plus three semicircular canals (horizontal, anterior vertical, posterior vertical) containing Crista ampullaris.

⚡ Key Ionic Exception

Unlike typical neurons that depolarize via Na⁺, vestibular hair cells depolarize via K⁺ influx — because the endolymph is K⁺-rich (similar to intracellular fluid).

⚠️ Exam Favourite High-Yield
🔄 Two Receptor Systems

Maculae: Detect gravity + linear acceleration via Otoconia (calcium carbonate crystals).
Crista ampullaris: Detect angular acceleration via cupula deflection.

🧬 Neural Output

Vestibular ganglion → Vestibular nucleus → Spinal cord, Reticular formation, Contralateral thalamus, Cerebellum, CN III/IV/VI nuclei (via MLF).

🌊 Co-planar Canal Rule

Each semicircular canal on one side corresponds to one SCC on the opposite side lying in the same geometric plane. Both act together symmetrically. MCQ Favourite

🕐 Signal Flow Timeline

From head movement to conscious perception

1
Head Movement
Angular or linear acceleration displaces the endolymph relative to the membranous labyrinth
2
Mechanical Transduction
Deflects cupula (SCCs) or otolith membrane (maculae) → bends stereocilia toward or away from kinocilium
3
Ionic Response ⚠️
Toward kinocilium → K⁺ channels open → K⁺ influx → depolarization. Away → channels close → hyperpolarization
4
Vestibular Nerve
Changed firing rate travels from vestibular ganglion along CN VIII to the vestibular nucleus in the brainstem
5
Central Processing
Vestibular nucleus distributes signals to spinal cord (posture), reticular formation (autonomics), cerebellum, and extraocular motor nuclei
6
Conscious Awareness
Fibers pass to contralateral thalamus → somatic sensory cortex → conscious sense of equilibrium and spatial orientation

Basal Discharge Rate

Inertia vs Momentum
Inertia acts at onset of acceleration (opposite direction). Momentum acts at deceleration (same direction).

⚡ Clinical Manifestations
Effects of excessive or asymmetrical vestibular stimulation
⚠️ Exam written question: "These manifestations include…" — learn all four categories: Vertigo, Nystagmus, Autonomic reactions, Changes in muscle tone
🌀
Vertigo
A false sensation of rotational movement. The brain misinterprets an asymmetrical discharge pattern as genuine rotation. Occurs after sudden stoppage of rotation or in labyrinthine disease.
Mechanism: Asymmetrical discharge from both vestibular apparatuses → cortex interprets as rotation
👁️
Nystagmus
Rapid, oscillatory eye movements. Has a slow phase (VOR attempt to stabilize gaze) and a fast corrective phase. Direction named after fast phase.
Mechanism: Asymmetrical vestibular input → vestibular nucleus → MLF → CN III, IV, VI nuclei → rhythmic extraocular muscle contractions
🤢
Motion Sickness
The classic triad: Bradycardia, Hypotension (cardiovascular), plus Nausea, Vomiting, Profuse Sweating (GI/autonomic). Triggered by unusual or excessive patterns of vestibular stimulation.
Mechanism: Vestibular nucleus → reticular formation autonomic centers → parasympathetic/sympathetic dysregulation
💪
Muscle Tone Changes
Altered tonus of skeletal muscles, especially those maintaining posture. The system attempts to counter perceived falling by increasing tone in anti-gravity muscles.
Mechanism: Vestibular nucleus → vestibulospinal tract → spinal motor neurons → postural muscle adjustment
🛑
Post-Rotatory Vertigo
Appears 20–30 seconds after stopping rotation. Endolymph momentum continues moving after body stops, bending cupula "in reverse" — creating a false counter-rotation sensation.
Mechanism: Endolymph momentum → cupula deflection opposite to recent rotation → asymmetrical discharge → false counter-rotation percept
🩺
Menière's Disease
Excess endolymph (endolymphatic hydrops) causing episodic: Vertigo + Nystagmus + Tinnitus + Hearing loss. Classic example of asymmetrical excessive SCC stimulation.
Mechanism: Endolymphatic hydrops → asymmetrical & excessive stimulation of SCCs → unpredictable bursts of vertigo
