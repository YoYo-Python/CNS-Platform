📍 Visual Pathway — Step by Step
① Photoreceptors (Rods & Cones)Light transduction occurs. Rods for scotopic, cones for photopic and color vision. Cones contain L (red), M (green), S (blue) pigments.
② Bipolar Cells — 1st Order NeuronON (depolarizing) and OFF (hyperpolarizing) bipolar cells create positive and negative images. Lateral inhibition via Horizontal & Amacrine cells sharpens contrast.
③ Ganglion Cells — 2nd Order NeuronTheir axons form the Optic Nerve. Functions: 2-point discrimination, contrast detection, motion, color analysis.
④ Optic ChiasmaNasal fibers CROSS (decussate) → contralateral side. Temporal fibers STAY → ipsilateral side. This enables binocular visual field representation.
⑤ Lateral Geniculate Body (LGB) — 3rd OrderIn the thalamus. Fuses retinal images; stereoscopic vision. Contains Magnocellular (B&W + motion) and Parvocellular (color + spatial detail) neurons.
⑥ Visual Cortex (Areas 17, 18, 19)Area 17: calcarine fissure, detects lines/borders. Area 18 (visuo-psychic): interprets/recognizes objects. Area 19 (occipital eye field): controls eye movements.

🧠 Cortical Visual Areas
Area 17
Primary Visual Cortex
Surrounds calcarine fissure, occipital lobe. Detects lines, borders, orientation, basic colors. Fuses images from both eyes.

Upper retina → Above fissure
Lower retina → Below fissure
Area 18
Visuo-psychic Area ⚠️
Interprets visual sensations. Recognizes objects, correlates colors, depth perception.

Lesion → Visual Agnosia
Patient sees but cannot recognize what they see.
Area 19
Occipital Eye Field
Controls different types of eyeball movements. Surrounds Area 18. Shares some visual association functions with Area 18.

Lesion → Impaired tracking of moving objects.
