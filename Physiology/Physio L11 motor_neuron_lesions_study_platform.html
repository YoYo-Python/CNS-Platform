🧠 Topic Overview

Quick-reference summary of all three core topics — UMNL, LMNL, and Spinal Cord Lesions

🔴 Babinski's Sign — High Yield ⭐ Internal Capsule — Exam Favourite 💎 Brown-Séquard Dissociation — Pearl 📘 USMLE Step 1 Topic 🔴 Clasp-Knife Spasticity — High Yield 💎 Fasciculations vs Fibrillations ⭐ Spinal Shock — Exam Favourite 📘 Mass Reflex — Clinical Pearl

🔴 Upper Motor Neuron Lesion UMNL

  • Site: Internal capsule (lenticulostriate artery thrombosis)
  • Contralateral hemiplegia — distal limbs + lower face + tongue
  • Paresis only of axial muscles & upper face (bilateral innervation)
  • Clasp-knife spasticity (antigravity muscles)
  • Hyperreflexia, clonus, Babinski's sign ✅
  • Loss of cremasteric & abdominal reflexes
  • Mechanism: Unopposed vestibulo/reticulospinal facilitation

🟢 Lower Motor Neuron Lesion LMNL

  • Site: Anterior horn cells or peripheral nerves
  • Flaccid paralysis (voluntary + postural + reflex lost)
  • Wallerian degeneration (distal) + retrograde degeneration
  • Fasciculations — visible, synchronous, entire motor unit
  • Fibrillations — invisible, EMG-detected, individual fibres
  • Denervation supersensitivity — ACh receptors spread over whole membrane
  • Marked muscle atrophy

🟡 Spinal Cord Lesions SPINAL

  • Spinal shock: lasts 2–6 weeks after transection
  • Areflexia, flaccidity, autonomic retention with overflow incontinence
  • Recovery: flexion reflex → flexor stretch → extensor stretch → mass reflex
  • Mass reflex: scratch → bilateral flexion + bladder/bowel + sweating + ↑ABP
  • Brown-Séquard: ipsi LMNL at level, ipsi UMNL + proprioception loss below
  • Contralateral loss of pain & temperature below lesion

🔬 Diagnosis & Investigations

How to identify UMNL vs LMNL vs Spinal Cord lesions clinically

🔴 UMNL — Diagnostic Algorithm
1

History & Presentation

Sudden onset hemiplegia (or weakness) affecting contralateral face, arm, and leg. History of stroke, hypertension, or TIA. Look for risk factors for lenticulostriate artery thrombosis.

2

Neurological Examination

Check tone (spasticity, clasp-knife), reflexes (hyperreflexia, clonus), and plantar response. Positive Babinski's sign confirms UMNL. Loss of abdominal and cremasteric reflexes.

Babinski's Sign ✅ Hyperreflexia Clasp-Knife Test Clonus
3

Imaging

MRI brain — gold standard to identify the internal capsule lesion, ischemic or hemorrhagic territory of the lenticulostriate artery. CT brain is faster in emergency to rule out hemorrhage.

MRI Brain 🥇 CT Brain (Emergency)
4

Differentiate from LMNL

In UMNL: no atrophy (or mild), no fasciculations, Babinski positive, spastic tone, hyperreflexia. In LMNL: marked atrophy, fasciculations, no Babinski, flaccid tone, areflexia.

EMG (for LMNL fibrillations) NCS (nerve conduction)
🟡 Brown-Séquard — Diagnostic Criteria
1

At the Level of the Lesion (Ipsilateral)

LMNL type: flaccid paralysis + total loss of ALL sensations (all modalities) at that exact dermatome level on the same side.

2

Below the Lesion — Motor (Ipsilateral)

UMNL type: spastic paralysis, hyperreflexia, Babinski's sign, loss of voluntary movement — all on the same side as the lesion.

3

Below the Lesion — Dorsal Column Sensory (Ipsilateral)

Loss of proprioception, vibration sense, and fine/discriminative touch — same side as the lesion (fibres don't cross until medulla).

4

Below the Lesion — Anterolateral Sensory (Contralateral)

Loss of pain and temperature — opposite side from the lesion (fibres cross immediately in the spinal cord before ascending).

💊 Management Principles

Approach to each lesion type — urgency-coded by colour

🚨 Acute / Emergency (UMNL — Stroke)

🚨 Immediate Stroke Protocol

  • ABC stabilisation
  • Emergency CT to rule out hemorrhage
  • Thrombolytics (tPA) if ischemic within 4.5h window
  • Admit to stroke unit

🔴 ICP Management

  • Head elevation 30°
  • Osmotherapy (mannitol) if cerebral oedema
  • Avoid hypoxia, hypotension, hyperthermia

⚠️ Spasticity Management (UMNL Chronic)

⚠️ Physiotherapy (First Line)

  • Stretching and range-of-motion exercises
  • Splinting and positioning
  • Gait rehabilitation

💊 Antispasticity Drugs

  • Baclofen (GABA-B agonist — reduces γ-MN firing)
  • Tizanidine (α2 agonist)
  • Diazepam (GABA-A — adjunct)
  • Botulinum toxin (focal spasticity)

🟢 LMNL Management

🟢 Address the Cause

  • Traumatic: surgical decompression/repair
  • Neuropathy: treat underlying disease (e.g., DM control)
  • GBS: IVIG or plasmapheresis

🟢 Nerve Regeneration Support

  • Physiotherapy to prevent contractures
  • Electrical stimulation to maintain muscle bulk
  • B12 and neurotrophin supplementation

📊 Monitor with EMG

  • Serial EMG to track reinnervation
  • Nerve conduction studies
  • Watch for fibrillation → motor unit potential (recovery sign)

🟡 Spinal Cord Injury Management

🚨 Acute SCI

  • Immobilise spine immediately
  • High-dose methylprednisolone (within 8h — controversial)
  • Surgical decompression if indicated
  • Manage spinal shock: IV fluids, vasopressors

⚠️ Bladder/Bowel Care

  • Indwelling or intermittent catheterisation (retention phase)
  • Later: bladder training (reflex voiding phase)
  • Bowel programme to prevent fecal impaction

🟢 Mass Reflex Prevention

  • Avoid noxious skin stimuli
  • Treat pressure ulcers aggressively
  • Baclofen (oral or intrathecal) for reflex reduction

📊 Rehabilitation

  • Multidisciplinary team approach
  • Occupational therapy + physiotherapy
  • Psychological support
  • FES (functional electrical stimulation)
