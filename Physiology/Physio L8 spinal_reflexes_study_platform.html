MODULE 1 Overview & Key Concepts
➡️
One-Way Conduction
Impulses travel only presynaptic → post-synaptic. Reason: no transmitter vesicles in the post-synaptic membrane.
⚠️ MCQ EXAM
⏱️
Central Delay
Time for impulse to traverse central synapses. Depends primarily on the number of synapses in the pathway.
⚠️ MCQ EXAM
😴
Synaptic Fatigue
Gradual decline/disappearance of reflex with prolonged stimulation due to depletion of neurotransmitter vesicles.
🔬 Clinical Pearl
📍
Localization (Local Sign)
Stimulus site determines response type. Medial thigh → flexors & abductors. Lateral → flexors & adductors.
🔬 Clinical Pearl
📡
Irradiation
Stronger stimuli → more receptors activated → divergence → wider spinal cord area → more widespread response.
⭐ High Yield
🔄
Reciprocal Inhibition
Agonist contraction → reflex inhibition of antagonist via inhibitory interneurons. Exception: Positive Supporting Reaction ⚠️
⚠️ Very Important!
📈
Recruitment
Gradual increase in reflex magnitude to maximum. Caused by varying afferent thicknesses & interneuron circuits.
⭐ High Yield
🔊
After Discharge
Reflex response continues for some time after stimulus stops — due to reverberating interneuronal circuits.
🔬 Clinical Pearl
⚡
Reflex vs Motor Tetanus
Reflex tetanus: gradual rise & decline, much longer latent period. Motor: sudden rise/fall, shorter latency.
⚠️ MCQ EXAM
🦵
Positive Supporting Reaction
Deep pressure on the sole → simultaneous contraction of flexors AND extensors. Converts limb into a rigid pillar. Only exception to reciprocal inhibition!
⚠️ Very Important!
🔬
Muscle Spindle
Length receptor with 4-12 intrafusal fibers (nuclear bag & chain) in a capsule. Detects muscle stretch.
⭐ High Yield
⚡
Primary Afferents (Ia)
Annulospiral endings. Thick myelinated, rapidly conducting Type A-α (Ia) fibers. From both bag & chain fibers.
⚠️ MCQ EXAM
🌸
Secondary Afferents (II)
Flower spray endings. Thin myelinated, less rapidly conducting Type A-β (II) fibers. Only from nuclear chain.
⚠️ MCQ EXAM
🎯
Gamma Efferents
γ-dynamic → nuclear bag. γ-static → nuclear chain. Supraspinal stimulation → intrafusal contraction → spindle activation.
⭐ High Yield
🚨
Flexion-Withdrawal Reflex
Polysynaptic. Receptors: free nerve endings. Afferents: A-δ fibers. Prepotent — suppresses all other reflexes. Survival value.
⚠️ MCQ EXAM
🔀
Crossed-Extensor Reflex
Accompanies flexion-withdrawal. Interneurons cross → excite contralateral extensors & inhibit contralateral flexors. Supports posture.
⚠️ MCQ EXAM
Pathway Sequence Reflex Arc Timeline
🎯
1. Stimulus & Receptor Activation
Stimulus (pain, stretch, pressure) activates receptors. Flexion-withdrawal: free nerve endings → A-δ. Stretch reflex: muscle spindle → Ia/II. Positive supporting: pressure receptors in sole.
Afferent limb begins
⚡
2. Afferent Conduction
Signal travels up afferent nerve fibers. A-δ for pain, Ia (Type A-α) for primary spindle afferents, Type II (A-β) for secondary. Myelination determines speed.
Central delay starts
🧠
3. Central Synaptic Processing
Interneuronal pool in spinal cord processes signal. Duration = central delay (depends on number of synapses). Divergence occurs with strong stimuli (irradiation). After-discharge circuits here.
Reciprocal inhibition wired here
🏃
4. Efferent Output
α-motor neurons activate target extrafusal muscle. γ-motor neurons maintain spindle sensitivity. Anterior horn cells (AHCs) are the final common pathway.
Effector limb
💪
5. Motor Response & After-Effects
Reflex contraction of agonist + reciprocal inhibition of antagonist. After-discharge may continue response post-stimulus. Recruitment gradually builds maximum response.
Reflex complete
