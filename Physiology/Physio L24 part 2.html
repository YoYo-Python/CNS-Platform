📋 Course Overview

🐚 Cochlear Structure

A coiled bony tube (~3.5 cm) divided into 3 compartments: scala vestibuli (perilymph), scala media (endolymph, high K⁺), and scala tympani (perilymph). Houses the Organ of Corti on the basilar membrane.

⚡ Mechano-transduction

Stereocilia bending → K⁺ influx → depolarization → Ca²⁺ influx → glutamate release → cochlear nerve activation. Endocochlear potential (+80 mV) creates −140 mV gradient for extreme sensitivity.

🌊 Traveling Wave

Ossicles transmit to oval window → fluid waves in cochlea → tonotopic basilar membrane: base (short, stiff → high freq) to apex (long, lax → low freq). Place Principle of pitch discrimination.

🧠 Auditory Pathway

5 neuronal orders: Spiral ganglion → Cochlear nuclei → Superior olive → Lateral lemniscus → Inferior colliculus → MGB → Auditory cortex (areas 41, 42). Bilateral crossing via trapezoid body.

🎵 Sound Encoding

Pitch: Place & Frequency principles. Intensity: Temporal summation, spatial summation, high-intensity cells. Localization: Medial olive (time-lag), lateral olive (intensity difference).

🔄 Sound Transmission Timeline

1. Pinna & External Canal

Pinna collects and directs sound waves into the external auditory canal toward the tympanic membrane.

2. Tympanic Membrane

Sound waves cause the tympanic membrane to vibrate rapidly.

3. Middle Ear Ossicles

Malleus → Incus → Stapes amplify and transmit vibrations to the oval window.

4. Oval Window & Cochlear Fluid

Stapes footplate movement creates traveling waves in perilymph of scala vestibuli.

5. Basilar Membrane Displacement

Traveling wave causes specific regions to maximally vibrate based on frequency (tonotopic).

6. Hair Cell Mechano-transduction

Stereocilia shearing → K⁺ influx → depolarization → Ca²⁺ → glutamate release → nerve impulse.

7. Auditory Pathway to Cortex

Spiral ganglion → cochlear nuclei → superior olive → lateral lemniscus → inferior colliculus → MGB → auditory cortex.

🧪 Cochlear Potentials & Diagnostic Testing

⚡ Cochlear Potentials

🔋 Endocochlear Potential
Resting+80 mV

A resting electrical potential between endolymph and perilymph. Maintained by the K⁺ pump in stria vascularis. Creates the −140 mV gradient across hair cell apex (endolymph +80 mV vs. intracellular −60 mV). Greatly sensitizes hair cells to the slightest sounds.

🎵 Cochlear Microphonic Potential
Sound-EvokedOuter Hair Cells

Sum of receptor potentials generated by a large population of hair cells, mainly outer (lateral) hair cells, during sound exposure. Can be recorded from the round window. Reflects outer hair cell function.

📊 Receptor Potential
Hair CellGraded

Generated individually in each hair cell by K⁺ influx through mechanically-gated apical channels. Proportional to the intensity of basilar membrane displacement. Triggers voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channel opening.

🧠 Auditory Pathway & Cortex

🛤️ Ascending Auditory Pathway — 5 Neuronal Orders

1️⃣ Spiral Ganglion (1st Order)

Bipolar cells in the cochlea. Peripheral process → Organ of Corti. Central process → cochlear nerve (CN VIII).

⬇️
Dorsal & Ventral Cochlear Nuclei

In the medulla. Fibers cross via trapezoid body (bilateral projection).

⬇️
2️⃣ Superior Olivary Nucleus (2nd Order) — Pons

Site of binaural processing. Medial part: time-lag detection. Lateral part: intensity difference.

⬇️
3️⃣ Lateral Lemniscus (3rd Order)

Ascending tract from superior olive to midbrain. Some fibers synapse in the lemniscal nucleus.

⬇️
4️⃣ Inferior Colliculus (4th Order) — Midbrain

Major relay station. Integrates ascending auditory information. Auditory reflexes (startle).

⬇️
5️⃣ Medial Geniculate Body (MGB) — Thalamus

ALL auditory fibers synapse here. Final subcortical relay before cortex.

⬇️
🧠 Primary Auditory Cortex (Areas 41 & 42)

Upper temporal lobe. Tonotopic: anterolateral = apex (low pitch), posteromedial = base (high pitch).

🧠 Auditory Cortex Functions

🎯

Primary Auditory Area (41 & 42)

Conscious Perception

Perceives pitch, amplitude, and pattern of sound. Tonotopically organized (anterolateral→low pitch, posteromedial→high pitch).

Unilateral Lesion

Slightly reduces hearing in the opposite ear (due to bilateral fiber crossing).

Bilateral Lesion

Greatly reduces the capacity for hearing. Also impairs sound localization.

💬

Secondary Auditory Area (22)

Interpretation & Understanding

Receives processed impulses from primary area. Interprets and understands the meaning of words and complex sounds.

Lesion → Auditory Aphasia

Patient can hear sounds (primary area intact) but cannot understand the meaning of words — known as "word deafness".

🧭 Sound Localization

⏱️

Medial Superior Olivary Nucleus

Detects time-lag between sound reaching both ears. The shortest detectable delay is about 0.06 msec. Critical for horizontal sound localization.

📢

Lateral Superior Olivary Nucleus

Detects intensity differences between the two ears. Sound arriving at the closer ear is louder, enabling directional detection.
