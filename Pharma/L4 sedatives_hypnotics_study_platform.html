📋 Drug Class Overview
Four major categories — mechanisms, key drugs, clinical use
Class 01
⚗️ Barbiturates
Mechanism: GABA-like action OR enhance GABA → ↑Cl⁻ conductance → hyperpolarization. Non-selective CNS & medullary depression.

Key drugs: Thiopental Na (ultra-short, 20 min), Pentobarbital (short), Phenobarbital (long, 1–2 days).

Uses: Anesthesia induction, seizures/neonatal jaundice, anxiety/insomnia.
⚠️ Exam Redistribution Porphyria
Class 02
💜 Benzodiazepines
Mechanism: Allosteric modulation of GABA on GABAA → ↑Cl⁻ → hyperpolarization. Subtype 1 = sedative & anticonvulsant.

Key drugs: Midazolam (short), Diazepam, Clonazepam (long), Lorazepam, Oxazepam (conjugation).

Uses: First-line anxiety (<6 wk), seizures, preanesthesia.
⚠️ Exam Flumazenil antidote Allosteric
Class 03
🟢 Z-Drugs (BZD Agonists)
Drugs: Zolpidem, Zopiclone (metallic taste), Zaleplon (shortest t½).

Mechanism: Same as BZDs — bind BZD receptors, antagonized by flumazenil.

Advantage: Minimal sleep disturbance, little tolerance/dependence, no hangover. NOT anticonvulsants or muscle relaxants.
⚠️ Short-term only <2 weeks insomnia
Class 04
✨ Non-BZD / Non-Barb
Ramelteon: MT1 & MT2 melatonin agonist — sleep onset, no rebound insomnia, ↑Prolactin.

Buspirone: Partial 5-HT1A agonist — GAD in elderly. Delayed onset 1–2 weeks. No sedation, amnesia, ataxia, or alcohol potentiation.

Chloral hydrate: Hypnosis in 30 min; CI in liver/kidney disease; gastric irritation.
⚠️ Buspirone: 1–2 wk onset Elderly favorite

💡

💡
Flumazenil pharmacokinetics: Very low oral bioavailability → IV only · Fast onset (seconds) · Short duration (1–2 hrs) due to rapid metabolism.
🔄 GABAA Receptor Activation Pathway
Step-by-step mechanism — know every step
GABA binds to the GABAA receptor (orthosteric site).
Benzodiazepines bind to the adjacent BZD receptor site on the complex (allosteric modulation).
Separation of beta subunits — conformational change in the receptor.
Opening of the ligand-gated Chloride (Cl⁻) channel.
Increased Cl⁻ conductance — chloride ions flow into the cell.
Hyperpolarization of the neuron → cell is harder to excite.
Generalized CNS inhibition → anxiolysis, sedation, anticonvulsant, muscle relaxation.

🔁 Barbiturate Toxicity Cascade
How repeated dosing leads to toxicity
Thiopental Na given IV → high lipid solubility → rapid CNS penetration → anesthesia onset in seconds.
Redistribution: drug leaves brain → enters muscle → then fat. Brain concentration falls → patient wakes up in ~20 min.
With single dose: redistribution is efficient, drug enters adipose stores, effect terminates safely.
With repeated doses: adipose tissue becomes saturated → drug can no longer redistribute → remains in plasma/brain.
Result: prolonged CNS & medullary depression → respiratory failure, cardiovascular collapse. ⚠️ Fatal without support.
🔁 Porphyria Mechanism
Why Phenobarbital is absolutely contraindicated in AIP
Phenobarbital (and all barbiturates) induce hepatic CYP450 microsomal enzymes.
Induction includes enzymes in the porphyrin synthesis pathway (δ-ALA synthase).
In susceptible individuals (genetic enzyme deficiency), porphyrin precursors accumulate.
Accumulation → Acute Intermittent Porphyria (AIP) crisis: severe abdominal pain + neurological deficits.
Management: stop the offending drug immediately, supportive care, IV heme therapy.

Step-by-step rational prescribing for sedatives & hypnotics
📍 Patient Scenario: Anxiety in Elderly with Liver Disease
1
Assess Hepatic Function
Is there liver cirrhosis or hepatic impairment? Yes → oxidative metabolism (CYP450) is reduced.
Long-acting BZDs metabolized by oxidation will accumulate → toxic CNS depression.
Avoid: DiazepamAvoid: ChlordiazepoxideAvoid: Clorazepate
2
What is the primary symptom?
Anxiety (GAD)? → Buspirone preferred (no hepatic toxicity risk, no dependence).
Insomnia? → Ramelteon (MT1/MT2 agonist — no GABA effect, no dependence).
Acute severe anxiety? → Short-acting BZD metabolized by conjugation.
Buspirone: 1st choice GAD elderlyRamelteon: sleep onset
3
If BZD needed — choose by metabolism
Select BZDs metabolized by conjugation (not oxidation) = inactive metabolites = safe in liver disease + old age.
LorazepamOxazepamTemazepamMnemonic: LOT
4
Check for Porphyria History
History of Acute Intermittent Porphyria? → All barbiturates are absolutely contraindicated. Even a single dose can trigger a fatal crisis.
CONTRAINDICATED: All BarbituratesSafe: Z-drugs, Ramelteon, Buspirone
5
Monitor & Limit Duration
BZDs: limit to <6 weeks. Z-drugs: limit to <2 weeks. Buspirone has no duration limit but needs 1–2 weeks to take effect. Warn patient about delayed onset.

🔬 BZD Overdose Management
Emergency protocol
1
Recognize BZD Toxidrome
Sedation → respiratory depression → apnea. Over-enhancement of GABA → hyperpolarization of medullary respiratory centers.
2
Administer Flumazenil IV
Competitive antagonist at BZD receptor site → rapidly reverses sedation & respiratory depression. Note: oral bioavailability very low → IV route only.
3
Re-sedation Risk
Flumazenil duration = 1–2 hours. BZD may outlast flumazenil → re-sedation. Monitor closely and repeat dose if needed.
4
No Antidote for Barbiturates
Barbiturate overdose has no specific antidote. Management is supportive only: airway management, activated charcoal, alkalinize urine (for Phenobarbital). This is a key BZD advantage.

💊 Drug Profiles
Key pharmacology for each agent — uses, kinetics, adverse effects
🔴 Barbiturates
Thiopental Na
Ultra-short Barbiturate · IV
  • Duration: 20 minutes
  • Short effect = redistribution (brain → fat/muscle)
  • Use: Induction of general anesthesia
  • Risk: repeated doses → fat saturation → prolonged depression
  • High lipid solubility → rapid CNS penetration
Phenobarbital
Long-acting Barbiturate (1–2 days)
  • Use: Seizures (epilepsy) + neonatal jaundice
  • ⚠️ Induces CYP450 → ↑porphyrin synthesis
  • Contraindicated: AIP (Acute Intermittent Porphyria)
  • Can cause neonatal bleeding (↓Vit K-dependent factors)
  • Tolerance and dependence develop
Pentobarbital
Short-acting Barbiturate
  • Use: anxiety, insomnia (largely replaced)
  • All barbiturate adverse effects apply
  • Narrow therapeutic index
  • No specific antidote
💜 Benzodiazepines
Diazepam
Long-acting BZD
  • Uses: anxiety, muscle relaxant, status epilepticus, alcohol withdrawal
  • Metabolism: oxidation → active metabolites (nordiazepam)
  • Avoid in elderly/liver disease (accumulates)
  • t½ = 20–100 hours
Lorazepam / Oxazepam
Intermediate BZD — Conjugation
  • Metabolism: conjugation → inactive metabolites
  • Preferred in elderly, liver disease, renal disease
  • Part of "LOT" mnemonic (Lorazepam, Oxazepam, Temazepam)
  • No active metabolites — predictable effect
Flumazenil
BZD Antidote — Competitive Antagonist
  • Mechanism: competitive antagonist at BZD receptor
  • Very low oral bioavailability → IV only
  • Onset: seconds | Duration: 1–2 hours
  • Re-sedation risk if BZD half-life > flumazenil
  • Does NOT reverse barbiturate OD
Midazolam
Short-acting BZD
  • Use: procedural sedation, preanesthetic medication
  • High IV dose → acute anterograde amnesia
  • Conjugation metabolism
  • Rapid onset
Alprazolam
Intermediate BZD — Exception
  • Intermediate-acting but has an active metabolite (exception)
  • Metabolized by oxidation (CYP450)
  • Use: panic disorder, GAD
  • Use with caution in liver disease
🟢 Z-Drugs (BZD Agonists)
Zaleplon
Z-Drug · Shortest t½
  • Shortest t½ of all Z-drugs
  • Use: sleep-onset insomnia exclusively
  • Minimal residual effect (no morning hangover)
  • Acts on BZD receptors; antagonized by flumazenil
  • NOT an anticonvulsant or muscle relaxant
Zopiclone
Z-Drug
  • Adverse effect: metallic bitter taste + dry mouth
  • Short-term insomnia (<2 weeks)
  • Minimal sleep architecture disturbance
  • Little dependence/tolerance vs BZDs
Zolpidem
Z-Drug
  • Most commonly used Z-drug for insomnia
  • t½ longer than Zaleplon but still short
  • Structurally different from BZDs but same mechanism
  • Adverse: sleepwalking reported
✨ Non-BZD Non-Barbiturate
Buspirone
5-HT1A Partial Agonist
  • Mechanism: partial agonist at 5-HT1A receptors (midbrain)
  • Use: GAD — especially elderly
  • Onset: 1–2 weeks (delayed!)
  • Does NOT treat: insomnia, panic attacks
  • No: sedation, amnesia, ataxia, alcohol potentiation
  • No abuse potential — not a controlled substance
Ramelteon
MT1/MT2 Melatonin Agonist
  • Mechanism: selective MT1 and MT2 melatonin receptor agonist
  • Use: difficulty falling asleep (sleep onset insomnia)
  • No rebound insomnia, no cognitive impairment
  • Not a controlled substance
  • Adverse: ↑Prolactin, dizziness, fatigue
Chloral Hydrate
Non-BZD Non-Barb Hypnotic
  • Onset: hypnosis in 30 min, lasts 6 hrs
  • Adverse: bad taste/odor, gastric irritation
  • Contraindicated: liver & kidney disease
  • Addiction may occur (rare)
