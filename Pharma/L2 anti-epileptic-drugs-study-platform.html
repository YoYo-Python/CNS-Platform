⚡ Epilepsy & Anti-Epileptic Pharmacology

Epilepsy is a brain disorder with a continuous predisposition to generate epileptic seizures. It is NOT a single disease entity — different epilepsies require different drugs. A seizure is a sudden, single episodic electrical discharge in cerebral neurons (measured by EEG), which may cause convulsions (violent, involuntary contraction of voluntary muscles).

🧬 Core Concepts

🔴 Status Epilepticus

Prolonged seizures without recovery of full consciousness between episodes. Life-threatening emergency.

→ Emergency: IV Diazepam/Lorazepam first
⚠️ EXAMHigh-Yield

🔵 Focal Seizures

Simple: Localized, no LOC. Complex: Spreads, LOC. DOC: Carbamazepine.

→ Na+ channel blocker
DOC: CBZ

🟡 Generalized Absence

Blank staring 10–14 sec, up to 100×/day. No loss of posture. T-type Ca²⁺ channel driven.

→ Ethosuximide (T-type Ca²⁺ block)
DOC: Ethosuximide⚠️ EXAM

🟠 Generalized Tonic-Clonic

Tonic rigidity → Clonic jerking → Post-ictal stupor. Bilateral synchronous discharge.

→ Valproic Acid (broad-spectrum)
DOC: Valproate

⚡ Myoclonic Seizures

Brief, electric shock-like muscle jerks of one or more limbs, singly or in bursts.

→ Valproate / Levetiracetam
Levetiracetam

🟣 Reflex Seizures

Triggered by specific stimuli: flashing lights, hyperventilation. Avoid precipitating factors.

→ Avoid triggers; AED prophylaxis
Trigger-Induced

🌊 Status Epilepticus Treatment Flowchart
1

IV Diazepam / Lorazepam (or buccal Midazolam in children / rectal)
Augments GABA-A → ↑ Cl⁻ influx → hyperpolarization → seizure termination

↓ if unresolved
2

IV Fosphenytoin
Blocks Na⁺ channels → reduces high-frequency neuronal firing

↓ if unresolved
3

IV Phenobarbitone
Non-selective GABA stimulation → broad CNS depression

↓ severe/refractory
4

General Anesthesia (Thiopental) + Artificial Respiration
ICU management for refractory status epilepticus

🔬 Diagnostic Algorithm
1

Clinical History — Characterize seizure: onset, spread, LOC, duration, postictal state. Is it focal or generalized?

↓
2

EEG (Electroencephalogram) — Gold standard. Documents electrical discharges. Identifies pattern (3 Hz spike-wave = absence; focal spikes = partial; generalized = GTC).

↓
3

Rule out Provoked Causes — Acute brain infections (meningitis), trauma, metabolic (hypoglycemia, hyponatremia), fever, toxins. These are not epilepsy.

↓
4

Classify Epilepsy Type — Use seizure semiology + EEG pattern to assign type (focal, GTC, absence, myoclonic, etc.).

↓
5

Select Appropriate AED — Match drug to seizure type. Monotherapy first. Adjust by plasma concentration and response.

↓
6

Monitor & Adjust — Plasma drug levels, liver enzymes (valproate, carbamazepine), CBC (phenytoin). Avoid drug interactions.

🚨 EMERGENCY — Status Epilepticus
1st: IV Diazepam / Lorazepam
GABA-A allosteric modulation → ↑ Cl⁻ influx → hyperpolarization
DOC for status epilepticus. Rectal in children; buccal Midazolam alternative.
⚠️ Additive with other CNS depressants
2nd: IV Fosphenytoin
Prodrug of Phenytoin → Na⁺ channel block
Given if benzodiazepines fail to terminate seizure.
3rd: IV Phenobarbitone
Non-selective GABA receptor stimulation
Third-line; significant sedation.
Refractory: Thiopental + Ventilation
General anesthesia + ICU
For refractory cases unresponsive to above.
💊 Long-Term AED Management
Valproic Acid
Na⁺ block + T-Ca²⁺ block + ↑ GABA synthesis | CYP450: Inhibitor
DOC: Generalized Tonic-Clonic, Myoclonic. Broad-spectrum.
⚠️ Hepatotoxicity, Alopecia, Teratogenic (spina bifida), Weight gain. Monitor LFTs. Avoid <2 yrs & pregnancy.
Carbamazepine
Na⁺ channel block | CYP450: Auto-inducer (dose needs ↑ over time)
DOC: Focal (partial) epilepsy. Also trigeminal neuralgia.
⚠️ Dilutional hyponatremia (↑ADH), Steven-Johnson Syndrome, Diplopia. Contraindicated in absence epilepsy.
Ethosuximide
T-type Ca²⁺ channel block in thalamo-cortical neurons
DOC: Generalized Absence (Petit-Mal) exclusively.
⚠️ GI upset, Sedation, Hiccups
Phenytoin
Na⁺ channel block (prolongs inactivated state) | CYP450: Inducer
Partial seizures, GTC. NOT for absence.
⚠️ Gingival hyperplasia, Hirsutism, Megaloblastic anemia (↓ folate), Aplastic anemia (rare), Nystagmus, Ataxia. Decreases oral contraceptive efficacy.
Levetiracetam
SV2A vesicle protein block → ↓ presynaptic glutamate release | No CYP450 interactions
Broad-spectrum: Focal, GTC, Myoclonic. Excellent safety profile.
⚠️ Dizziness, Nervousness, Behavioral changes (rare)
Lamotrigine
Blocks glutamate receptors + Na⁺ + Ca²⁺ channels
Broad-spectrum: Focal, GTC, Absence. Used in women (better teratogenic profile).
⚠️ Steven-Johnson Syndrome (severe rash) — must discontinue immediately
Topiramate
Blocks glutamate receptors (AMPA/Kainate) + Na⁺ channels
Focal and generalized seizures. Also migraine prophylaxis.
⚠️ Cognitive decline ("Dopamax"), Weight loss, Kidney stones
Gabapentin / Pregabalin
↑ GABA release; block α2δ subunit of L-type Ca²⁺ → ↓ glutamate
Adjuvant only for focal seizures. Also: neuropathic pain, anxiety.
⚠️ Sedation, Dizziness, Nystagmus, Weight gain. NOT monotherapy.
Phenobarbitone
Non-selective GABA receptor stimulation → CNS depression | CYP450: Inducer
GTC, Focal. Status epilepticus (3rd line).
⚠️ Sedation, Dependence, Contraindicated in absence (worsens)
⚠️
Critical AED Management Rules
  • 🔹 Start with monotherapy — more efficient, fewer side effects
  • 🔹 Never abrupt withdrawal — may precipitate status epilepticus (always taper gradually)
  • 🔹 Discontinue only after ≥2 years seizure-free + normal EEG
  • 🔹 Women of childbearing age: 5 mg folic acid daily before pregnancy; family planning essential
  • 🔹 CYP450 inducers (CBZ, Phenytoin, Phenobarbitone) decrease oral contraceptive efficacy
  • 🔹 Carbamazepine auto-induction: Plasma levels fall → dose must increase over months
  • 🔹 Observe activities: No swimming alone, advise regarding driving
