🧬 Pathophysiology Theories
🧪 Biogenic Amine & Receptor Theory ⚠️ Most Accepted
• Depression = deficiency of NA and/or 5-HT in CNS
• Mania = functional excess of NA and/or 5-HT
• Overactivity of 5-HT2A receptors involved in depression & psychosis
• Evidence: drugs that ↑NA/5-HT improve symptoms → theory confirmed clinically
🔬 Neurotrophic & Cytokines Theory
• ↓ Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor (BDNF)
• Dysregulation of hypothalamo-pituitary axis (HPA axis)
• ↑ Pro-inflammatory cytokines: IL-6, IL-1, TNF-α

⚙️ Pathophysiology

Step-by-step mechanisms and key pathways

🧀 Cheese Reaction Pathway
1
Tyramine ingestion
Patient eats tyramine-rich food: aged cheese, herrings, yogurt, red wine
2
Blocked hepatic MAO-A
Patient on non-selective MAOI → MAO-A in liver is inhibited
3
Tyramine escapes metabolism
Normally, hepatic MAO-A degrades tyramine before it enters systemic circulation → now bypassed
4
Massive NA release
Tyramine (indirect sympathomimetic) enters systemic circulation → triggers massive noradrenaline release from presynaptic neurons
5
Hypertensive crisis ‼️
Severe, life-threatening hypertension. Treatment: α + β blockers = prazosin + propranolol
💙 TCA Toxicity Mechanism
1
TCA overdose
Massive TCA ingestion saturates all receptor targets
2
Muscarinic blockade
Dry mouth, blurred vision, urinary retention, constipation (atropine-like action)
3
α-adrenergic blockade
Profound hypotension, postural instability
4
Metabolic acidosis + Cardiac toxicity
Wide QRS complex, long QT interval, tachycardia → life-threatening arrhythmias
5
Treatment ✅
1st line: IV NaHCO₃ (corrects acidosis). Lidocaine for arrhythmias. Hemodialysis INEFFECTIVE — large Vd, highly lipophilic
🔴 Serotonin Syndrome Mechanism
1
MAOI + SSRI co-administration
MAOI ↓ 5-HT degradation + SSRI blocks 5-HT reuptake = toxic synergism
2
Massive 5-HT accumulation → Serotonin Syndrome
Hyperthermia, muscle rigidity (clonus), cardiovascular collapse, altered consciousness

💉 Treatment

Drug-by-drug treatment guide with mechanisms and indications

🔴 Emergency Treatment
🧪
IV NaHCO₃ — TCA Toxicity (1st Line) ⚠️
First-line treatment for TCA overdose. Corrects metabolic acidosis and reduces cardiac toxicity
  • Corrects metabolic acidosis caused by TCA overdose
  • Alkalinization reduces cardiac sodium channel blockade
  • Reduces severity of arrhythmias
  • Hemodialysis INEFFECTIVE: TCAs are highly lipophilic with large volume of distribution
  • Follow with IV Lidocaine for persistent arrhythmias
💊
Prazosin + Propranolol — Cheese Reaction ⚠️
Combined α + β blockade for tyramine-induced hypertensive crisis
  • Prazosin: α1-blocker → reduces peripheral vasoconstriction
  • Propranolol: β-blocker → reduces heart rate and cardiac output
  • Combined blockade addresses both components of the adrenergic surge
  • Must use combined α+β blockade — β-blockade alone can worsen hypertension!
🟣 MAOIs
⛔
Phenelzine / Tranylcypromine / Isocarboxazide
Non-selective, irreversible MAOIs. Refractory depression. Recovery takes weeks.
  • Inhibit MAO-A and MAO-B irreversibly
  • ↑ NA, 5-HT, dopamine in brain
  • Cheese reaction risk: avoid tyramine-rich foods
  • Serotonin syndrome if combined with SSRIs or TCAs
  • Recovery after stopping: 2-3 weeks (new enzyme must be synthesized)
✅
Moclobemide — Reversible MAO-A Inhibitor
Reversible inhibitor — safer tyramine profile, faster recovery
  • Reversibly inhibits MAO-A
  • Lower risk of cheese reaction compared to irreversible MAOIs
  • Faster enzyme recovery after discontinuation
  • Fewer drug interactions
🧠
Selegiline — MAO-B Inhibitor ⚠️
Specific MAO-B inhibitor. Used for Parkinsonism — not depression.
  • Specific inhibitor of MAO-B (acts primarily on dopamine)
  • Used for Parkinsonism — increases dopamine in striatum
  • At therapeutic doses: minimal effect on MAO-A → lower cheese reaction risk
  • MCQ favorite: "Selegiline → Parkinsonism"
🔵 Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs)
💊
Imipramine / Amitriptyline — Non-selective TCAs
Block 5-HT + NA reuptake. Also block α, H1, muscarinic receptors.
  • Mechanism: Inhibit amine pump (reuptake) for both 5-HT and NA
  • Off-label: Imipramine for nocturnal enuresis ⚠️ (anticholinergic + sleep architecture)
  • Amitriptyline for neuropathic pain and migraine prophylaxis
  • α blockade → postural hypotension
  • H1 blockade → sedation
  • Muscarinic blockade → dry mouth, urinary retention, blurred vision, constipation
  • AVOID in: glaucoma, BPH, post-MI patients
⚡
Maprotiline / Amoxapine — Selective NA blockers
Selectively block NA reuptake only (NOT 5-HT). 2nd generation TCAs.
  • Maprotiline: selective NA reuptake inhibitor
  • Amoxapine: also blocks dopamine receptors (useful if psychotic depression)
  • Fewer anticholinergic side effects than classic TCAs
  • Still risk of seizures (especially Maprotiline at high doses)
🟢 SSRIs
💊
Fluoxetine — Long Half-Life SSRI ⚠️
Half-life 24–96h (active metabolites). Inhibits CYP2D6. Risk of drug interactions.
  • Selectively blocks 5-HT reuptake
  • Very long half-life (24–96h) due to active metabolite norfluoxetine
  • Inhibits hepatic CYP2D6 → ↑TCA levels if combined → toxicity risk
  • Requires 5-week washout before starting MAOI
  • Indications: MDD, OCD, Bulimia nervosa, PMDD
  • Must taper slowly over 4 weeks to stop
❤️
Sertraline — Post-MI Safe SSRI ⚠️
Preferred antidepressant following acute myocardial infarction.
  • SSRI with best evidence for cardiac safety post-MI
  • Fewer drug interactions than fluoxetine
  • Good tolerability profile
  • First-line for depression in cardiac patients
🟡 Atypical Antidepressants
🎗️
Mirtazapine — Best for Cancer Patients ⚠️
Unique receptor profile: α2 + 5-HT2C + 5-HT3 + H1 blockade
  • Blocks presynaptic α2 receptors → ↑ NA and 5-HT release (antidepressant effect)
  • H1 blockade → sedation (helps insomnia in cancer patients)
  • 5-HT3 blockade → antiemetic (reduces nausea/vomiting)
  • 5-HT2C blockade → ↑ appetite, ↑ body weight (counteracts cachexia)
  • 5-HT2 blockade → less sexual dysfunction than SSRIs
  • MCQ: "Best antidepressant in cancer patient" = Mirtazapine ⚠️
🚬
Bupropion — Smoking Cessation ⚠️
NDRI — dopamine + NA reuptake inhibitor. No sexual dysfunction.
  • Mechanism: blocks dopamine AND norepinephrine reuptake
  • Does NOT affect serotonin → less sexual dysfunction than SSRIs
  • Indications: MDD, smoking cessation (Zyban), ADHD
  • Contraindication: seizure disorders, eating disorders (lowers seizure threshold)
  • MCQ: "Smoking cessation antidepressant" = Bupropion ⚠️
💜
Trazodone — 5-HT2A blocker + SSRI
Unique adverse effect: Priapism (prolonged painful erection) ⚠️
  • Serotonin reuptake inhibitor + 5-HT2A receptor blocker
  • Notable adverse effect: PRIAPISM (prolonged erection) — MCQ favorite
  • Sedating → useful for insomnia
  • Less sexual dysfunction than SSRIs paradoxically
🔵
Venlafaxine — SNRI
Active metabolite: Desvenlafaxine ⚠️. Used for MDD and chronic pain.
  • Inhibits reuptake of both 5-HT and NA
  • Metabolized by CYP450 to active metabolite desvenlafaxine ⚠️
  • Little activity at α-adrenergic, muscarinic, or histamine receptors
  • Indications: MDD, generalized anxiety disorder, neuropathic pain, fibromyalgia
  • Duloxetine: another SNRI used for diabetic neuropathy
🔋 Mood Stabilizers
⚗️
Lithium Carbonate — Long-term Mania Prophylaxis ⚠️
Gold standard for long-term bipolar prophylaxis. Narrow therapeutic index.
  • Mechanism: ↓ cAMP in neuronal cells (↓ neuronal firing)
  • ↓ NA release in CNS
  • Interferes with Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase activity
  • Adverse effects: Nephrogenic DI (polyuria/polydipsia), anorexia, polyphagia, fine tremor, thyroid enlargement, teratogenicity
  • Drug selection: Lithium = LONG-TERM prophylaxis ⚠️
  • Narrow therapeutic index → monitor serum levels
⚡
Sodium Valproate — Acute Mania ⚠️
Treatment of choice for ACUTE manic episodes. Faster than lithium.
  • Treatment of choice in ACUTE stages of mania ⚠️
  • Also used as anticonvulsant and migraine prophylaxis
  • Works faster than lithium for acute mania
  • Adverse effects: hepatotoxicity, teratogenicity (neural tube defects), weight gain, hair loss
  • Other options for acute mania: Chlorpromazine, Haloperidol (antipsychotics as adjuncts)
