Analgesics — The Big Picture 🎯

Pain management pharmacology covers three major pillars: Opioid analgesics (μ/κ receptor agonists), Non-opioid analgesics (NSAIDs & Paracetamol, COX inhibitors), and Neuropathic pain agents (Ca²⁺ channel modulators, SNRIs, Na⁺ channel blockers). Understanding the mechanism of each drug class is fundamental to both clinical application and exam success.

💊 Drug Class Overview
🟣 Opioid Agonists
Bind μ (mu) and κ (kappa) receptors. Presynaptic: ↓ Ca²⁺ influx → ↓ transmitter release. Postsynaptic: ↑ K⁺ conductance → hyperpolarization (IPSP). Inhibit ascending pain + augment descending inhibitory pathways.
High-YieldExam Fave
🟠 NSAIDs / COX Inhibitors
Block COX enzymes → ↓ prostaglandin synthesis. Non-selective (Aspirin, Ibuprofen) block COX-1 + COX-2. Selective COX-2 inhibitors (Celecoxib) spare gastric mucosa but increase thrombotic risk.
High-YieldClinical Pearl
🔵 Neuropathic Agents
Target aberrantly-firing damaged nerves. Gabapentin/Pregabalin (↓ Ca²⁺ influx), Duloxetine (↑ serotonin + NE), Carbamazepine/Lidocaine (block Na⁺ channels).
MCQClinical Pearl
🔑 Key Clinical Signs & Exam Pearls
📌 Morphine Contraindications
HEAD INJURY — CO₂ retention → cerebral vasodilation → ↑ ICP → herniation. Also: biliary colic, acute undiagnosed abdomen (masks pain), bronchial asthma (histamine release → bronchospasm), extreme ages, hypotension.
Exam-FaveWritten Q
🔴 Opioid Toxicity Triad
PPP — Pinpoint pupils (miosis), Pallor/Perspiration, Pulmonary depression. Severe miosis = toxicity (tolerance does NOT develop to miosis or constipation — classic MCQ trap!).
High-YieldMCQ
⚗️ Paracetamol Overdose
NAPQI (toxic metabolite via CYP450) → glutathione depletion → hepatocellular necrosis. Antidote: N-acetylcysteine (sulfhydryl donor, replenishes glutathione). Give within 8 hours for best efficacy.
High-YieldExam Fave
👶 Aspirin in Children
CONTRAINDICATED in children under 12 years with viral infections — risk of Reye's Syndrome (hepatic necrosis + encephalopathy). Use Ibuprofen or Paracetamol instead.
Exam FaveClinical Pearl
💊 Oral vs IV Morphine
Oral morphine has only 25% bioavailability due to significant first-pass hepatic metabolism. IV, SC, or epidural routes bypass first-pass and are far more potent. Classic MCQ fact.
MCQHigh-Yield
🟢 Morphine-6-Glucuronide
The primary metabolite of morphine conjugation with glucuronic acid. It is a very potent analgesic — more potent than morphine itself. Eliminated renally — accumulates in renal failure.

🧪 Diagnosis
Recognition, criteria, and investigation findings
🔴 Opioid Overdose — Recognition
👁
Pinpoint miosis (PPP) — most specific sign; tolerance does NOT develop to this. Classic written Q.
🫁
Respiratory depression — RR <12, hypoxia, hypercapnia. Primary cause of death.
😴
CNS depression — drowsiness → stupor → coma. GCS often <8.
💉
Reversal test — IV Naloxone causes rapid improvement. Diagnostic and therapeutic.
🟡 Paracetamol Toxicity Staging
1️⃣
0–24h: Often asymptomatic or mild nausea/vomiting. DO NOT underestimate!
2️⃣
24–72h: RUQ pain, raised ALT/AST begin to rise (as in Scenario 1).
3️⃣
72–96h: Peak hepatotoxicity — jaundice, coagulopathy, encephalopathy.
4️⃣
>96h: Either recovery or fulminant hepatic failure / death.
✅
Antidote window: N-acetylcysteine most effective if given within 8 hours of ingestion.
📋 Neuropathic Pain Diagnosis
🔥
Burning/tingling quality — distinguishes from nociceptive pain; often described as "electric".
👋
Allodynia — pain from normally non-painful stimuli (e.g., light touch, clothing).
📍
Underlying cause — diabetic neuropathy, post-herpetic neuralgia, nerve compression, MS.
💊
Fails NSAIDs/opioids — poor response to standard analgesics; requires neuro-modulatory agents.
🔬 Investigation Findings Summary
Paracetamol overdose investigations:
⬆️ ALT / AST elevated ⬆️ Bilirubin elevated ⬆️ PT / INR prolonged 🩸 Serum Paracetamol level 📉 Glucose ↓ (liver failure) ✅ NAC = treatment
Opioid toxicity investigations:
⬇️ RR <12/min ⬆️ PaCO₂ elevated (ABG) ⬇️ SpO₂ desaturation 👁 Pinpoint pupils 🔬 Urine toxicology screen ✅ Naloxone = treatment

💉 Treatment
Drug-by-drug reference with clinical pearls
🚨 Emergency Treatments
💊
Naloxone (IV)
Pure opioid antagonist. Drug of choice for acute opioid overdose. Competitively displaces opioids from μ receptors. Reverses respiratory depression, miosis, and coma. Short-acting — may need repeat dosing or infusion. Also inject into umbilical cord for neonatal apnea post-maternal morphine.
🧪
N-Acetylcysteine (NAC)
Antidote for paracetamol overdose. Acts as sulfhydryl donor → replenishes hepatic glutathione → detoxifies NAPQI. Most effective within 8 hours of ingestion. IV or oral. Can still help later but less effective.
🟣 Opioid Analgesics
💉
Morphine
Gold standard for severe pain. IV/SC/epidural preferred (oral bioavailability only 25%). Metabolized to M-6-G (potent). Contraindicated: head injury, bronchial asthma, acute abdomen, biliary colic, hypotension. Causes miosis (PPP), constipation (no tolerance).
🏥
Meperidine (Pethidine)
Preferred in labor (shorter-acting, does not prolong labor). Preferred in biliary colic (weak atropine-like action → less sphincter of Oddi spasm). Preferred in inferior MI with bradycardia (atropine-like → no additional bradycardia). Avoid in renal failure (normeperidine accumulates → seizures).
⏳
Methadone
Long-acting oral μ-agonist. High oral bioavailability. Used for opioid dependence maintenance — stabilizes patient, prevents withdrawal, blocks euphoria of shorter-acting opioids. Slow onset prevents rapid "high".
🩹
Fentanyl
Highly potent (100× morphine). Short duration. Available as transdermal patch for chronic severe pain. Also IV for procedural sedation/anesthesia.
🔄
Buprenorphine
Partial μ-agonist + weak κ-antagonist. Ceiling effect on respiratory depression → lower overdose risk. Used in opioid withdrawal management. ⚠️ Can precipitate withdrawal if given to patients on full agonists.
💊
Codeine
Prodrug → converted to morphine by CYP2D6 in liver. Weak analgesic / antitussive. Variable effect (poor metabolizers get no effect; ultra-rapid metabolizers get toxicity).
🔀
Tramadol
Dual mechanism: μ-opioid receptor agonism + SNRI (inhibits serotonin + norepinephrine reuptake). Moderate pain. Risk of serotonin syndrome if combined with MAOIs or SSRIs.
🟠 NSAIDs & Non-Opioid Analgesics
🌡
Paracetamol (Acetaminophen)
COX-3 inhibitor (CNS-selective). Analgesic + antipyretic. No anti-inflammatory effect. Drug of choice for fever/pain in children <12 and pregnant women. Safe gastric profile. ⚠️ Overdose = hepatotoxicity via NAPQI.
💊
Aspirin
Non-selective COX-1+2 inhibitor. Anti-inflammatory, analgesic, antipyretic, anti-platelet. Low dose = antiplatelet (irreversible, 7-day washout pre-surgery). ⚠️ Contraindicated in children <12 (Reye's syndrome), peptic ulcer, bleeding disorders.
🔥
Ibuprofen
Non-selective COX inhibitor. Good for mild-moderate pain and inflammation. Better GI tolerance than aspirin (reversible COX inhibition). Safe alternative to aspirin in children for fever.
🎯
Celecoxib (COX-2 Selective)
Selective COX-2 inhibitor. Fewer GI ulcers (spares COX-1). ⚠️ Increased cardiovascular thrombotic risk (prostacyclin ↓, TXA2 unopposed). Avoid in patients with cardiac disease. Used in arthritis.
🔵 Neuropathic Pain
⚡
Gabapentin / Pregabalin
Modulate voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels (α2δ subunit). ↓ Ca²⁺ influx → ↓ neurotransmitter release from hyperactive neurons. Used in diabetic neuropathy, post-herpetic neuralgia, fibromyalgia, epilepsy.
🧠
Duloxetine (SNRI)
Inhibits reuptake of serotonin + norepinephrine → enhances descending inhibitory pain pathways. FDA-approved for diabetic peripheral neuropathy, fibromyalgia, chronic MSK pain.
⚡
Carbamazepine
Voltage-gated Na⁺ channel blocker. First-line for trigeminal neuralgia. Also used for neuropathic pain, epilepsy, bipolar. Monitor for agranulocytosis, Steven-Johnson syndrome.
🩹
Lidocaine Patch (5%)
Peripheral Na⁺ channel blocker. Topical application over painful area. Used in localized neuropathic pain (e.g., post-herpetic neuralgia). Minimal systemic absorption.
🟢 Dependence & Relapse Prevention
🔒
Naltrexone (Oral)
Pure opioid antagonist, oral formulation. Used for relapse prevention in opioid dependence. Blocks μ-receptors → prevents euphoria if patient takes opioids. No physical dependence risk.
🔄
Loperamide / Diphenoxylate
Selective antidiarrheal opioids. Act on GI μ-receptors → ↓ peristalsis, ↑ sphincter tone. Minimal CNS penetration → no euphoria or dependence at therapeutic doses.
